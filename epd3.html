BJT Transistor: Introduction

A bipolar junction transistor (BJT) is a three terminal device consisting of either a p-type semiconductor or n-type semiconductor sandwiched between opposite types. It has two p–n junctions connected back-to-back.

In a BJT, the operation depends on the active participation of both the majority carriers, and the minority carriers; hence the name "bipolar".

The combination of two terms transfer + resistor results in TRANSISTOR as the current is transferred from a low to a high-resistance circuit.

NPN structure: N (emitter) | P (base) | N (collector) — two back-to-back p-n junctions.
PNP structure: P (emitter) | N (base) | P (collector) — two back-to-back p-n junctions (reversed).

BJT Transistor: Construction

The three terminals of a Bipolar junction transistor are Emitter, Base & Collector.

Terminals

The Emitter (E) is the portion of the transistor that supplies charge carriers (either electrons or holes) and it is a heavily doped region. The area is moderate in size.

The Base (B) is the middle portion of the transistor that forms two PN junction between the emitter and the collector and it is a lightly doped region. It is the smallest in width of all the three regions.

The Collector (C) is the portion of the transistor that collects charge carriers (either electrons or holes) and it is a moderately doped region. It is the widest of all the three regions as maximum heat is dissipated in this region.

NPN symbol: Emitter arrow points outward (away from base). PNP symbol: Emitter arrow points inward (toward base). Both have three terminals: E (Emitter), B (Base), C (Collector).

Types of BJTs

1. PNP Transistor

PNP structure: Emitter (p⁺) | Base (n) | Collector (p)
Emitter "emits" holes → Narrow Base controls number of holes emitted → Collector "collects" holes emitted by the emitter

2. NPN Transistor

NPN structure: Emitter (n⁺) | Base (p) | Collector (n)
Emitter "emits" electrons → Narrow Base controls number of electrons emitted → Collector "collects" electrons emitted by the emitter

BJT Operation

The transistor operated in Common Base configuration, with the external sources, VEE and VCC, connected as shown:

  • The emitter – base junction is forward biased
  • The collector – base junction is reverse biased

As the EB junction is forward biased the current flow is because of majority charge carriers.

Similarly as the CB junction is reverse biased the current flow is because of minority charge carriers (Hence wider depletion region)

Forward Bias (EB junction): Majority carriers flow from emitter into base region. Depletion region is narrow.
Reverse Bias (CB junction): Minority carriers are swept across. Depletion region is wide.

Currents in a BJT

  • A large number of majority carriers will diffuse across the forward biased p–n junction into the n-type material.
  • Since the n-type material is very thin and lightly doped, a very small number of these (holes) will recombine with the electrons in base region and flow through the base terminal as base current (is typically in the order of microamperes).
  • Thus, a larger number of these majority carriers from the emitter region will diffuse across the reverse-biased junction into the p-type material connected to the collector terminal.

BJT Operation (pnp Transistor) — Currents

Applying KCL to the transistor, we get:

KCL — Transistor Currents
\[ I_E = I_C + I_B \]

The collector current comprises two components — the majority and the minority carriers.

The minority-current is called as leakage current (ICO — IC with emitter terminal Open).

Therefore the total collector current is given as:

Total Collector Current
\[ I_C = I_{C_{\text{majority}}} + I_{CO_{\text{minority}}} \]

BJT Operation (npn Transistor) — Currents

Emitter current is the sum of the collector and base currents:

\[ I_E = I_C + I_B \]

The collector current is comprised of two currents:

\[ I_C = I_{C\,(\text{Majority})} + I_{CO\,(\text{Minority})} \]
NPN current directions: I_E flows into emitter terminal (conventional), I_C flows out of collector, I_B flows into base. The majority carrier flow in the n-p-n is from emitter to collector (electron flow).

Base Width Modulation (Early Effect)

A greater reverse bias across the collector-base junction increases the collector-base depletion width. There is a variation in the width of the base in a bipolar transistor due to a variation in the applied base-to-collector voltage.

Base-narrowing has two consequences that affect the current:

  • There is a lesser chance for recombination within the "smaller" base region.
  • The charge gradient is increased across the base, and consequently, the current of minority carriers injected across the collector-base junction increases.

The Early effect is observed as an increase in the collector or "output" current with increasing collector-emitter voltage.

Regions of Operation (CB)

Active region: The operating range of the amplifier. It is noticed that IE is approximately equal to IC (IE ≈ IC). BJT is a current controlled device in the active region and is independent of the output voltage VCB.

Cut-off region: The region where the collector current IC is approximately equal to 0 (IC < ICBO). The device is basically OFF i.e., there's no input voltage, but only reverse – bias output voltage and negligible reverse saturation current.

Reverse saturation current circuit: Emitter open (I_E = 0), collector-to-base reverse biased. Only I_CBO = I_CO flows (minority carrier leakage current).

Saturation region: The region to the left of VCB = 0V. Note the exponential increase in collector current IC as VCB approaches 0V. In this region, IC depends on VCB but is independent of IE.

  • Base – emitter voltage (VBE): VBE = 0.7 V (Si)

Numericals — Common Base

Problem 1

A common base transistor amplifier has an input resistance of 20 Ω and output resistance of 100 kΩ. The collector load is 1 kΩ. If a signal of 500 mV is applied between emitter and base, find the voltage amplification. Assume αac to be nearly one.

Solution

Given: Rin = 20 Ω, Rout = 100 kΩ, RC = 1 kΩ, Signal = 500 mV, αac ≈ 1

\[ I_E = \frac{\text{Signal}}{R_{in}} = \frac{500\,\text{mV}}{20\,\Omega} = 25\,\text{mA} \]

Since αac is nearly 1, output current IC = IE = 25 mA.

\[ V_{\text{out}} = I_C R_C = 25\,\text{mA} \times 1\,\text{k}\Omega = 25\,\text{V} \] \[ \therefore\quad A_v = \frac{V_{\text{out}}}{\text{signal}} = \frac{25\,\text{V}}{500\,\text{mV}} = \mathbf{50} \]

Regions of Operation (CE)

Active region: The B-E junction is forward biased. Collector voltage is greater than the base voltage which reverse biases the C-B junction.

\[ V_{CE} > V_{BE} \]

In this region, IC depends on IE and is almost independent of VCE.

\[ I_C = \beta \cdot I_B \]

Cut-off region: Both junctions are reverse biased. No base current. Only a small reverse leakage current between C and E. For practical purposes, current is assumed to be zero.

Saturation region: The E-B and C-B junctions are forward biased. IC varies with VCE and is independent of IB and β.

CE Active Region — Derivation

Step-by-step derivation
\[ I_C = \alpha I_E + I_{CB0} \] \[ = \alpha (I_C + I_B) + I_{CB0} \qquad (\because\; I_E = I_C + I_B) \] \[ I_C = \frac{\alpha I_B + I_{CB0}}{1-\alpha} \]

Let \(\beta = \dfrac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\)    \(\left[\because\; \dfrac{1}{1-\alpha} = 1+\beta\right]\)

\[ I_C = \beta I_B + (1+\beta)I_{CB0} \] \[ \boxed{I_C = \beta I_B + I_{CE0}} \]

where \(I_{CE0} = (1+\beta)I_{CB0} \approx \beta I_{CB0}\) or \(I_{CE0} = \dfrac{I_{CB0}}{1-\alpha}\bigg|_{I_B=0\,\mu\text{A}}\)

Neglecting leakage current I_CEO
\[ \boxed{I_C = \beta I_B} \] \[ \boxed{50 \leq \beta \leq 500} \]
Current relationships
\[ I_C = \beta I_B \] \[ I_E = I_C + I_B = \beta I_B + I_B \] \[ \boxed{I_E = (\beta + 1)I_B} \]

Relationship Between α and β

A relationship can be developed between α and β using the basic relationships introduced thus far. Using β = IC / IB we have IB = IC / β, and from α = IC / IE we have IE = IC / α. Substituting into IE = IC + IB:

\[ \frac{I_C}{\alpha} = I_C + \frac{I_C}{\beta} \]

and dividing both sides of the equation by IC will result in:

\[ \frac{1}{\alpha} = 1 + \frac{1}{\beta} \] \[ \text{or}\quad \beta = \alpha\beta + \alpha = (\beta + 1)\alpha \] \[ \boxed{\alpha = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta} \qquad \text{or} \qquad \beta = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}} \]
Summary of CE current relations
\[ I_C = \beta I_B \] \[ I_E = I_C + I_B = \beta I_B + I_B = (\beta+1)I_B \]

Limits of Operation

The limit of operation ensures that the transistor works within its maximum rating with minimum signal distortion. It is specified in the data sheet.

VCESat: Specifies the minimum VCE that can be applied without transistor going to saturation region.

The maximum power dissipation is given as:

CE Maximum Power Dissipation
\[ P_{C_{\max}} = V_{CE} I_C \]

• Top: I_C_max horizontal line (~50 mA)
• Right: BV_CEO line (vertical, ~20 V)
• Curved: P_C_max = V_CE × I_C = 300 mW hyperbola

The transistor must operate within this "safe" region for minimum distortion.

The cutoff region is the region below IC = ICEO (= βICBO). This region must also be avoided to have minimum distortion.

If the characteristic curve/specification sheet is not available, one must ensure the following conditions are satisfied to ensure minimum distortion:

\[ I_{CEO} \leq I_C \leq I_{C_{\max}} \] \[ V_{CE_{\text{sat}}} \leq V_{CE} \leq V_{CE_{\max}} \] \[ V_{CE} I_C \leq P_{C_{\max}} \]

For CB Characteristics, PCmax is defined as:

CB Maximum Power Dissipation
\[ P_{C_{\max}} = V_{CB} I_C \]

Operating Point (Q-Point)

Biasing refers to application of DC voltages to establish a fixed level of current and voltage in a transistor.

For a transistor amplifier, the resulting DC current and voltage on the characteristic curve establish an Operating Point. This indicates the region of operation of the transistor.

Since the operating point is fixed on the Characteristic curve, it is also called as Quiescent point or Q point.

Biasing circuit can be used to set the operating point within the active region. A, B, C, D are some of the possible operating points.

Operating Point graph: I_C (mA) vs V_CE (V). Multiple β-curves shown. Points A, B, C, D marked at different locations:
• A: At origin/cutoff (I_B = 0) — device OFF
• B: Mid-active region (I_B = 30 μA) — ideal Q-point
• C: Near saturation boundary — limits signal swing
• D: Near V_CE_max — limits positive swing
Safe operating area (SOA) indicated.

Analysis of Operating Points

  • Q Point at A: If there is no bias, the device is completely off, resulting in Q point at A. This point is not suitable as complete input range can not be covered for amplification.
  • Q Point at B: Can cover a large range of input signal. It is also far from both cut-off and saturation region.
  • Q Point at C: This is near to both cutoff and saturation region thus limiting the swing of input signal.
  • Q Point at D: As is near to the maximum voltage and power level, limits the positive swing of input signal.

Effect of Temperature on Operating Point

Temperature also has its effect on the Operating point.

Temperature causes the device parameters like transistor current gain (βac) and the transistor leakage current (ICEO) to change.

As temperature increases, ICEO increases, resulting in changing the Q point. Thus the network design must also consider temperature stability to reduce the changes in Q point.

The variation of operating point due to variation in temperature is indicated by stability factor (S). Higher the stability of the circuit, better is the circuit.

Transistor Biasing

Biasing is the process of providing DC voltage which helps in the functioning of the circuit. A transistor is biased in order to make the base-emitter junction forward biased and base-collector junction reverse biased, so that it maintains in active region, to work as an amplifier.

Need for DC Biasing

If a signal of very small voltage is given to the input of BJT, it cannot be amplified. Because, for a BJT, to amplify a signal, two conditions have to be met:

  • The input voltage should exceed cut-in voltage for the transistor to be ON.
  • The BJT should be in the linear / active region, to be operated as an amplifier.

DC Biasing

  • The operating point is a fixed point on the characteristics and is also called quiescent point, denoted by Q-point. The term biasing means the application of dc voltages used to setup a fixed level of current and voltage. Called "dc biasing" or "biasing analysis".
  • This step determines both the region of operation and the small-signal parameters of each device.

Different Regions of Operation of BJT

Operation in the cutoff, saturation, and linear regions of the BJT characteristic are provided as follows:

  1. Linear-region (or Active region) operation:
    Base–emitter junction forward-biased  |  Base–collector junction reverse-biased
  2. Cutoff-region operation:
    Base–emitter junction reverse-biased  |  Base–collector junction reverse-biased
  3. Saturation-region operation:
    Base–emitter junction forward-biased  |  Base–collector junction forward-biased

Fixed-Bias Configuration

The fixed-bias circuit is the simplest transistor dc bias configuration. Hence, it is also called as simple biasing.

For dc, f = 0 Hz, and XC = 1/(2πfC) = 1/(2π(0)C) = ∞Ω.

Fixed-Bias Circuit (NPN): V_CC at top, R_B from V_CC to base, R_C from V_CC to collector. Coupling capacitors C1 (input) and C2 (output). Base-emitter junction forward biased via R_B, collector-emitter via R_C and V_CC.

Forward Bias of Base–Emitter

Consider first the base–emitter circuit loop. Writing Kirchhoff's voltage equation in the clockwise direction for the loop, we obtain +VCC – IBRB – VBE = 0:

Base Current (Fixed Bias)
\[ I_B = \frac{V_{CC} - V_{BE}}{R_B} \]

The supply voltage VCC and the base–emitter voltage VBE are constants, the selection of a base resistor RB sets the level of base current for the operating point.

Collector–Emitter Loop

The collector–emitter section of the network with the indicated direction of current IC and the resulting polarity across RC: IC = βIB

  • Changing RC to any level will not affect the level of IB or IC as long as the device is in the active region.
  • The level of RC will determine the magnitude of VCE.
Collector-Emitter Voltage
\[ V_{CE} + I_C R_C - V_{CC} = 0 \] \[ \boxed{V_{CE} = V_{CC} - I_C R_C} \]
Voltage relationships
\[ V_{CE} = V_C - V_E \qquad V_{BE} = V_B - V_E \] \text{Since emitter is ground:}\quad V_{CE} = V_C \qquad V_{BE} = V_B

Determine the following for the fixed-bias configuration: VCC = +12 V, RB = 240 kΩ, RC = 2.2 kΩ, β = 50.

Find: (a) IB_Q and IC_Q   (b) VCE_Q   (c) VB and VC   (d) VBC

Solution
\[\text{(a)}\quad I_{B_Q} = \frac{V_{CC} - V_{BE}}{R_B} = \frac{12\,\text{V} - 0.7\,\text{V}}{240\,\text{k}\Omega} = \mathbf{47.08\,\mu\text{A}}\] \[I_{C_Q} = \beta I_{BQ} = (50)(47.08\,\mu\text{A}) = \mathbf{2.35\,\text{mA}}\] \[\text{(b)}\quad V_{CE_Q} = V_{CC} - I_C R_C = 12\,\text{V} - (2.35\,\text{mA})(2.2\,\text{k}\Omega) = \mathbf{6.83\,\text{V}}\] \[\text{(c)}\quad V_B = V_{BE} = \mathbf{0.7\,\text{V}} \qquad V_C = V_{CE} = \mathbf{6.83\,\text{V}}\] \[\text{(d)}\quad V_{BC} = V_B - V_C = 0.7\,\text{V} - 6.83\,\text{V} = \mathbf{-6.13\,\text{V}}\]

The negative sign revealing that the junction is reversed-biased, as it should be for linear amplification.

Emitter-Bias Configuration

The DC bias network contains an emitter resistor to improve the stability level over that of the fixed bias configuration.

Emitter-Bias Circuit (NPN): V_CC at top, R_B from V_CC to base, R_C from V_CC to collector, R_E from emitter to ground. Coupling capacitors C1 (input) and C2 (output). BJT bias circuit with emitter resistor.

Base–Emitter Loop

Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the indicated loop in the clockwise direction:

\[ +V_{CC} - I_B R_B - V_{BE} - I_E R_E = 0 \]

Since IE = (β + 1)IB, substitute in the above equation:

\[ V_{CC} - I_B R_B - V_{BE} - (\beta+1)I_B R_E = 0 \] \[ -I_B(R_B + (\beta+1)R_E) + V_{CC} - V_{BE} = 0 \] \[ \boxed{I_B = \frac{V_{CC} - V_{BE}}{R_B + (\beta+1)R_E}} \]
Note

The only difference between this equation for IB and that obtained for the fixed bias configuration is the term (β+1)RE.

Note that aside from the base-to-emitter voltage VBE, the resistor RE is reflected back to the input base circuit by a factor (β+1). In other words, the emitter resistor, which is a part of the collector-emitter loop, "appears as" (β+1)RE in the base-emitter loop. Thus, the reflected impedance level of RE in the base circuit gives the input impedance:

\[ R_i = (\beta+1)R_E \]

Collector–Emitter Loop

Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law for the indicated loop in the clockwise direction:

\[ +I_E R_E + V_{CE} + I_C R_C - V_{CC} = 0 \]

Substituting IE ≅ IC and grouping terms gives:

\[ \boxed{V_{CE} = V_{CC} - I_C(R_C + R_E)} \]
Additional voltage relationships
\[ V_E = I_E R_E \qquad V_{CE} = V_C - V_E \] \[ V_C = V_{CE} + V_E = V_{CC} - I_C R_C \] \[ V_B = V_{CC} - I_B R_B = V_{BE} + V_E \]

Saturation Level — Emitter Bias

The collector saturation level or maximum collector current for an emitter-bias design can be determined using the same approach applied to the fixed-bias configuration. Apply a short circuit between the collector–emitter terminals and calculate the resulting collector current:

Saturation Current
\[ I_{C_{\text{sat}}} = \frac{V_{CC}}{R_C + R_E} \]

Voltage-Divider Bias Configuration

In the previous bias configurations, the bias current ICQ and voltage VCEQ were a function of the current gain β of the transistor.

Since β is temperature sensitive, especially for silicon transistors, and the actual value of beta is usually not well defined it is necessary to develop a bias circuit that is less dependent on, or independent of, the transistor beta.

Thus, Voltage divider bias configuration is preferred.

Voltage Divider Bias Circuit (NPN): V_CC at top. R1 from V_CC to base, R2 from base to ground. R_C from V_CC to collector. R_E from emitter to ground. Coupling capacitors C1 and C2. FIG. 4.28.

Approximate Analysis

Approximate Analysis can be applied only if specific conditions are satisfied.

The input section of the voltage-divider configuration can be represented by the network of Fig. 4.36. The Ri is the resistance between base and ground for the transistor with the emitter resistance, with the reflected resistance between the base and emitter, similar to the Emitter bias configuration and is given by Ri = (β+1)RE.

Approximation Condition

If βRE ≥ 10R2, then the approximation method can be applied to determine the biasing current and voltage.

VE and IE are calculated by VE = VB − VBE and \(I_E = \dfrac{V_E}{R_E}\)

\[ I_{C_Q} \cong I_E \]

The collector-to-emitter voltage is determined by:

\[ V_{CE} = V_{CC} - I_C R_C - I_E R_E \]

Since IE ≅ IC:

\[ \boxed{V_{CE_Q} = V_{CC} - I_C(R_C + R_E)} \]

In all the biasing voltage and current equation β does not appear and the Q-point (as determined by ICQ and VCEQ) is therefore independent of the value of β.

Single Stage CE Amplifier

The following shows a single stage CE amplifier. The different circuit elements and their functions are described as follows.

Single stage CE amplifier circuit: R1 and R2 form voltage divider bias, R_E is emitter resistor, R_C is collector load, C_in is input coupling capacitor, C_E is emitter bypass capacitor, C is output coupling capacitor. Weak AC input signal applied through C_in to base. V_CC powers the circuit. V_out taken from collector through C.

(i) Biasing Circuit

The resistances R1, R2 and RE form the biasing and stabilization circuit.

(ii) Input Capacitance Cin

This is used to couple the signal to the base of the transistor. If this is not used, the signal source resistance will come across R2 and thus changes the bias condition of the transistor. The capacitor Cin allows only a.c. signal to flow.

(iii) Emitter Bypass Capacitor CE

This is connected in parallel with RE to provide a low reactance path to the amplified a.c. signal. If it is not used, then amplified a.c. signal flowing through RE will cause a voltage drop across it, thereby shifting the output voltage.

(iv) Coupling Capacitor C

This is used to couple the amplified signal to the output device. This capacitor C allows only a.c. signal to flow.

(v) Working

  • When a weak input a.c. signal is applied to the base of the transistor, a small base current flows.
  • Due to transistor action, a much larger a.c. current flows through collector load RC, a large voltage appears across RC and hence at the output.
  • Therefore, a weak signal applied to the base appears in amplified form at the collector of circuit.
  • Voltage gain (Av) of the amplifier is the ratio of the amplified output voltage to the input voltage.

(vi) Frequency Response and Bandwidth

Frequency Response Curve: Gain (A_v) on Y-axis vs Frequency on X-axis (log scale).
• A_M: midband (maximum) gain level
• A_M / √2 ≈ 0.707 A_M: −3 dB points
• F_L: lower cutoff frequency (left −3 dB point)
• F_H: upper cutoff frequency (right −3 dB point)
• Three regions: Low frequency region (gain rises), Mid frequency region (flat, maximum gain), High frequency region (gain falls)
• BW = F_H − F_L (bandwidth)
Bandwidth
\[ BW = F_H - F_L \]

Op-Amp Parameters

Input Offset Voltage

The input offset voltage is the differential DC voltage that must be applied between the input terminals (inverting and non-inverting) of the op-amp to make the output voltage zero. Ideally its value is 0 V. Its value is 1 mV to 6 mV for 741.

Output Offset Voltage

The output offset voltage of an operational amplifier refers to the small DC voltage that appears at its output even when the input terminals are shorted together and no input signal is applied. This offset is caused by internal mismatches in the op-amp, such as differences in the transistor parameters or imbalances in the internal circuitry. Ideally its value is 0 V.

Input Resistance

The input resistance of an operational amplifier (op-amp) refers to the effective resistance seen at its input terminals. It is generally measured in open-loop condition. Ideally its value is infinity. Its value of 2 MΩ for 741 under open loop condition.

Output Resistance

The output resistance of the operational amplifier is the resistance seen at the output terminal when the op-amp is configured in an open-loop condition. Ideally, its value is 0 ohm. Its value is 75 ohms for 741.

Gain Bandwidth

FIG. 10.47 — Gain versus frequency plot (log scale): A_v on Y-axis, Frequency on X-axis.
• A_VD: open-loop DC gain (very high, ~100,000)
• 0.707 A_VD: −3 dB cutoff point at f_c
• f_c: cutoff frequency (3 dB bandwidth)
• f_1: unity-gain frequency (gain = 1)
• B_1: gain-bandwidth product region

Gain Bandwidth: The frequency at which the gain drops by 3 dB is known as the cutoff frequency fc of the Op-Amp. The unity-gain frequency f1 and cutoff frequency are related by:

\[ f_1 = A_{VD} f_c \]

Unity-gain frequency may also be called the gain-bandwidth product of the op-amp. Ideally its value is infinity. Its value is 1 MHz for 741.

Slew Rate

Slew rate: The maximum rate at which amplifier output can change in volts per microsecond is known as Slew rate. The slew rate provides a parameter specifying the maximum rate of change of the output voltage when driven by a large step-input signal. Ideal value of slew rate is infinity. It is 0.5 V/μ seconds for 741.

Slew Rate
\[ SR = \frac{\Delta V_o}{\Delta t}\;\text{V}/\mu\text{s} \qquad\text{with }t\text{ in }\mu\text{s} \]

Common Mode Rejection Ratio (CMRR)

Common Mode Rejection Ratio: It is the ratio of differential gain to the common mode gain of the amplifier. Ideally, CMRR is infinity. Typically, its value is 90 dB for 741.

\[ CMRR = \frac{A_d}{A_c} \]
where
\[ A_d = \text{Differential gain of the amplifier} \] \[ A_c = \text{Common mode gain of the amplifier} \]

The value of CMRR can also be expressed in logarithmic terms:

\[ CMRR(log) = 20\log_{10}\left\{\frac{A_d}{A_c}\right\}\;\text{dB} \]

Virtual Ground Concept

  • For example, if Vo = −10 V and gain Av = 20,000, then the input voltage Vi = −V0/Av = −10/20,000 = 0.5 mV.
  • If the circuit has an overall gain of 1, then value of V1 is 10 V.
  • Compared to all other input and output voltages, the value of Vi is very small and can be considered as 0 V.
  • The fact that Vi ≈ 0 V leads to the concept that at the amplifier input there exists a virtual short-circuit or virtual ground.

  • The heavy line in the figure is used to indicate that a short exists with Vi ≈ 0 V.
  • Current will not flow through short and ground and flows only through resistors R1 and Rf as shown.

Practical Op-Amp Circuits

1. Noninverting Amplifier

Noninverting Amplifier: Input V1 applied to + (non-inverting) terminal. R_f from output to − (inverting) terminal. R1 from inverting terminal to ground. Feedback network R1 and R_f form voltage divider. Output: V_o = (1 + R_f/R_1) × V_1
  • Input is given to noninverting terminal of an Amplifier.
  • Voltage across R1 is V1 (since Vi = 0 V) and is equal to the output voltage, through a voltage divider of R1 and Rf and is given by:
\[ V_1 = \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_f} V_o \] \[ \boxed{\frac{V_o}{V_1} = \frac{R_1+R_f}{R_1} = 1 + \frac{R_f}{R_1}} \]

2. Inverting Amplifier

Inverting Amplifier: Input V1 → R1 → node A (inverting, −) → op-amp → V_o. R_f from output back to node A. Non-inverting (+) terminal B grounded. V_A = V_B = 0 (Virtual ground). Output: V_o = −(R_f / R_1) × V_1

VA = VB = 0 (Virtual ground)

At Input: I = (V1 − VA)/R1 = V1/R1 ——(1)

At Output: I = (VA − VO)/Rf = −(VO/Rf) ——(2)

Equating 1 and 2:

Output Voltage — Inverting Amplifier
\[ \boxed{V_O = -\frac{R_f}{R_1} \cdot V_1} \] \[\text{Gain} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}\]
  • Input is given to inverting terminal of the amplifier.
  • Inverting amplifier is the most widely used constant-gain amplifier circuit.
  • The output is obtained by multiplying the input by a fixed or constant gain, set by the input resistor R1 and feedback resistor Rf and output is being inverted from the input.
  • Output Voltage of Inverting amplifier is VO = −(Rf/R1) × V1 (from virtual ground concept).

Ideal OP-Amp Negative Feedback

Negative Feedback circuit: V_in → (+) input of op-amp A → V_out. Feedback network: R1 and R2 voltage divider from output back to (−) input. V_f = feedback voltage across R2.
\[ \text{Closed loop Gain} = \frac{V_{\text{out}}}{V_{\text{in}}} = 1 + \frac{R_1}{R_2} \] \[ \text{Feedback}\;\beta = \frac{V_f}{V_{\text{out}}} = \frac{R_2}{R_2+R_2} \]

3. Unity Follower (Voltage Follower)

Fig b — Equivalent: V_i ≈ 0 between + and − (virtual short), since both are at V1.

4. Summing Amplifier

Fig a — Three-input inverting summing amplifier: V1→R1, V2→R2, V3→R3 all connect to inverting (−) input node. R_f from that node to output. Non-inverting (+) grounded.
Fig b — Equivalent with virtual ground: V_i ≈ 0 at summing node.

  • If more inputs are used, they each add an additional component to the output.

Output of a Summing Amplifier

Applying KCL at node A:

\[ i_1 + i_2 + i_3 = i_f \qquad\text{---(1)} \] \[ i_1 = \frac{v_1 - v_A}{R_1},\quad i_2 = \frac{v_2-v_A}{R_2},\quad i_3 = \frac{v_3-v_A}{R_3},\quad i_f = \frac{v_A-v_{\text{out}}}{R_f} \qquad\text{----(2)} \]

Substituting eq (2) in (1) and considering VA = 0 (Virtual ground):

\[ \frac{v_1}{R_1} + \frac{v_2}{R_2} + \frac{v_3}{R_3} = \frac{-v_{\text{out}}}{R_f} \] \[ \boxed{v_{\text{out}} = -R_f\left(\frac{v_1}{R_1}+\frac{v_2}{R_2}+\frac{v_3}{R_3}\right)} \]

6. Voltage Subtraction (Difference Amplifier)

Voltage Subtractor: V1 → R1 → node A (inverting −). V2 → R2 → node B (non-inverting +). R_f from output to node A. R_f from node B to ground. Superposition used for analysis. When R1 = R2 = R_f: V_o = V2 − V1.
  • Superposition principle is used to find the relation between output and input.
  • Let Vo1 be the output, with input V1, assuming V2 to be zero. And Vo2 be the output, with input V2, assuming V1 to be zero.
  • With V2 zero, the circuit acts as an inverting amplifier and the output equation is:
\[ V_{o1} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}V_1 \qquad\text{------(1)} \]

While with V1 as zero, the circuit reduces as shown. Let potential of node B be VB. The potential of node A is same as B i.e. VA = VB (Virtual short). Applying voltage divider rule to the input V2 loop:

\[ V_B = \frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}V_2 \qquad\text{------(2)} \] \[ I = \frac{V_A}{R_1} = \frac{V_B}{R_1} \qquad\text{------(3)} \] \[ I = \frac{V_{o2}-V_A}{R_f} = \frac{V_{o2}-V_B}{R_f} \qquad\text{------(4)} \]

Equating the equations (3) and (4):

\[ \frac{V_B}{R_1} = \frac{V_{o2}-V_B}{R_f} \] \[ V_{o2} = \frac{R_1+R_f}{R_1}V_B = \left[1+\frac{R_f}{R_1}\right]V_B \qquad\text{-----(5)} \]

Substituting VB from (2) in (5) we get:

\[ V_{o2} = \left[1+\frac{R_f}{R_1}\right]\left[\frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}\right]V_2 \]

Hence using Superposition principle:

\[ V_o = V_{o1} + V_{o2} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}V_1 + \left[1+\frac{R_f}{R_1}\right]\left[\frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}\right]V_2 \]

If R1 = R2:

\[ V_o = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}V_1 + \frac{R_f}{R_1}V_2 \] \[ \boxed{V_o = +\frac{R_f}{R_1}(V_2-V_1)} \]
  • The output voltage is proportional to the difference between the two input voltages. Thus it acts as a Subtractor using Op Amp circuit or difference amplifier.

If R1 = R2 = Rf is selected, then:

\[ V_o = V_2 - V_1 \]

Comparators

FIG. 13.1 — Comparator unit:
(a) Basic unit: − input, + input, output. Powered by +V and −V supply.
(b) Typical application: V_ref (+2 V) at − (inverting) input, V_in at + (non-inverting) input. When V_in > 2 V → output goes HIGH (~+10 V). When V_in < 2 V → output goes LOW (~−10 V).
  • A comparator circuit accepts input of linear voltages and provides a digital output that indicates when one input is less than or greater than the second.
  • A basic comparator circuit can be represented as in Fig. 13.1 a.
  • The output is a digital signal that stays at a high voltage level when the noninverting (+) input is greater than the voltage at the inverting (−) input and switches to a lower voltage level when the noninverting input voltage goes below the inverting input voltage.
  • Figure 13.1 b shows a typical connection with one input (the inverting input in this example) connected to a reference voltage of 2 V, the non-inverting input terminal connected as the input signal voltage. As long as Vin is less than the reference voltage level of 2 V, the output remains at a low voltage level (near 10 V). When the input rises just above 2 V, the output quickly switches to a high-voltage level (near 10 V). Thus the high output indicates that the input signal is greater than 2 V.
FIG. 13.2 — Operation of a 741 op-amp as a comparator:
(a) Circuit: Inverting (−) at pin 2 grounded (0 V reference). Non-inverting (+) at pin 3 receives sinusoidal input V_i. Powered ±V.
(b) Waveforms: Top — sinusoidal V_i (linear). Bottom — digital square wave output V_o switching between +V_sat and −V_sat as V_i crosses 0 V.
  • With reference input (at pin 2) set to 0 V, a sinusoidal signal applied to the noninverting input (pin 3) will cause the output to switch between its two output states, as shown in Fig. 13.2 b.
  • The input Vi going even a fraction of a millivolt above the 0-V reference level will be amplified by the very high voltage gain (typically over 100,000), so that the output rises to its positive output saturation level and remains there, while the input stays above Vref = 0 V.
  • When the input drops just below the zero volts reference level, the output is driven to its lower saturation level and stays there, while the input remains below Vref = 0 V.
  • Figure 13.2 b clearly shows that the input signal is linear, whereas the output is digital.
