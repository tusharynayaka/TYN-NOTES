1. An Introduction to Semiconductor

Semiconductor: A special class of elements having a conductivity between that of a good conductor and that of an insulator.

The three semiconductors used most frequently in the construction of electronic devices are Ge (Germanium), Si (Silicon), and GaAs (Gallium Arsenide).

1.1 Atomic Structure

Both silicon and germanium have four valence electrons in their outermost shell. Silicon atoms form a crystal lattice by sharing these electrons with neighboring atoms (covalent bonding).

1.2 Energy Bands

Electrons in atoms occupy discrete energy levels. When atoms are brought together in a solid, these discrete levels broaden into energy bands. The key bands are:

  • Valence Band: The outermost shell occupied by valence electrons (bound to atoms).
  • Conduction Band: The band where electrons are free to move and establish conduction.
  • Energy Gap (Eg): The gap between valence and conduction bands.

1.3 Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material that has been carefully refined to reduce the number of impurities to a very low level — essentially as pure as can be made available through modern technology.
Extrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material that has been subjected to the doping process is called an extrinsic material.

In pure (intrinsic) silicon, each Si atom shares its 4 valence electrons with 4 neighbors, forming covalent bonds. This creates a complete valence shell for each atom, leaving no free carriers at 0 K.

1.4 N-Type Semiconductor

An n-type material is created by introducing impurity elements that have five valence electrons (pentavalent), such as:

  • Antimony (Sb)
  • Arsenic (As)
  • Phosphorus (P)

When a pentavalent atom replaces a silicon atom in the lattice, four of its five valence electrons form covalent bonds with neighboring Si atoms. The fifth electron is free to move through the lattice, contributing to electrical conduction.

Donor Atoms: Diffused impurities with five valence electrons are called donor atoms because they “donate” a free electron to the material.
In n-type material:
  • Majority carriers: Electrons (negative)
  • Minority carriers: Holes (positive)
  • Donor ions (positive) are fixed in the lattice

1.5 P-Type Semiconductor

The p-type material is formed by doping a pure germanium or silicon crystal with impurity atoms having three valence electrons (trivalent). The elements most frequently used are:

  • Boron (B)
  • Gallium (Ga)
  • Indium (In)

When a trivalent atom is placed in the silicon lattice, it can only form three covalent bonds. The fourth bond position has a void (hole) — a missing electron that acts as a positive charge carrier.

Acceptor Atoms: The diffused impurities with three valence electrons are called acceptor atoms because they “accept” an electron from the lattice, creating a hole.
In p-type material:
  • Majority carriers: Holes (positive)
  • Minority carriers: Electrons (negative)
  • Acceptor ions (negative) are fixed in the lattice

2. An Introduction to the Semiconductor Diode

Diode: A two-electrode (two-terminal) device. We can construct our first solid-state electronic device — the semiconductor diode — by sandwiching p-type and n-type material together to form a depletion region between the two.

2.1 Biasing of Diodes

  • Forward Bias: p-type connected to positive terminal, n-type to negative terminal of voltage source.
  • Reverse Bias: p-type connected to negative terminal, n-type to positive terminal.

2.2 No-Bias Condition

At the instant the two materials are joined, the electrons and the holes in the region of the junction will combine, resulting in a lack of free carriers in the region near the junction.

Depletion Region: This region of uncovered positive and negative ions is called the depletion region due to the “depletion” of free carriers in the region.
Key fact: In the absence of an applied bias across a semiconductor diode, the net flow of charge in one direction is zero. \( I_D = 0 \text{ mA}, \; V_D = 0 \text{ V} \)

2.3 Forward-Bias Condition

A forward-bias or “on” condition is established by applying the positive potential to the p-type material and the negative potential to the n-type material.

The application of a forward-bias potential \(V_D\) will “pressure” electrons in the n-type material and holes in the p-type material to recombine with the ions near the boundary and reduce the width of the depletion region.

The current that exists under forward-bias conditions is called the forward-bias current: \[ I_D = I_{\text{majority}} - I_s \]

2.4 Reverse-Bias Condition

When a P-N junction is reverse-biased, the depletion region widens. This establishes too great a barrier for the majority carriers to overcome, effectively reducing the majority carrier flow to zero.

The current that exists under reverse-bias conditions is called the reverse saturation current and is represented by \(I_s\).

3. Semiconductor Diode Characteristics & Parameters

3.1 Forward Bias Parameters

  • Knee Voltage (VF): The forward voltage at which the diode begins to conduct significantly.
    • Silicon (Si): ~0.7 V
    • Germanium (Ge): ~0.3 V
  • Forward Current Rating: Maximum allowable forward current.
  • Maximum Power Dissipation: P = VD × ID

3.2 Reverse Bias Parameters

  • Reverse Saturation Current (Is): Very small current due to minority carriers.
  • Breakdown Voltage (VBR): Reverse voltage at which large reverse current flows.
  • Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) Rating: Maximum reverse voltage the diode can withstand.

4. Diode Current Expression — Shockley's Current Equation

Shockley's Equation — also called the diode equation — helps us determine the current at a given temperature knowing the diode voltage and saturation current.

\[ I_D = I_S \left( e^{\frac{qV_D}{nkT}} - 1 \right) \]

4.1 Variables and Constants

\(V_D\)Diode voltageVolts (V)
\(I_S\)Reverse saturation currentAmperes (A)
\(q\)Charge of electron1.6 × 10¹&sup9; C

\(T\)TemperatureKelvin (K)
\(V_T = kT/q\)Thermal voltage≈ 25.9 mV at 300 K (room temp)

4.2 Special Cases

  • When \(V_D\) is negative (Reverse bias):
    \[ I_D \approx -I_S \]
  • When \(V_D\) is positive (Forward bias):
    \[ I_D \approx I_S \, e^{\frac{qV_D}{nkT}} \]

4.3 Thermal Voltage

\[ V_T = \frac{kT}{q} = \frac{T}{11600} \text{ (V)} \]

At room temperature (T = 300 K): \(V_T \approx 25.9 \text{ mV} \approx 26 \text{ mV}\)

7. Types of Diode Breakdown

7.1 Avalanche Breakdown

In the reverse bias condition, as the voltage increases, the free charge carriers obtain velocity and associated kinetic energy and release additional carriers through collision with other atomic structures. This cumulative process is called Avalanche multiplication or ionization process.

Avalanche breakdown is observed in diodes with Zener breakdown voltage Vz > 8 V.

7.2 Zener Breakdown

Zener breakdown occurs when both P and N type material are heavily doped in a semiconductor diode. Hence the depletion layer region is narrowed down. When a small reverse bias voltage is applied, a very strong electric field is produced and covalent bonds break.

Zener breakdown is observed in diodes with Zener breakdown voltage Vz of 5 to 8 V.

8. Temperature Effects on V-I Characteristics of a Diode

  • Rise in temperature generates more electron-hole pairs, thus conductivity increases and current increases.
  • PN junction diode parameters like reverse saturation current, bias current, reverse breakdown voltage, and barrier voltage are dependent on temperature.
  • Increase in the temperature increases carrier concentration. As a result: Knee voltage decreases, Breakdown voltage increases, Reverse saturation current increases.

8.1 Under Forward Bias

Barrier voltage is dependent on temperature — it decreases by 2.5 mV/°C rise in temperature for both Germanium and Silicon.

8.2 Under Reverse Bias

Reverse saturation current (IS) increases with temperature. The rise is 7%/°C for both Germanium & Silicon and approximately doubles for every 10°C rise in temperature.

8.3 Summary Rules

  1. The reverse saturation current Is will double in magnitude for every 10°C increase in temperature.
  2. The Knee voltage shifts to the left at a rate of 2.5 mV per degree centigrade increase in temperature.

9. Ideal and Practical Diode Characteristics

9.1 Ideal Diode

Ideal Diode: When an ideal diode is forward biased, it offers no resistance and acts like a closed switch. Likewise, the ideal diode under reverse bias offers infinite resistance and acts like an open switch.

Characteristics: \(V_d = 0\) V when forward biased, \(I_D = 0\) A when reverse biased.

9.2 Practical Diode

Practical Diode: A diode which is said to be forward biased, it starts conducting at the knee voltage (VK). For Si: \(V_K = 0.7 \text{ V}\); For Ge: \(V_K = 0.3 \text{ V}\)

10. Diode Resistances

An actual diode offers a very small resistance (not zero) when forward biased — called forward resistance. Whereas, it offers a very high resistance (not infinite) when reverse biased — called reverse resistance.

  1. DC or Static Resistance
  2. AC or Dynamic Resistance
  3. Average AC Resistance

10.1 DC or Static Resistance (RD)

\[ R_D = \frac{V_D}{I_D} \]
Typical DC resistance: Forward bias: 10 Ω to 80 Ω | Reverse bias: 10 MΩ

10.2 AC or Dynamic Resistance (rd)

\[ r_d = \frac{\Delta V_d}{\Delta I_d} = \frac{26 \text{ mV}}{I_D} \text{ (ohms)} \]

Including bulk resistance:

\[ r'_d = \frac{26 \text{ mV}}{I_D} + r_B \text{ (ohms)} \]
Typically, the AC resistance of a diode in the active region will range from 1 Ω to 100 Ω.

10.3 Average AC Resistance (rav)

\[ r_{av} = \frac{\Delta V_d}{\Delta I_d}\bigg|_{\text{pt. to pt.}} \]

11. Diode Equivalent Circuits / Diode Approximations

Diode Approximation: A mathematical method used to approximate the nonlinear behavior of real diodes to enable calculations and circuit analysis.
  1. Piecewise-Linear Equivalent Circuit
  2. Simplified Diode Characteristics
  3. Ideal Diode Characteristics

11.1 Piecewise-Linear Equivalent Circuit

  • Under forward-bias: The diode behaves as a practical voltage source equal to the knee voltage, in series with the diode's average AC resistance. Formula: \(V_d = V_B + I_d \times R_B\)
  • Under reverse-bias: The diode is an open switch and no current flows through it.

11.2 Simplified Diode Characteristics

  • Under forward-bias: The diode turns ON when VK is applied. Thus, \(V_d = V_B\).
  • Under reverse bias: It is an open switch.
For Si: VB = 0.7 V; For Ge: VB = 0.3 V

11.3 Ideal Diode Characteristics

  • The diode is considered as a closed switch with zero voltage drop when forward-biased (\(V_d = 0\)).
  • It acts as an open switch with zero current when reverse biased.

13. Logical Operations

Logic Gate: A basic building block of digital circuits that has the ability to produce one output level with the combinations of input levels. Example: AND, OR and NOT functions.

Truth Table: A table with all combinations of input variables and the corresponding output. It explains how the logic circuit output responds to various combinations of logic levels at the inputs.

14.2 Series Clippers

The series configuration is defined as one where the diode is in series with the load.

14.2.1 Biased Series Clipper

The addition of a DC supply to the network can have a pronounced effect on the analysis of the series clipper configuration.

General instructions for analysis:

  1. Take careful note of where the output voltage is defined.
  2. Try to develop an overall sense of the response by simply noting how the input supply affects the conventional current direction through the diode.
  3. Determine the “transition voltage” that will result in a change of state for the diode.
  4. It is often helpful to draw the output waveform directly below the applied voltage.

For the “ON” region:

\[ v_o = v_i - V \]

For the “OFF” region (diode open circuit, ID = 0 mA):

\[ v_o = 0 \text{ V} \]

Peak output voltage:

\[ v_{o\text{peak}} = V_m - V \]
Example 1 — Biased Series Clipper

Q: Determine the output waveform for sinusoidal input. Vm = 20 V, V = 5 V, R in series, Si diode.

Solution:

Transition: \( v_i + 5 \text{ V} = 0 \Rightarrow v_i = -5 \text{ V} \)

For voltages less than −5 V the diode is open and the output is 0 V. For the ON condition: \( v_o = v_i + 5 \text{ V} \)

Peak output: \(v_o = 20 + 5 = 25 \text{ V}\)

Note: The analysis of clipper networks with square-wave inputs is actually easier than with sinusoidal inputs because only two levels have to be considered.
Example 2 — Square Wave Input

Q: Find the output voltage for the network in Example 1 if the applied signal is a square wave: +20 V (0→T/2), −10 V (T/2→T).

Solution:

For vi = 20 V: Diode “ON”. \( v_o = 20 + 5 = 25 \text{ V} \)

For vi = −10 V: Diode “OFF”. \( v_o = 0 \text{ V} \)

15. Clampers

Clamper: A circuit with a diode, resistor, and capacitor that shifts a waveform to a new DC level without altering its shape. Also known as a DC restorer.
  • Clamping networks have a capacitor connected directly from input.
  • A resistive element connected in parallel with the output signal.
  • The diode is also in parallel with the output signal but may or may not have a series DC supply as an added element.

15.1 Steps Used for Analysis

  1. Step 1: Examine the response of the portion of the input signal that will forward bias the diode.
  2. Step 2: During the on period of the diode, assume that the capacitor will charge up instantaneously.
  3. Step 3: Assume the capacitor retains its voltage while the diode is “off.”
  4. Step 4: Track the position and polarity of vo to ensure correct levels.
  5. Step 5: Check that the total swing of the output matches that of the input.

15.2 Clamper Numericals

Example 1 — Clamper

Q: Determine Vo for the network. Square wave: +10 V (t1→t2), −20 V (t2→t3); C = 1 μF, R = 100 kΩ, V = 5 V battery.

Solution:

For t1→t2: Diode ON, \(v_o = 5 \text{ V}\). KVL: \(-20 + V_C - 5 = 0 \Rightarrow V_C = 25 \text{ V}\)

For t2→t3: Diode OFF. \(+10 + 25 - v_o = 0 \Rightarrow v_o = 35 \text{ V}\)

\[ \zeta = RC = (100\text{ k}\Omega)(0.1\;\mu\text{F}) = 10\text{ ms},\quad 5\zeta = 50\text{ ms} \]

Output swing of 30 V matches the input swing.

Example 2 — With Silicon Diode (VK = 0.7 V)

Q: Repeat Example 1, using a silicon diode with Vk = 0.7 V.

Solution:
\[ v_o = 5 - 0.7 = 4.3 \text{ V} \]\[ V_C = 24.3 \text{ V} \]\[ v_o \text{ (off period)} = 10 + 24.3 = 34.3 \text{ V} \]

Input and output swings remain the same (30 V swing).

Regulated Power Supply

Power supplies are designed for a dedicated load and environment. But sometimes main supply voltage, load, and surrounding temperature keep changing, altering the component parameters and hence changing the output voltage.

Regulated power supply converts an alternating current signal to a constant signal.

Block diagram of regulated power supply:
120 V rms → Transformer → Rectifier → Filter → IC Regulator → Load
Each stage progressively smooths the signal: sinusoidal AC → half-wave/full-wave DC → ripple-reduced DC → constant regulated DC.

Block Diagram Components

  • Transformer: The output sinusoidal voltage is either step-up or step-down from the input sinusoidal voltage value.
  • Rectifier: Converts an alternating current into a direct one by allowing the current to flow through it in one direction only.
  • Filter: Removes the AC ripples from the DC signal obtained from the rectifier.
  • Regulator: Converts DC voltage into a lower constant voltage.

Half Wave Rectifier (HWR)

HWR – Ideal Diode

The input waveform is \(v_i = V_m \sin \omega t\). The HWR circuit consists of a diode in series with a load resistor \(R_L\).

Positive Half Cycle:

Conduction region \(0 \to T/2\). The diode is forward biased.

\[ I_m = \frac{V_m}{R_L},\quad V_o = V_m \]

Negative Half Cycle:

Non-conduction region \(T/2 \to T\). The diode is reverse biased.

\[ I = 0,\quad V_o = 0 \]

HWR – Non-Ideal Diode

For a silicon diode with knee voltage \(V_k = 0.7\,\text{V}\), the diode only conducts when \(v_i > V_k\). The output is offset by \(V_k\).

\[ V_{o\,\max} = V_m - V_k \]\[ I_{o\,\max} = \frac{V_m - V_k}{R_L} \]

Average Voltage / Current for HWR – Ideal Diode

\[ V_{dc} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} V_m \sin\omega t\, d\omega t = \frac{V_m}{\pi} \]
\[ \boxed{V_{dc} = \frac{V_m}{\pi} = 0.318\,V_m \quad (\text{half-wave})} \]\[ I_{dc} = \frac{V_{dc}}{R_L} = \frac{I_m}{\pi} \]

RMS Voltage / Current for HWR – Ideal Diode

\[ V_{rms} = \left[\frac{V_m^2}{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\frac{1-\cos 2\omega t}{2}\,d\omega t\right]^{1/2} = \frac{V_m}{2} \]\[ I_{rms} = \frac{V_m}{2R_L} = \frac{I_m}{2} \]

Average & RMS for HWR – Non-Ideal Diode

\[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_m - V_k}{\pi} \]\[ I_{dc} = \frac{V_m - V_k}{\pi R_L} = \frac{I_m}{\pi} \quad \text{where } I_m = \frac{V_m - V_k}{R_L} \]\[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_m - V_k}{2} \]\[ I_{rms} = \frac{V_m - V_k}{2R_L} = \frac{I_m}{2} \]

Ripple Factor of HWR

Ripple Factor is a measure of purity of the dc output of a rectifier:

\[ r = \frac{\text{rms value of the ac component of the output}}{\text{average or dc value of the output}} \]\[ \gamma_{HWR} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_{rms}}{V_{dc}}\right)^2 - 1} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_m/2}{V_m/\pi}\right)^2 - 1} \]\[ V'_{rms} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_m}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{V_m}{\pi}\right)^2} = 0.385\,V_m \]\[ \therefore \text{Ripple factor} = 1.21 \]

Half Wave Rectifier PIV

Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) is the maximum reverse biased voltage the diode can withstand without entering the breakdown region. During the negative half cycle, the full source voltage appears across the reverse-biased diode.

\[ \boxed{\text{PIV rating} \geq V_m \quad \text{(half-wave rectifier)}} \]

Full Wave Rectifier: Centre Tap (Ideal Diode)

A centre-tap transformer with turns ratio 1:2 provides two equal secondary voltages. Two diodes D1 and D2 conduct on alternating half-cycles.

Circuit Description: 1:2 centre-tap transformer. Terminal A connects through D1, terminal B through D2, centre tap (CT) is the ground reference. Load R is connected from the diode junction to CT.

Positive Half Cycle:

Diode D1 conducts; D2 is reverse-biased.

\[ V_o = V_m,\quad I_o = \frac{V_m}{R_L} \]

Negative Half Cycle:

Diode D2 conducts; D1 is reverse-biased.

\[ V_o = V_m,\quad I_m = \frac{V_m}{R_L} \]

Both half-cycles produce a positive output: \(V_{dc} = 0.636\,V_m\).

Four diodes (D1, D2, D3, D4) arranged in a bridge configuration.

Shunt Capacitor Filter

Filter is a circuit which converts pulsating DC to smooth DC. By connecting a capacitor in parallel with the load, the ac ripple present at the output of a rectifier can be reduced.

Basic Capacitor Filter: AC input → Rectifier circuit → [Capacitor C in parallel with DC load RL] → Output. The capacitor filters the ripple from the rectifier output.

FWR with Shunt Capacitor Filter

FWR with Shunt Capacitor Filter Circuit: 230 V, 50 Hz AC Supply → Centre-tap transformer → D1 (from terminal A) and D2 (from terminal B) → Junction point X → Capacitor C in parallel with RL → Output Vo.

Working

During positive half cycle the diode D1 is forward biased and the capacitor C will charge from 0 V towards the peak value of input Vm. When input voltage Vi reaches Vm the charge on capacitor will also be equal to Vm, hence the diodes will turn off (since both anode and cathode of diodes will be at same potential). Now the capacitor starts discharging through the load RL. If the load is very light (load resistance is very high and load current is low) the capacitor will discharge by a small amount, by that time next half cycle charges the capacitor back to Vm. Thus the output voltage remains almost constant.

Capacitor Filter — Output Voltage Waveform

Output voltage waveform with capacitor filter: The waveform shows the capacitor charging up to Vm during time T1 (diodes conducting), then discharging slightly during time T2 (diodes off). Peak-to-peak ripple voltage (Vr)p-p is marked. Average DC level Vdc is close to Vm.
  • Time T1: The time during which diodes conduct, charging the capacitor up to Vm.
  • Time T2: The time interval during which the rectifier voltage drops below Vm, and the capacitor discharges through the load.

Expression for Ripple Factor — Shunt Capacitor Filter (FWR)

\[ \gamma = \frac{1}{4\sqrt{3}\,f\,C\,R_L} \]\[ \gamma \propto \frac{1}{f} \cdot \frac{1}{C} \cdot \frac{1}{R_L} \]\[ V_{r(rms)} = \frac{I_{DC}}{4\sqrt{3}\,fc},\quad V_{dc} = V_m - \frac{I_{DC}}{4\,fc} \]

Shunt Capacitor Filter for Half-Wave Rectifier

Capacitor C is connected in shunt/parallel with load resistor RL.

Ripple Factor for Capacitor Filter of a Half Wave Rectifier:

\[ \text{Ripple Factor (HWR with C)} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}\,f\,C\,R_L} \]\[ V_{r(rms)} = \frac{I_{DC}}{2\sqrt{3}\,fc},\quad V_{dc} = V_m - \frac{I_{DC}}{2\,fc} \]

Zener Diode

  • Heavily Doped PN-Junction Device specially designed to operate under breakdown region.
  • Zener Diode under forward bias condition works like a normal semiconductor diode.
  • Zener Diode under reverse bias condition acts like Voltage regulator.

When the reverse voltage across the junction is increased the electric field across the depletion layer becomes high so that electrons are pulled out of covalent bonds resulting in a sudden rise in current which is known as zener breakdown.

Zener Diode Characteristics

Symbol of Zener Diode: Like a regular diode but with bent ends on the cathode bar. The black colour ring denotes the negative terminal.

V-I Characteristics: In Forward Bias: Behaves like a normal diode. In Reverse Bias: Small reverse current (Io) until breakdown at −VZ; then current rises sharply. The voltage remains nearly constant at VZ for a wide range of currents from IZ(min) to IZ(max). This is the “Constant Zener Voltage” region.

Zener Diode as Voltage Regulator

Conditions required to operate Zener Diode as Voltage Regulator:

  • Zener Diode should be in Reverse Biased Condition.
  • \(V_{in}\) should be Greater than \(V_Z\).
  • \(I_Z\) should be greater than \(I_{Zmin}\).
  • \(I_Z\) should be less than or equal to \(I_{Zmax}\).
Zener Voltage Regulator Circuit: Vi (variable input) → Series resistor R → Node → Zener diode VZ (in parallel) → Ground. Load RL in parallel with Zener. IZ flows through Zener, IL flows through RL, IR = IZ + IL flows through R.

BJT Transistor: Introduction

A bipolar junction transistor (BJT) is a three terminal device consisting of either a p-type semiconductor or n-type semiconductor sandwiched between opposite types. It has two p–n junctions connected back-to-back.

In a BJT, the operation depends on the active participation of both the majority carriers, and the minority carriers; hence the name "bipolar".

The combination of two terms transfer + resistor results in TRANSISTOR as the current is transferred from a low to a high-resistance circuit.

NPN structure: N (emitter) | P (base) | N (collector) — two back-to-back p-n junctions.
PNP structure: P (emitter) | N (base) | P (collector) — two back-to-back p-n junctions (reversed).

BJT Transistor: Construction

The three terminals of a Bipolar junction transistor are Emitter, Base & Collector.

Terminals

The Emitter (E) is the portion of the transistor that supplies charge carriers (either electrons or holes) and it is a heavily doped region. The area is moderate in size.

The Base (B) is the middle portion of the transistor that forms two PN junction between the emitter and the collector and it is a lightly doped region. It is the smallest in width of all the three regions.

The Collector (C) is the portion of the transistor that collects charge carriers (either electrons or holes) and it is a moderately doped region. It is the widest of all the three regions as maximum heat is dissipated in this region.

NPN symbol: Emitter arrow points outward (away from base). PNP symbol: Emitter arrow points inward (toward base). Both have three terminals: E (Emitter), B (Base), C (Collector).

Types of BJTs

1. PNP Transistor

PNP structure: Emitter (p⁺) | Base (n) | Collector (p)
Emitter "emits" holes → Narrow Base controls number of holes emitted → Collector "collects" holes emitted by the emitter

2. NPN Transistor

NPN structure: Emitter (n⁺) | Base (p) | Collector (n)
Emitter "emits" electrons → Narrow Base controls number of electrons emitted → Collector "collects" electrons emitted by the emitter

  • 1) Common Base (CB) configuration
  • 1) Common Emitter (CE) configuration
  • 1) Common Collector (CC) configuration

BJT Operation

The transistor operated in Common Base configuration, with the external sources, VEE and VCC, connected as shown:

  • The emitter – base junction is forward biased
  • The collector – base junction is reverse biased

As the EB junction is forward biased the current flow is because of majority charge carriers.

Similarly as the CB junction is reverse biased the current flow is because of minority charge carriers (Hence wider depletion region)

Forward Bias (EB junction): Majority carriers flow from emitter into base region. Depletion region is narrow.
Reverse Bias (CB junction): Minority carriers are swept across. Depletion region is wide.

Currents in a BJT

  • A large number of majority carriers will diffuse across the forward biased p–n junction into the n-type material.
  • Since the n-type material is very thin and lightly doped, a very small number of these (holes) will recombine with the electrons in base region and flow through the base terminal as base current (is typically in the order of microamperes).
  • Thus, a larger number of these majority carriers from the emitter region will diffuse across the reverse-biased junction into the p-type material connected to the collector terminal.

BJT Operation (pnp Transistor) — Currents

Applying KCL to the transistor, we get:

KCL — Transistor Currents
\[ I_E = I_C + I_B \]

The collector current comprises two components — the majority and the minority carriers.

The minority-current is called as leakage current (ICO — IC with emitter terminal Open).

Therefore the total collector current is given as:

Total Collector Current
\[ I_C = I_{C_{\text{majority}}} + I_{CO_{\text{minority}}} \]

BJT Operation (npn Transistor) — Currents

Emitter current is the sum of the collector and base currents:

\[ I_E = I_C + I_B \]

The collector current is comprised of two currents:

\[ I_C = I_{C\,(\text{Majority})} + I_{CO\,(\text{Minority})} \]
NPN current directions: I_E flows into emitter terminal (conventional), I_C flows out of collector, I_B flows into base. The majority carrier flow in the n-p-n is from emitter to collector (electron flow).

Base Width Modulation (Early Effect)

A greater reverse bias across the collector-base junction increases the collector-base depletion width. There is a variation in the width of the base in a bipolar transistor due to a variation in the applied base-to-collector voltage.

Base-narrowing has two consequences that affect the current:

  • There is a lesser chance for recombination within the "smaller" base region.
  • The charge gradient is increased across the base, and consequently, the current of minority carriers injected across the collector-base junction increases.

The Early effect is observed as an increase in the collector or "output" current with increasing collector-emitter voltage.

Regions of Operation (CB)

Active region: The operating range of the amplifier. It is noticed that IE is approximately equal to IC (IE ≈ IC). BJT is a current controlled device in the active region and is independent of the output voltage VCB.

Cut-off region: The region where the collector current IC is approximately equal to 0 (IC < ICBO). The device is basically OFF i.e., there's no input voltage, but only reverse – bias output voltage and negligible reverse saturation current.

Reverse saturation current circuit: Emitter open (I_E = 0), collector-to-base reverse biased. Only I_CBO = I_CO flows (minority carrier leakage current).

Saturation region: The region to the left of VCB = 0V. Note the exponential increase in collector current IC as VCB approaches 0V. In this region, IC depends on VCB but is independent of IE.

Step-by-step derivation
\[ I_C = \alpha I_E + I_{CB0} \] \[ = \alpha (I_C + I_B) + I_{CB0} \qquad (\because\; I_E = I_C + I_B) \] \[ I_C = \frac{\alpha I_B + I_{CB0}}{1-\alpha} \]

Let \(\beta = \dfrac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\)    \(\left[\because\; \dfrac{1}{1-\alpha} = 1+\beta\right]\)

\[ I_C = \beta I_B + (1+\beta)I_{CB0} \] \[ \boxed{I_C = \beta I_B + I_{CE0}} \]

where \(I_{CE0} = (1+\beta)I_{CB0} \approx \beta I_{CB0}\) or \(I_{CE0} = \dfrac{I_{CB0}}{1-\alpha}\bigg|_{I_B=0\,\mu\text{A}}\)

Neglecting leakage current I_CEO
\[ \boxed{I_C = \beta I_B} \] \[ \boxed{50 \leq \beta \leq 500} \]

\[ I_C = \beta I_B \] \[ I_E = I_C + I_B = \beta I_B + I_B \] \[ \boxed{I_E = (\beta + 1)I_B} \]

Relationship Between α and β

A relationship can be developed between α and β using the basic relationships introduced thus far. Using β = IC / IB we have IB = IC / β, and from α = IC / IE we have IE = IC / α. Substituting into IE = IC + IB:

\[ \frac{I_C}{\alpha} = I_C + \frac{I_C}{\beta} \]

and dividing both sides of the equation by IC will result in:

\[ \frac{1}{\alpha} = 1 + \frac{1}{\beta} \] \[ \text{or}\quad \beta = \alpha\beta + \alpha = (\beta + 1)\alpha \] \[ \boxed{\alpha = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta} \qquad \text{or} \qquad \beta = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}} \]

Operating Point (Q-Point)

Biasing refers to application of DC voltages to establish a fixed level of current and voltage in a transistor.

For a transistor amplifier, the resulting DC current and voltage on the characteristic curve establish an Operating Point. This indicates the region of operation of the transistor.

Since the operating point is fixed on the Characteristic curve, it is also called as Quiescent point or Q point.

Biasing circuit can be used to set the operating point within the active region. A, B, C, D are some of the possible operating points.

Operating Point graph: I_C (mA) vs V_CE (V). Multiple β-curves shown. Points A, B, C, D marked at different locations:
• A: At origin/cutoff (I_B = 0) — device OFF
• B: Mid-active region (I_B = 30 μA) — ideal Q-point
• C: Near saturation boundary — limits signal swing
• D: Near V_CE_max — limits positive swing
Safe operating area (SOA) indicated.

Analysis of Operating Points

  • Q Point at A: If there is no bias, the device is completely off, resulting in Q point at A. This point is not suitable as complete input range can not be covered for amplification.
  • Q Point at B: Can cover a large range of input signal. It is also far from both cut-off and saturation region.
  • Q Point at C: This is near to both cutoff and saturation region thus limiting the swing of input signal.
  • Q Point at D: As is near to the maximum voltage and power level, limits the positive swing of input signal.

Effect of Temperature on Operating Point

Temperature also has its effect on the Operating point.

Temperature causes the device parameters like transistor current gain (βac) and the transistor leakage current (ICEO) to change.

As temperature increases, ICEO increases, resulting in changing the Q point. Thus the network design must also consider temperature stability to reduce the changes in Q point.

The variation of operating point due to variation in temperature is indicated by stability factor (S). Higher the stability of the circuit, better is the circuit.

Transistor Biasing

Biasing is the process of providing DC voltage which helps in the functioning of the circuit. A transistor is biased in order to make the base-emitter junction forward biased and base-collector junction reverse biased, so that it maintains in active region, to work as an amplifier.

Need for DC Biasing

If a signal of very small voltage is given to the input of BJT, it cannot be amplified. Because, for a BJT, to amplify a signal, two conditions have to be met:

  • The input voltage should exceed cut-in voltage for the transistor to be ON.
  • The BJT should be in the linear / active region, to be operated as an amplifier.

DC Biasing

  • The operating point is a fixed point on the characteristics and is also called quiescent point, denoted by Q-point. The term biasing means the application of dc voltages used to setup a fixed level of current and voltage. Called "dc biasing" or "biasing analysis".
  • This step determines both the region of operation and the small-signal parameters of each device.

Different Regions of Operation of BJT

Operation in the cutoff, saturation, and linear regions of the BJT characteristic are provided as follows:

  1. Linear-region (or Active region) operation:
    Base–emitter junction forward-biased  |  Base–collector junction reverse-biased
  2. Cutoff-region operation:
    Base–emitter junction reverse-biased  |  Base–collector junction reverse-biased
  3. Saturation-region operation:
    Base–emitter junction forward-biased  |  Base–collector junction forward-biased

Fixed-Bias Configuration

The fixed-bias circuit is the simplest transistor dc bias configuration. Hence, it is also called as simple biasing.

For dc, f = 0 Hz, and XC = 1/(2πfC) = 1/(2π(0)C) = ∞Ω.

Fixed-Bias Circuit (NPN): V_CC at top, R_B from V_CC to base, R_C from V_CC to collector. Coupling capacitors C1 (input) and C2 (output). Base-emitter junction forward biased via R_B, collector-emitter via R_C and V_CC.

Forward Bias of Base–Emitter

Consider first the base–emitter circuit loop. Writing Kirchhoff's voltage equation in the clockwise direction for the loop, we obtain +VCC – IBRB – VBE = 0:

Base Current (Fixed Bias)
\[ I_B = \frac{V_{CC} - V_{BE}}{R_B} \]

The supply voltage VCC and the base–emitter voltage VBE are constants, the selection of a base resistor RB sets the level of base current for the operating point.

Collector–Emitter Loop

The collector–emitter section of the network with the indicated direction of current IC and the resulting polarity across RC: IC = βIB

  • Changing RC to any level will not affect the level of IB or IC as long as the device is in the active region.
  • The level of RC will determine the magnitude of VCE.
Collector-Emitter Voltage
\[ V_{CE} + I_C R_C - V_{CC} = 0 \] \[ \boxed{V_{CE} = V_{CC} - I_C R_C} \]
Voltage relationships
\[ V_{CE} = V_C - V_E \qquad V_{BE} = V_B - V_E \] \text{Since emitter is ground:}\quad V_{CE} = V_C \qquad V_{BE} = V_B

Emitter-Bias Configuration

The DC bias network contains an emitter resistor to improve the stability level over that of the fixed bias configuration.

Emitter-Bias Circuit (NPN): V_CC at top, R_B from V_CC to base, R_C from V_CC to collector, R_E from emitter to ground. Coupling capacitors C1 (input) and C2 (output). BJT bias circuit with emitter resistor.

Base–Emitter Loop

Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the indicated loop in the clockwise direction:

\[ +V_{CC} - I_B R_B - V_{BE} - I_E R_E = 0 \]

Since IE = (β + 1)IB, substitute in the above equation:

\[ V_{CC} - I_B R_B - V_{BE} - (\beta+1)I_B R_E = 0 \] \[ -I_B(R_B + (\beta+1)R_E) + V_{CC} - V_{BE} = 0 \] \[ \boxed{I_B = \frac{V_{CC} - V_{BE}}{R_B + (\beta+1)R_E}} \]
Note

The only difference between this equation for IB and that obtained for the fixed bias configuration is the term (β+1)RE.

Note that aside from the base-to-emitter voltage VBE, the resistor RE is reflected back to the input base circuit by a factor (β+1). In other words, the emitter resistor, which is a part of the collector-emitter loop, "appears as" (β+1)RE in the base-emitter loop. Thus, the reflected impedance level of RE in the base circuit gives the input impedance:

\[ R_i = (\beta+1)R_E \]

Collector–Emitter Loop

Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law for the indicated loop in the clockwise direction:

\[ +I_E R_E + V_{CE} + I_C R_C - V_{CC} = 0 \]

Substituting IE ≅ IC and grouping terms gives:

\[ \boxed{V_{CE} = V_{CC} - I_C(R_C + R_E)} \]
Additional voltage relationships
\[ V_E = I_E R_E \qquad V_{CE} = V_C - V_E \] \[ V_C = V_{CE} + V_E = V_{CC} - I_C R_C \] \[ V_B = V_{CC} - I_B R_B = V_{BE} + V_E \]

Saturation Level — Emitter Bias

The collector saturation level or maximum collector current for an emitter-bias design can be determined using the same approach applied to the fixed-bias configuration. Apply a short circuit between the collector–emitter terminals and calculate the resulting collector current:

Saturation Current
\[ I_{C_{\text{sat}}} = \frac{V_{CC}}{R_C + R_E} \]

Voltage-Divider Bias Configuration

In the previous bias configurations, the bias current ICQ and voltage VCEQ were a function of the current gain β of the transistor.

Since β is temperature sensitive, especially for silicon transistors, and the actual value of beta is usually not well defined it is necessary to develop a bias circuit that is less dependent on, or independent of, the transistor beta.

Thus, Voltage divider bias configuration is preferred.

Voltage Divider Bias Circuit (NPN): V_CC at top. R1 from V_CC to base, R2 from base to ground. R_C from V_CC to collector. R_E from emitter to ground. Coupling capacitors C1 and C2. FIG. 4.28.

Approximate Analysis

Approximate Analysis can be applied only if specific conditions are satisfied.

The input section of the voltage-divider configuration can be represented by the network of Fig. 4.36. The Ri is the resistance between base and ground for the transistor with the emitter resistance, with the reflected resistance between the base and emitter, similar to the Emitter bias configuration and is given by Ri = (β+1)RE.

If Ri is much larger than the resistance R2, the current IB will be much smaller than I2 and I2 will be approximately equal to I1.

Since IB ≅ 0, the voltage drop across R2 is given by \(\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2}V_{CC}\) (using the voltage divider rule, hence the name of the configuration).

Approximation Condition

If βRE ≥ 10R2, then the approximation method can be applied to determine the biasing current and voltage.

VE and IE are calculated by VE = VB − VBE and \(I_E = \dfrac{V_E}{R_E}\)

\[ I_{C_Q} \cong I_E \]

The collector-to-emitter voltage is determined by:

\[ V_{CE} = V_{CC} - I_C R_C - I_E R_E \]

Since IE ≅ IC:

\[ \boxed{V_{CE_Q} = V_{CC} - I_C(R_C + R_E)} \]

In all the biasing voltage and current equation β does not appear and the Q-point (as determined by ICQ and VCEQ) is therefore independent of the value of β.

Transistor Saturation

\[ I_{C_{\text{sat}}} = I_{C_{\max}} = \frac{V_{CC}}{R_C + R_E} \]

Single Stage CE Amplifier

The following shows a single stage CE amplifier. The different circuit elements and their functions are described as follows.

Single stage CE amplifier circuit: R1 and R2 form voltage divider bias, R_E is emitter resistor, R_C is collector load, C_in is input coupling capacitor, C_E is emitter bypass capacitor, C is output coupling capacitor. Weak AC input signal applied through C_in to base. V_CC powers the circuit. V_out taken from collector through C.

(i) Biasing Circuit

The resistances R1, R2 and RE form the biasing and stabilization circuit.

(ii) Input Capacitance Cin

This is used to couple the signal to the base of the transistor. If this is not used, the signal source resistance will come across R2 and thus changes the bias condition of the transistor. The capacitor Cin allows only a.c. signal to flow.

(iii) Emitter Bypass Capacitor CE

This is connected in parallel with RE to provide a low reactance path to the amplified a.c. signal. If it is not used, then amplified a.c. signal flowing through RE will cause a voltage drop across it, thereby shifting the output voltage.

(iv) Coupling Capacitor C

This is used to couple the amplified signal to the output device. This capacitor C allows only a.c. signal to flow.

(v) Working

  • When a weak input a.c. signal is applied to the base of the transistor, a small base current flows.
  • Due to transistor action, a much larger a.c. current flows through collector load RC, a large voltage appears across RC and hence at the output.
  • Therefore, a weak signal applied to the base appears in amplified form at the collector of circuit.
  • Voltage gain (Av) of the amplifier is the ratio of the amplified output voltage to the input voltage.

(vi) Frequency Response and Bandwidth

Frequency Response Curve: Gain (A_v) on Y-axis vs Frequency on X-axis (log scale).
• A_M: midband (maximum) gain level
• A_M / √2 ≈ 0.707 A_M: −3 dB points
• F_L: lower cutoff frequency (left −3 dB point)
• F_H: upper cutoff frequency (right −3 dB point)
• Three regions: Low frequency region (gain rises), Mid frequency region (flat, maximum gain), High frequency region (gain falls)
• BW = F_H − F_L (bandwidth)
Bandwidth
\[ BW = F_H - F_L \]

Op-Amp Parameters

Input Offset Voltage

The input offset voltage is the differential DC voltage that must be applied between the input terminals (inverting and non-inverting) of the op-amp to make the output voltage zero. Ideally its value is 0 V. Its value is 1 mV to 6 mV for 741.

Output Offset Voltage

The output offset voltage of an operational amplifier refers to the small DC voltage that appears at its output even when the input terminals are shorted together and no input signal is applied. This offset is caused by internal mismatches in the op-amp, such as differences in the transistor parameters or imbalances in the internal circuitry. Ideally its value is 0 V.

Input Resistance

The input resistance of an operational amplifier (op-amp) refers to the effective resistance seen at its input terminals. It is generally measured in open-loop condition. Ideally its value is infinity. Its value of 2 MΩ for 741 under open loop condition.

Output Resistance

The output resistance of the operational amplifier is the resistance seen at the output terminal when the op-amp is configured in an open-loop condition. Ideally, its value is 0 ohm. Its value is 75 ohms for 741.

Gain Bandwidth

Gain Bandwidth: The frequency at which the gain drops by 3 dB is known as the cutoff frequency fc of the Op-Amp. The unity-gain frequency f1 and cutoff frequency are related by:

\[ f_1 = A_{VD} f_c \]

Unity-gain frequency may also be called the gain-bandwidth product of the op-amp. Ideally its value is infinity. Its value is 1 MHz for 741.

Slew Rate

Slew rate: The maximum rate at which amplifier output can change in volts per microsecond is known as Slew rate. The slew rate provides a parameter specifying the maximum rate of change of the output voltage when driven by a large step-input signal. Ideal value of slew rate is infinity. It is 0.5 V/μ seconds for 741.

Slew Rate
\[ SR = \frac{\Delta V_o}{\Delta t}\;\text{V}/\mu\text{s} \qquad\text{with }t\text{ in }\mu\text{s} \]

Common Mode Rejection Ratio (CMRR)

Common Mode Rejection Ratio: It is the ratio of differential gain to the common mode gain of the amplifier. Ideally, CMRR is infinity. Typically, its value is 90 dB for 741.

\[ CMRR = \frac{A_d}{A_c} \]
where
\[ A_d = \text{Differential gain of the amplifier} \] \[ A_c = \text{Common mode gain of the amplifier} \]

The value of CMRR can also be expressed in logarithmic terms:

\[ CMRR(log) = 20\log_{10}\left\{\frac{A_d}{A_c}\right\}\;\text{dB} \]

Virtual Ground Concept

  • Compared to all other input and output voltages, the value of Vi is very small and can be considered as 0 V.
  • The fact that Vi ≈ 0 V leads to the concept that at the amplifier input there exists a virtual short-circuit or virtual ground.

Practical Op-Amp Circuits

1. Noninverting Amplifier

Noninverting Amplifier: Input V1 applied to + (non-inverting) terminal. R_f from output to − (inverting) terminal. R1 from inverting terminal to ground. Feedback network R1 and R_f form voltage divider. Output: V_o = (1 + R_f/R_1) × V_1
  • Input is given to noninverting terminal of an Amplifier.
  • Voltage across R1 is V1 (since Vi = 0 V) and is equal to the output voltage, through a voltage divider of R1 and Rf and is given by:
\[ V_1 = \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_f} V_o \] \[ \boxed{\frac{V_o}{V_1} = \frac{R_1+R_f}{R_1} = 1 + \frac{R_f}{R_1}} \]

2. Inverting Amplifier

Inverting Amplifier: Input V1 → R1 → node A (inverting, −) → op-amp → V_o. R_f from output back to node A. Non-inverting (+) terminal B grounded. V_A = V_B = 0 (Virtual ground). Output: V_o = −(R_f / R_1) × V_1

VA = VB = 0 (Virtual ground)

At Input: I = (V1 − VA)/R1 = V1/R1 ——(1)

At Output: I = (VA − VO)/Rf = −(VO/Rf) ——(2)

Equating 1 and 2:

Output Voltage — Inverting Amplifier
\[ \boxed{V_O = -\frac{R_f}{R_1} \cdot V_1} \] \[\text{Gain} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}\]
  • Input is given to inverting terminal of the amplifier.
  • Inverting amplifier is the most widely used constant-gain amplifier circuit.
  • The output is obtained by multiplying the input by a fixed or constant gain, set by the input resistor R1 and feedback resistor Rf and output is being inverted from the input.
  • Output Voltage of Inverting amplifier is VO = −(Rf/R1) × V1 (from virtual ground concept).

Ideal OP-Amp Negative Feedback

Negative Feedback circuit: V_in → (+) input of op-amp A → V_out. Feedback network: R1 and R2 voltage divider from output back to (−) input. V_f = feedback voltage across R2.
\[ \text{Closed loop Gain} = \frac{V_{\text{out}}}{V_{\text{in}}} = 1 + \frac{R_1}{R_2} \] \[ \text{Feedback}\;\beta = \frac{V_f}{V_{\text{out}}} = \frac{R_2}{R_2+R_2} \]

3. Unity Follower (Voltage Follower)

Fig a — Voltage Follower: Output directly connected back to inverting (−) input (100% feedback). Input V1 applied to (+) non-inverting terminal. V_o = V1 (gain = 1, no inversion).
Fig b — Equivalent: V_i ≈ 0 between + and − (virtual short), since both are at V1.
  • The unity-follower circuit provides a gain of unity with no polarity or phase reversal as shown in fig a. Vo = V1.

4. Summing Amplifier

  • If more inputs are used, they each add an additional component to the output.

Output of a Summing Amplifier

Applying KCL at node A:

\[ i_1 + i_2 + i_3 = i_f \qquad\text{---(1)} \] \[ i_1 = \frac{v_1 - v_A}{R_1},\quad i_2 = \frac{v_2-v_A}{R_2},\quad i_3 = \frac{v_3-v_A}{R_3},\quad i_f = \frac{v_A-v_{\text{out}}}{R_f} \qquad\text{----(2)} \]

Substituting eq (2) in (1) and considering VA = 0 (Virtual ground):

\[ \frac{v_1}{R_1} + \frac{v_2}{R_2} + \frac{v_3}{R_3} = \frac{-v_{\text{out}}}{R_f} \] \[ \boxed{v_{\text{out}} = -R_f\left(\frac{v_1}{R_1}+\frac{v_2}{R_2}+\frac{v_3}{R_3}\right)} \]

5. Constant Gain / Fixed Gain Amplifier

a. Inverting Constant Gain Multiplier

Provides a precise gain of:

\[ A = -\frac{R_f}{R_1} \]
Inverting Constant Gain Multiplier: V1 → R1 → (−) input → op-amp → V_o. R_f feedback. R_C from (+) to ground (for bias balance). Gain = −R_f/R_1.

b. Non-Inverting Constant Gain Multiplier

Provides a precise gain of:

\[ A = 1 + \frac{R_f}{R_1} \]
Non-Inverting Constant Gain Multiplier: V1 applied to (+) non-inverting input. R1 from (−) to ground. R_f from output to (−) input. Gain = 1 + R_f/R_1.

6. Voltage Subtraction (Difference Amplifier)

Voltage Subtractor: V1 → R1 → node A (inverting −). V2 → R2 → node B (non-inverting +). R_f from output to node A. R_f from node B to ground. Superposition used for analysis. When R1 = R2 = R_f: V_o = V2 − V1.
  • Superposition principle is used to find the relation between output and input.
  • Let Vo1 be the output, with input V1, assuming V2 to be zero. And Vo2 be the output, with input V2, assuming V1 to be zero.
  • With V2 zero, the circuit acts as an inverting amplifier and the output equation is:
\[ V_{o1} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}V_1 \qquad\text{------(1)} \]

While with V1 as zero, the circuit reduces as shown. Let potential of node B be VB. The potential of node A is same as B i.e. VA = VB (Virtual short). Applying voltage divider rule to the input V2 loop:

\[ V_B = \frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}V_2 \qquad\text{------(2)} \] \[ I = \frac{V_A}{R_1} = \frac{V_B}{R_1} \qquad\text{------(3)} \] \[ I = \frac{V_{o2}-V_A}{R_f} = \frac{V_{o2}-V_B}{R_f} \qquad\text{------(4)} \]

Equating the equations (3) and (4):

\[ \frac{V_B}{R_1} = \frac{V_{o2}-V_B}{R_f} \] \[ V_{o2} = \frac{R_1+R_f}{R_1}V_B = \left[1+\frac{R_f}{R_1}\right]V_B \qquad\text{-----(5)} \]

Substituting VB from (2) in (5) we get:

\[ V_{o2} = \left[1+\frac{R_f}{R_1}\right]\left[\frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}\right]V_2 \]

Hence using Superposition principle:

\[ V_o = V_{o1} + V_{o2} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}V_1 + \left[1+\frac{R_f}{R_1}\right]\left[\frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}\right]V_2 \]

If R1 = R2:

\[ V_o = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}V_1 + \frac{R_f}{R_1}V_2 \] \[ \boxed{V_o = +\frac{R_f}{R_1}(V_2-V_1)} \]
  • The output voltage is proportional to the difference between the two input voltages. Thus it acts as a Subtractor using Op Amp circuit or difference amplifier.

If R1 = R2 = Rf is selected, then:

\[ V_o = V_2 - V_1 \]

1's Complement

Definition

The 1's complement of a binary number is obtained by inverting all the bits — swapping all 0s for 1s and all 1s for 0s.

Example: The 1's complement of 1010 is 0101.

In 1's complement representation, negative numbers are represented by inverting all bits of the corresponding positive number.

Example: Represent −34 in 8-bit 1's Complement Form

+34 =0 0 1 0 0 0 1 0
↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓   (Invert all bits)
−34 =1 1 0 1 1 1 0 1  (1's complement of +34)

2's Complement

Definition

2's complement is the most common method used to represent signed numbers in digital systems.

Steps to Find 2's Complement

  1. Find the 1's complement (invert all bits)
  2. Add 1 to the 1's complement

Example: Represent −34 in 8-bit 2's Complement Form

+34 =0 0 1 0 0 0 1 0
↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓   (1's complement)
1 1 0 1 1 1 0 1  (1's complement of +34)
+              1
−34 =1 1 0 1 1 1 1 0  (2's complement of +34)

Absorption Theorem

(i) A + AB = A
LHS = A + AB
= A·1 + AB     (since A·1 = A)
= A(1 + B)     (since 1 + B = 1)
= A·1 = A = RHS ✓
(ii) A(A+B) = A
LHS = A(A + B)
= A·A + A·B
= A + AB     (since A·A = A)
= A(1 + B) = A·1 = A = RHS ✓
(iii) A + ĀB = A + B
LHS = A + ĀB
= (A + Ā)(A + B)   [Distributive: A+BC=(A+B)(A+C)]
= (1)(A + B)      (since A + Ā = 1)
= A + B = RHS ✓
(iv) A·(Ā+B) = AB
LHS = A·(Ā + B)
= A·Ā + A·B     (A·Ā = 0)
= 0 + AB = AB = RHS ✓

Redundancy Laws

The Redundancy Laws are a special case of the Absorption Theorem and are used to simplify expressions by eliminating redundant terms.

Consensus Theorem

AB + ĀC + BC = AB + ĀC     (BC is the redundant term)

LHS = AB + ĀC + BC
= AB + ĀC + BC·1
= AB + ĀC + BC(A + Ā)        (since A + Ā = 1)
= AB + ĀC + ABC + ĀBC
= AB(1 + C) + ĀC(1 + B)     (since 1+B = 1+C = 1)
= AB + ĀC = RHS ✓
Dual of Consensus Theorem:
(A+B)(Ā+C)(B+C) = (A+B)(Ā+C)
LHS = (A+B)(Ā+C)(B+C)
Using A+BC = (A+B)(A+C), we get B+AC = (B+A)(B+C)
LHS = (B+AC)(Ā+C)
= ĀB + ĀAC + BC + ACC
= ĀB + BC + AC + AĀ
= B(Ā+C) + A(Ā+C)
= (A+B)(Ā+C) = RHS ✓

Introduction: Sequential Circuits

Sequential Circuits
  • We want circuits that produce output depending on current and past input values — circuits with memory.
  • A sequential circuit contains a Combinational Circuit and a Storage Element with feedback.

Types of Sequential Circuits

1. Synchronous Circuits
In synchronous sequential circuits, the state of the device changes only at discrete times in response to a clock pulse.
2. Asynchronous Circuits
Asynchronous circuit is not synchronized by a clock signal; the outputs of the circuit change directly in response to the change in the inputs.

Flip-Flops — Introduction

Flip-flop circuits are constructed in such a way as to make them operate properly when they are part of a sequential circuit that employs a common clock.

The problem with the latch is that it responds to a change in the level of a clock pulse. As shown in Fig. (a), a positive level response in the enable input allows changes in the output when the D input changes while the clock pulse stays at logic 1.

The key to the proper operation of a flip-flop is to trigger it only during a signal transition.

Latch (Level Sensitive): Will change the output when the clock remains at logic 1 — Fig. (a) Positive level response.
Flip-Flop (Edge Triggered): Will change the output when the clock changes from logic 0 to logic 1 — Fig. (b) Positive-edge response.

Fig. (c) shows Negative-edge response — flip-flop triggers on falling edge (1→0 transition).

Edge Triggered D Flip-Flop

Edge-Triggered D Flip-Flop

Construction uses three SR latches. Two latches respond to the external D (data) and Clk (clock) inputs. The third latch provides the outputs for the flip-flop.

Operation

  • The S and R inputs of the output latch are maintained at logic-1 level when Clk = 0. This causes the output to remain in its present state.
  • If D = 0 when Clk becomes 1, R changes to 0. This causes the flip-flop to go to the reset state, making Q = 0. If there is a change in the D input while Clk = 1, terminal R remains at 0 because Q is 0. Thus, the flip-flop is locked out and is unresponsive to further changes in the input.
  • Similarly, if D = 1 when Clk goes from 0 to 1, S changes to 0. This causes the circuit to go to the set state, making Q = 1. Any change in D while Clk = 1 does not affect the output.

Positive-Edge vs. Negative-Edge Triggered D Flip-Flop

Positive-Edge Triggered: Absence of bubble in front of Clk input. Output changes on 0→1 clock transition.
Negative-Edge Triggered: Presence of bubble in front of Clk input. Output changes on 1→0 clock transition.

Registers

Register
  • A register is a group of flip-flops, each one of which shares a common clock and is capable of storing one bit of information.
  • An n-bit register consists of a group of n flip-flops capable of storing n bits of binary information.
  • In addition to flip-flops, a register may have combinational gates that perform certain data-processing tasks.
  • The flip-flops hold the binary information, and the gates determine how the information is transferred into the register.

4-bit Register

The simplest register consists of only flip-flops, without any gates. A 4-bit register is constructed with four D-type flip-flops.

  • The common clock input triggers all flip-flops on the positive edge of each pulse, and the binary data available at the four inputs are transferred into the register.
  • The four outputs can be sampled at any time to obtain the binary information stored in the register.
  • The input Clear goes to the active-low R (reset) input of all four flip-flops. When this input goes to 0, all flip-flops are reset asynchronously.
  • The R inputs must be maintained at logic 1 (de-asserted) during normal clocked operation.
  • The transfer of new information into a register is referred to as loading or updating the register.
  • If all the bits of the register are loaded simultaneously with a common clock pulse, we say that the loading is done in parallel.
  • A clock edge applied to the C inputs will load all four inputs in parallel.
  • If the contents of the register must be left unchanged, the inputs must be held constant or the clock must be inhibited from the circuit.

Shift Registers (SISO)

Shift Register

A register capable of shifting the binary information held in each cell to its neighboring cell, in a selected direction, is called a shift register.

  • The logical configuration consists of a chain of flip-flops in cascade, with the output of one flip-flop connected to the input of the next.
  • All flip-flops receive common clock pulses which activate the shift of data from one stage to the next.

Serial In Serial Out (SISO)

The simplest possible shift register (SISO) uses only flip-flops. The output of a given flip-flop is connected to the D input of the flip-flop at its right. This shift register is unidirectional (left-to-right). Each clock pulse shifts the contents of the register one bit position to the right.

4-bit SISO structure:
SI → [D₀/FA] → [D₁/FB] → [D₂/FC] → [D₃/FD] → SO
Common CLK connected to all C inputs.

Each flip-flop's output feeds the next flip-flop's D input. After each clock pulse, data moves one position right. The last flip-flop (D₃) provides the serial output (SO).

Synchronous and Asynchronous Counters

Counter
  • A register (group of flip-flops) that goes through a prescribed sequence of states upon the application of input pulses is called a counter.
  • The input pulses may be clock pulses, or they may originate from some external source and may occur at a fixed interval of time or at random.
  • The sequence of states may follow the binary number sequence or any other sequence of states.
  • A counter that follows the binary number sequence is called a binary counter.
  • An n-bit binary counter consists of n flip-flops and can count in binary from 0 through \(2^n - 1\).
Ripple (Asynchronous) Counters:
A flip-flop output transition serves as a source for triggering other flip-flops. The C input of some or all flip-flops are triggered not by the common clock pulses, but rather by the transition that occurs in other flip-flop outputs.
Synchronous Counters:
The C inputs of all flip-flops receive the common clock. All flip-flops change state simultaneously at the clock edge.
