Molecular Spectroscopy

Electromagnetic radiation, rotational, vibrational, and electronic spectra of molecules.

Introduction to Molecular Spectroscopy

Molecular spectroscopy is that branch of science which deals with the study of interaction of electromagnetic radiation with matter (molecules).

The basic experimental setup involves: Incident radiation → sample → detector → diffraction grating → recorder (spectrum)

Properties of Electromagnetic Radiation

Some important features of electromagnetic radiations are:

  • They have dual character — particle character as well as wave character. For example, a beam of light is a stream of particles called photons moving through space in the form of waves.
  • These waves are associated with electric and magnetic fields oscillating perpendicular to each other and also perpendicular to the direction of propagation.
  • All electromagnetic radiations travel with the velocity of light.
[Electromagnetic Wave Diagram] An EM wave propagates along a direction with the Electric Field (E) oscillating in one plane and the Magnetic Field (B) oscillating perpendicular to it. The wavelength λ is the distance between successive crests.

Wave Characteristics

Waves can be characterised by the following properties:

  • Wavelength: The distance between two successive crests and troughs of the wave; symbol λ; units: m, nm, or Å.
  • Frequency: The number of cycles completed in a certain amount of time; symbol ν; units: Hz or s⁻¹.
  • Wave number: Number of complete waves or cycles contained in unit distance; symbol ν̄; units: cm⁻¹.
Key Formulas

Energy of electromagnetic radiation: \( E = h\nu \)

where E is energy, h is Planck's constant (h = 6.625 × 10⁻³⁴ Js), and ν is frequency.

Wavelength–Frequency relation: \( \nu = \frac{c}{\lambda} \)

where c is the speed of light, λ is wavelength, and ν is frequency.

Wave number–Wavelength relation: \( \bar{\nu} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \)

Comparison: Atomic Spectra vs Molecular Spectra

Atomic Spectra

  • Due to electronic transitions from one atomic orbital to another.
  • Gives rise to line spectra.
[Atomic Energy Level Diagram] Electrons in a hydrogen atom can occupy shells n = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... The Hydrogen Emission Spectrum shows discrete lines at 410 nm, 434 nm, 486 nm, and 656 nm (Balmer series).

Molecular Spectra

  • Due to electronic transitions from the Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital (HOMO) to the Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbital (LUMO).
  • Gives rise to band spectra due to rotational and vibrational transitions possible in molecules along with electronic transitions.

Quantisation of Energy

  • Energy levels in atoms or molecules are discrete or quantised.
  • Energy can be absorbed only in packets called quanta.
  • Frequency of light absorbed when there is a transition between two energy levels depends on the difference in energy between the two energy levels.
  • If an atom or molecule in the ground state absorbs energy there exists a higher energy level corresponding to the frequency of light absorbed.
\[ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = h\nu \]

where E₂ is the upper energy level and E₁ is the lower energy level.

Absorption and Emission Spectrum

  • Absorption spectrum is observed when an atom or molecule absorbs energy and moves from a lower energy level to a higher energy level.
  • Emission spectrum arises when a molecule comes from a higher energy level to a lower energy level.
\[ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = h\nu \]
[Hydrogen Emission and Absorption Spectra] The emission spectrum shows bright lines on a dark background. The absorption spectrum shows dark lines on a bright background. Both appear at the same wavelengths (410 nm, 434 nm, 486 nm, 656 nm) representing Balmer series transitions between energy levels n = 2 (ground state) and n = 6, 5, 4, 3 (excited states).

Spectroscopic Units

\( E = h\nu \) where Energy is expressed in Joules.

Wave number is related to wavelength by: \( \bar{\nu} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \)

Since \( c = \lambda\nu \),

\[ \bar{\nu} = \frac{\nu}{c} \]

Therefore:

\[ E = hc\bar{\nu} \quad \text{or} \quad \bar{\nu} = \frac{E}{hc} \]
  • The spectroscopic unit for energy of a radiation is cm⁻¹.
  • It is energy expressed in wave numbers.
  • It is used for convenience of using small numerals.
  • Example: 1 cm⁻¹ = 1.99 × 10⁻²³ J

Raman Spectroscopy: Scattering of Light

When monochromatic radiation is passed through a transparent medium:

  • Most of the scattered radiation consists of radiation of incident wavelength — Rayleigh scattering.
  • Some of the scattered radiation consists of radiation with different wavelength from incident wavelength — Raman scattering.
  • When the wavelength of scattered radiation is more than that of incident radiation — Stokes lines.
  • When the wavelength of scattered radiation is less than that of incident radiation — anti-Stokes lines.
[Raman Scattering Diagram] A laser (λ_laser) hits the molecule. Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering exits with λ_scatter < λ_laser. Rayleigh Scattering exits with λ_scatter = λ_laser. Stokes Raman Scattering exits with λ_scatter > λ_laser.
[Energy Transition Diagram for Rayleigh and Raman Scattering] Energy levels: Ground state E₀ and excited state E₀ + hν_m. Rayleigh Scattering (elastic): molecule goes to virtual state with hν₀ and returns emitting hν₀. Stokes Scattering (inelastic): molecule goes to virtual state with hν₀ and returns to higher level emitting hν₀ − hν_m. Anti-Stokes Scattering: molecule starts from higher level, goes to virtual state and returns to ground state emitting hν₀ + hν_m.
Key Principle

There is an exchange of energy between the molecules and radiation in Raman scattering.

Homonuclear diatomic molecules which are microwave and Infra-red inactive are Raman active.

Different Types of Energies Possessed by a Molecule

  • Translational energy (E_trans): by virtue of translatory motion of the molecule.
  • Rotational energy (E_rot): by virtue of rotation of a molecule about its centre of gravity.
  • Vibrational energy (E_vib): by virtue of periodic displacement of the atoms of a molecule about its equilibrium position.
  • Electronic energy (E_elec): due to the different electronic arrangements in the molecule.

Born-Oppenheimer Approximation

  • Total energy of a molecule is the sum of translational, rotational, vibrational and electronic energies:
\[ E = E_{trans} + E_{rot} + E_{vib} + E_{elec} \]
  • Translational energy is negligibly small. Hence the Born-Oppenheimer approximation can be written as:
\[ E = E_{rot} + E_{vib} + E_{elec} \]
  • Electronic, vibrational and rotational energies of a molecule are completely independent of each other.
  • Because the electronic energy levels are much farther apart than the vibrational energy levels which are again much farther apart than the rotational energy levels.
  • This can be written as:
\[ E_{rot} \ll E_{vib} \ll E_{elec} \]

The ground state and excited electronic state are shown as two Morse potential wells. Within each electronic state, multiple vibrational levels are shown as horizontal lines. Within each vibrational level, closely spaced rotational sub-levels exist. Transitions: electronic (optical/UV region), vibrational (infrared), rotational (microwave).

Rotational Spectroscopy

  • A molecule undergoing rotation absorbs in the microwave region.
  • For a molecule to be rotationally active or microwave active, the molecule has to possess a permanent dipole moment.
  • Rotations of a molecule having permanent dipole moment will cause changes in electric dipoles that will interact with the electrical component of the electromagnetic radiation.
[H–Cl Rotation Diagram] The H–Cl molecule rotates end-over-end. As it rotates, the direction of the dipole moment changes periodically. The vertical component of the dipole oscillates as a sinusoidal wave with time (shown in the bottom panel), with the molecule shown at 5 orientations during one full rotation cycle. This oscillating dipole interacts with the EM radiation.
  • When the frequencies match, resonance occurs and the molecule absorbs energy and goes to the higher rotational levels and a rotational spectrum can be recorded.
  • Molecules such as HCl and CO show rotational spectra as they possess permanent dipole moment while H₂, Cl₂ and CO₂ do not.

Expressions for Rotational Energy Levels (Rigid Rotor Model)

Derivation of Moment of Inertia for a Heteronuclear Diatomic Molecule

[Rigid Rotor Diagram] A rigid diatomic molecule with masses m₁ (left) and m₂ (right) joined by a thin rod of length r₀ = r₁ + r₂. The centre of mass C is located at distance r₁ from m₁ and r₂ from m₂.

The molecule rotates end-over-end about point C (the centre of gravity). This is defined by the moment, or balancing, equation:

\[ m_1 r_1 = m_2 r_2 \]

The moment of inertia about C is defined by:

\[ I = m_1 r_1^2 + m_2 r_2^2 \]

Expanding:

\[ I = m_2 r_2 r_1 + m_1 r_1 r_2 = r_1 r_2 (m_1 + m_2) \]

Since \( m_1 r_1 = m_2 r_2 = m_2(r_o - r_1) \), we get: \( r_1 = \dfrac{m_2 r_o}{m_1 + m_2} \)

Since \( m_1 r_1 = m_2 r_2 = m_1(r_o - r_2) \), we get: \( r_2 = \dfrac{m_1 r_o}{m_1 + m_2} \)

Therefore:

\[ I = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} r_o^2 = \mu r_o^2 \]

Where μ is the reduced mass given by:

\[ \mu = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} \]

Rotational Energy and Quantum Numbers

Rotational energy is given by:

\[ E_r = \frac{1}{2} I\omega^2 = \frac{L^2}{2I} \quad \text{(since } L = I\omega \text{)} \]

Solving the Schrödinger equation for a rigid rotor shows that angular momentum is quantised and is given by:

\[ L = \frac{\sqrt{J(J+1)}}{2\pi} h \]

where J is the rotational quantum number.

The quantity J, the rotational quantum number, can take integral values from zero upwards: J = 0, 1, 2, 3, ...

Hence the rotational energy levels are quantised and given by the expression:

\[ E_J = \frac{h^2}{8\pi^2 I} J(J+1) \text{ Joules} \]

h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ Js and I is the moment of inertia.

Energy in Spectroscopic Units (cm⁻¹)

\[ \varepsilon_J = \frac{h}{8\pi^2 Ic} J(J+1) \text{ cm}^{-1} \]

Which can be written as:

\[ \varepsilon_J = BJ(J+1) \text{ cm}^{-1} \]

where:

\[ B = \frac{h}{8\pi^2 Ic} \text{ cm}^{-1} \]

B is known as the rotational constant.

Information from the Rotational Spectrum

  • The first line in the spectrum appears at 2B cm⁻¹ and the distance between any two consecutive lines is constant and is equal to 2B cm⁻¹.
  • We can get the value of 'B' from the spectrum and calculate I, the moment of inertia, using the expression: \( B = \dfrac{h}{8\pi^2 Ic} \text{ cm}^{-1} \).
  • Since I = μr₀², r₀ can be determined: \( r_o = \sqrt{\dfrac{I}{\mu}} \) — r₀ is the bond length of the molecule.
  • The spectrum also reveals that some higher rotational levels are also populated at room temperature.

Vibrational Spectroscopy

  • When a molecule undergoes vibrational changes it absorbs IR radiation.
  • For a molecule to be vibrationally active (IR active) the vibration of a molecule must be associated with change in dipole moment.
  • Vibrations of molecules can result in changes in electric dipoles that can interact with the electrical component of the electromagnetic radiation.
  • When the frequencies match, resonance occurs and the molecule absorbs energy and goes to the higher vibrational levels and exhibits vibrational spectrum.
  • Molecules such as HCl, CO and H₂O will show vibrational spectra while H₂, Cl₂ will not.
  • CO₂ molecule does not possess permanent dipole moment but is IR active as when it vibrates asymmetrically or in bending mode, there is change in dipole moment.

Vibrational Modes of CO₂

CO₂ molecule although does not possess permanent dipole moment is IR active because some of its vibrational modes are IR active:

  • CO₂ symmetric stretch is IR inactive as there is no change in dipole moment when the molecule vibrates in this mode.
  • CO₂ asymmetric stretch is IR active.
  • CO₂ bending mode is IR active.
[CO₂ Vibrational Modes] Three vibrational modes are illustrated: (1) Symmetrical stretching — both O atoms move symmetrically away from central C; no change in dipole moment, therefore IR inactive. (2) Asymmetrical stretching — one O moves outward while the other moves inward relative to C; change in dipole moment, therefore IR active. (3) In-plane bending — both O atoms move in the same direction perpendicular to the bond axis; change in dipole moment, therefore IR active.

Vibrational Spectroscopy of Diatomic Molecule: Simple Harmonic Oscillator (SHO) Model

  • Every type of bond in a molecule has a unique natural vibrational frequency.
  • Therefore the IR spectrum of every molecule is unique — as much as the fingerprint of human beings.
  • Bonds are not static but vibrating in different ways.
  • A vibrating bond can therefore be considered a spring with its ends tethered to two atoms — it obeys Hooke's law.
[Spring Model of a Diatomic Bond] Three diagrams: (1) Equilibrium — atoms at rest, spring at natural length (equilibrium bond length). (2) Stretched — atoms pulled apart, spring elongated, spring restoring force pulls inward. (3) Compressed — atoms pushed together, spring compressed, spring restoring force pushes outward.

The vibrating bond obeys Hooke's law: \( F = -k(r - r_{eq}) \)

where k is a force constant, r is the internuclear distance, r_eq is the equilibrium internuclear distance or bond length.

Energy is given by:

\[ E = \frac{1}{2} k(r - r_{eq})^2 \]

Hence the energy curve is parabolic.

[Parabolic Potential Energy Curve] A U-shaped (parabolic) curve of energy vs. internuclear distance, centred at r_eq (the equilibrium position). Two energy levels ε₁ and ε₂ are shown as horizontal lines within the parabolic well. At each energy level, the molecule oscillates between turning points B' and B''.

Oscillation Frequency

The oscillation frequency is given by:

\[ \nu_{osc} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{k}{\mu}} \text{ Hz} \]

where μ is the reduced mass.

Expressing frequency of oscillation in terms of wave number:

\[ \bar{\nu}_{osc} = \frac{1}{2\pi c} \sqrt{\frac{k}{\mu}} \text{ cm}^{-1} \]

Solving the Schrödinger equation for harmonic oscillator shows vibrational energy levels are quantised and are given by the expression:

\[ E_V = \left(v + \frac{1}{2}\right) h\nu_{osc} \text{ Joules} \]

where v = vibrational quantum number, which can take up values zero upwards: v = 0, 1, 2, 3, ...

Vibrational energy expressed in terms of wave number is given by:

\[ \varepsilon_v = \left(v + \frac{1}{2}\right) \bar{\nu}_{osc} \text{ cm}^{-1} \]

Vibrational Spectroscopy — Anharmonic Oscillator Model

  • In actual diatomics, the potential is anharmonic because the bond when stretched beyond a point breaks and the molecule dissociates.
  • A good description of an anharmonic oscillator is given by the Morse function:
\[ \text{P.E.} = D_{eq} \left[1 - \exp\{a(r_o - r)\}\right]^2 \]

where D_eq is the depth of the PE curve and r₀ is the bond length.

[Morse Potential vs. Harmonic Approximation] A plot of potential energy in units of D_e vs. bond length in units of a(r-r_e). The green curve (Morse potential) is U-shaped but asymmetric: steep on the compressed side and gradually levelling off (reaching the dissociation limit D_e = 1.0) on the stretched side. The red curve (harmonic approximation) is a perfect parabola that diverges at large distances, unlike the Morse curve. The minimum of both curves is at the equilibrium bond length r_e.

Energy Levels of an Anharmonic Oscillator

Solving the Schrödinger equation for anharmonic oscillator, the vibrational energy levels are given by the expression:

\[ \varepsilon_v = \frac{E_V}{hc} = \left(v + \frac{1}{2}\right)\bar{\nu}_e - \left(v + \frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \bar{\nu}_e x_e \text{ cm}^{-1} \]
  • \( x_e \) is called the anharmonicity constant whose value for bond stretching vibrations is always small and positive.
  • \( \bar{\nu}_e \) is the oscillation frequency expressed in wavenumbers.
  • v is the vibrational quantum number which can take up values 0, 1, 2, 3, …
  • Substituting values for v we get the energy levels for an anharmonic oscillator.

The spacing between energy levels decreases as v, the vibrational quantum number, increases.

[Anharmonic Oscillator Energy Level Diagram] A Morse potential curve showing energy levels v=0,1,2,3,4,5,6,7 becoming progressively more closely spaced as v increases. Above the dissociation energy D₀ lies the continuum. D_e is the depth of the potential well from the bottom. The distance between turning points A and B at a given energy level represents the vibrational amplitude.

Selection Rules for Anharmonic Oscillator

  • Gross selection rule: When a molecule vibrates there should be change in dipole moment.
  • \( \Delta v = \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \ldots \)
  • Most of the transitions start from v = 0 level because this state is most populated.

Hot Bands

At higher temperatures additional lines are visible in the spectrum as higher vibrational levels may also get populated. These lines originating from the higher vibrational levels are called hot bands.

For example, energy absorbed for the transition from v = 1 to v = 2 is given by:

\[ \Delta\varepsilon_{2\leftarrow 1} = \bar{\nu}_e(1 - 4x_e) \text{ cm}^{-1} \]

The expression shows that this line will appear to the left of the fundamental absorption in the spectrum.

Electronic Spectroscopy

Electronic Transitions

  • Highest Occupied Molecular Orbitals (HOMO) → Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbitals (LUMO).
  • Transitions in UV-Visible region.
  • Transition types: σ → σ*, π → π*, n → π*.
  • If transitions are in the visible region, colour can be seen.
  • Electron redistribution causes change in electric field.
  • All molecules can undergo electronic transitions.
[Electronic Transition Energy Diagram] Molecular orbital energy levels from bottom to top: σ(bonding), π(bonding), n(non-bonding), π*(anti-bonding), σ*(anti-bonding). Arrows indicate the possible transitions: n→π* (lowest energy, UV), π→π* (UV), n→σ* (UV), σ→σ* (highest energy, vacuum UV). Each transition requires absorption of a photon of the corresponding energy.

Born-Oppenheimer Approximation in Electronic Spectroscopy

According to Born-Oppenheimer approximation, the total energy of a molecule is:

\[ E_{Total} = E_t + E_r + E_v + E_e \]

which implies that electronic, vibrational and rotational energies are completely independent of each other.

The change in total energy of the molecule is given by:

\[ \Delta E_{Total} = \Delta E_{Rot} + \Delta E_{Vib} + \Delta E_{Electronic} \quad \text{in Joules} \]
\[ \Delta\varepsilon_{Total} = \Delta\varepsilon_{Rot} + \Delta\varepsilon_{Vib} + \Delta\varepsilon_{Electronic} \quad \text{in cm}^{-1} \]

The approximate orders of magnitude are:

\[ \Delta\varepsilon_{Rot} \times 10^6 \approx \Delta\varepsilon_{Vib} \times 10^3 \approx \Delta\varepsilon_{Electronic} \]
  • Electronic transitions involve electronic redistribution, hence electrostatic forces experienced by the molecule which vibrates in response and this in turn affects rotation of the molecule.
  • Electronic transitions are accompanied by vibrational and rotational changes. Hence vibrational changes will produce "coarse structure" and rotational changes will produce "fine structure" in the electronic spectra of molecules.
  • Information about vibrational and rotational structure of homonuclear molecules like H₂, N₂ (which are inactive in IR and Microwave region) can be obtained from their electronic spectra.

Vibrational Coarse Structure

  • The ground state energy levels are denoted by ε″ and v″, while the excited state energy levels are denoted by ε′ and v′.
  • There are no selection rules for vibrational transitions during electronic transition.
  • Most of the transitions start from v″ = 0 as it is the most populated level.

  • The lines in the resulting spectrum are denoted by (v′, v″).
  • The resulting spectrum is called progression.
  • The lines converge at higher energy levels showing anharmonicity in the excited electronic state.

Intensity of Spectral Lines

  • There are no selection rules for vibrational changes during an electronic transition.
  • Most of the transitions start from v″=0 as it is most populated level.
  • The excited state usually has longer internuclear distance because of antibonding character.
[Potential Energy Curves for Ground and Excited Electronic States] Two Morse potential curves: lower curve (ε″, ground state) has minimum at smaller r; upper curve (ε′, excited state) is shifted to larger r due to antibonding character. Vertical arrows (Franck-Condon transitions) go from v″=0 level upward to different vibrational levels in the excited state. The arrow reaching the classical turning point of the upper potential has the highest probability and hence highest intensity.

The transitions arise from the centre of v″=0 because the maximum probability of finding the nuclei is at the centre of v″=0 (according to quantum theory for the ground vibrational state).

[Probability Distribution for a Diatomic Molecule] Wavefunction probability |ψ|² plots for v=0,1,2,3,4 are shown inside the Morse potential well. For v=0, the maximum probability is at the centre (equilibrium position). For higher v values, the maximum probability shifts to the turning points (classical behaviour). This explains why Franck-Condon transitions originate from the centre of v″=0.

Franck-Condon Principle

  • All lines in the spectra are not of the same intensity though no selection rule for vibrational transitions exist.
Statement of Franck-Condon Principle

"An electronic transition takes place so rapidly that a vibrating molecule does not change its internuclear distance appreciably during the transition."

  • This implies that the electronic transitions are always vertical transitions.

r_e″ and r_e′ are the internuclear distances of the ground and excited states respectively.

Three Possibilities of Vibrational-Electronic Transitions

Case 1: r_e″ < r_e′ (Excited state has larger internuclear distance)

The spectrum shows maximum intensity for (v′, 0) line where v′ is the vibrational level in the excited state to which the electronic transition takes place.

[Franck-Condon — Case 1: r_e″ < r_e′] The excited state potential curve is shifted to the right (larger r). A vertical transition from v″=0 reaches an inner turning point of one of the higher vibrational levels (v′=2 or 3) in the excited state. The spectrum shows maximum intensity for the line corresponding to that v′ level, with intensity falling on both sides.

Case 2: r_e″ = r_e′ (No change in internuclear distance)

The spectrum shows maximum intensity for (0,0) line and intensity falls for the remaining lines.

[Franck-Condon — Case 2: r_e″ = r_e′] Both potential curves are aligned (same equilibrium internuclear distance). A vertical transition from v″=0 goes directly to v′=0. The (0,0) line has maximum intensity; intensity drops off rapidly for (1,0), (2,0), (3,0) lines.

Case 3: r_e″ << r_e′ (Excited state has very much larger internuclear distance)

The spectrum shows continuum since the transition results in the molecule going beyond dissociation energy. The molecule dissociates into atoms which gain kinetic energy. Since this energy is not quantised, a continuum is seen in the spectrum.

[Franck-Condon — Case 3: r_e″ << r_e′] The excited state potential curve is displaced far to the right. A vertical Franck-Condon transition from v″=0 goes above the dissociation limit of the excited state. The spectrum shows discrete lines followed by a continuum region at higher energies, as the molecule dissociates.

Phase Equilibria

Gibbs phase rule, phase diagrams, eutectic systems, and Pattinson's process.

Free Energy in Phase Equilibria

  • All substances have tendency to minimize their Gibbs energy at constant temperature and pressure to attain a stable state.
  • Phase transformations from one phase to another occur to reduce the free energy of the system.
  • Gibbs energy is an extensive property.

Chemical Potential

Chemical potential is defined as the partial molar Gibbs energy for a component i in a mixture, and is denoted by μ:

\[ \mu_i = \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial n_i}\right)_{p,T,n_{j\neq i}} \]

Phase Equilibria

  • Phase equilibria between phases exist when chemical potential of a component is equal in all the phases in equilibrium.

For example, for water at the triple point:

Solid ⇌ liquid ⇌ vapour

The chemical potential of water will be equal in all the three phases

  • For systems not at equilibrium, the chemical potential will point to the direction in which the system can move in order to achieve equilibrium — the system moves from higher chemical potential to lower chemical potential.
  • When various phases are in equilibrium with one another in a heterogeneous system, there can be no transfer of energy or mass from one phase to another.
  • For a system at equilibrium, the various phases must have the same temperature and pressure and their respective compositions must remain constant all along.
Equilibrium Condition (Chemical Potential)

Mathematically: \( \mu_i^\alpha = \mu_i^\beta = \mu_i^\gamma = \ldots \)

Phase

Definition

A phase is defined as any homogeneous and physically distinct part of a system bounded by a surface and is mechanically separable from other parts of the system. It is denoted by P.

  • Gaseous state: P = 1 (gases are completely miscible).
  • Liquid state: P = number of layers when liquids are immiscible; P = 1 when liquids are completely miscible.
  • Solid state: Each solid constitutes a separate phase; each polymorphic form constitutes a separate phase; P = 1 for solid solution.

Counting the Number of Phases — Examples

1) Solid ⇌ liquid ⇌ vapour ; P = 3

  • Ice in the system is a single phase even if it is present as a number of pieces.

2) Calcium carbonate thermal decomposition:

CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

  • P = 3: 2 solid phases (CaCO₃ and CaO) and one gaseous phase (CO₂).

3) Ammonium chloride thermal decomposition:

NH₄Cl(s) ⇌ NH₃(g) + HCl(g)

  • P = 2: one solid (NH₄Cl) and one gaseous (a mixture of NH₃ and HCl).

Components

Definition

A component is defined as the smallest number of independently varying chemical constituents using which the composition of each and every phase in the system can be expressed.

  • When no reaction is taking place in a system, the number of components is the same as the number of constituents.
  • While expressing in terms of constituents, zero and negative values are allowed.

Counting the Number of Components

1-Component System

Pure water; C = 1

solid water ⇌ liquid water

All the different phases can be expressed in terms of the single constituent water.

2-Component System — Salt Hydrate

Na₂SO₄·10H₂O; C = 2

Na₂SO₄·10H₂O= Na₂SO₄ + 10H₂O
Na₂SO₄·7H₂O= Na₂SO₄ + 7H₂O
Na₂SO₄= Na₂SO₄ + 0H₂O

The composition of all the phases can be expressed in terms of 2 components.

Thermal Decomposition of CaCO₃ in a Closed Container; C = 2

CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

  • Though there are 3 species present, the number of components is only two.
  • Phases are: CaCO₃(s), CaO(s) and CO₂(g).
  • Any two of the three constituents may be chosen as the components.

If CaO and CO₂ are chosen:

  • CaCO₃(s) = CaO + CO₂
  • CaO(s) = CaO + 0 CO₂
  • CO₂(g) = 0 CaO + CO₂

If CaCO₃ and CO₂ are chosen:

  • CaCO₃(s) = CaCO₃ + 0 CO₂
  • CaO(s) = CaCO₃ − CO₂
  • CO₂(g) = 0 CaCO₃ + CO₂

Thermal Decomposition of Ammonium Chloride in a Closed System; C = 1

NH₄Cl(s) ⇌ NH₃(g) + HCl(g)

  • Phases are: solid phase of NH₄Cl(s) and gaseous phase of NH₃(g) and HCl(g).
  • solid: NH₄Cl(s) = NH₄Cl
  • gas: NH₃(g) + HCl(g) = NH₄Cl
  • The composition of both the solid and gaseous phases can be expressed in terms of NH₄Cl. Hence C = 1.
  • If additional HCl (or NH₃) were added to the system, then C = 2 because the decomposition of NH₄Cl would not give the correct composition of the gas phase. A second component, HCl (or NH₃), would be needed to describe the gas phase.

Degrees of Freedom (or Variance)

Definition

The degrees of freedom or variance of a system is defined as the minimum number of intensive variables such as temperature, pressure, concentration, which must be fixed in order to define the system completely; it is denoted by F.

OR: The degree of freedom of a system may also be defined as the number of variables, such as temperature, pressure and concentration that can be varied independently without altering the number of phases.

Example: Water System

Only 1 phase (solid, liquid or gas):

  • Both temperature and pressure need to be mentioned in order to define the system; F = 2.

2 phases in equilibrium:

  • Only one variable, either temperature or pressure need to be specified in order to define the system; F = 1.
  • solid water ⇌ liquid water: If the pressure on the system is maintained at 1 atm, then the temperature gets automatically fixed at 0°C (the normal melting point of ice).

3 phases in equilibrium:

  • No variable can be changed; temperature and pressure are fixed; F = 0.
  • solid water ⇌ liquid water ⇌ water vapour: Three phases (ice, water, vapour) can coexist in equilibrium at the triple point of water at 0.0098°C and 4.58 mm Hg pressure only.

Gibbs Phase Rule

  • It was given by Williams Gibbs in 1874.
Statement of Gibbs Phase Rule

Provided the equilibrium in a heterogeneous system is not influenced by external forces (gravity, electrical or magnetic forces), the number of degrees of freedom (F) of the system is related to number of components (C) and number of phases (P) existing at equilibrium to one another by the equation:

\[ F = C - P + 2 \]

Derivation of the Phase Rule

A system at equilibrium satisfies the following conditions:

  • Thermal equilibrium — Temperature is constant.
  • Mechanical equilibrium — Pressure is constant.
  • Chemical or material equilibrium — Chemical potential of a substance is same in all the phases.

Mathematically: \( \mu_i^\alpha = \mu_i^\beta = \mu_i^\gamma = \ldots \)

The system considered is: all C components distributed between P phases.

Total Number of Intensive Variables

  • Temperature: 1
  • Pressure: 1
  • Composition: mole fraction of each component in every phase

For each phase, the sum of mole fractions equals unity:

\[ \chi_1^\alpha + \chi_2^\alpha + \chi_3^\alpha + \ldots + \chi_c^\alpha = 1 \quad (C-1 \text{ terms}) \]

…and similarly for phases β, γ, …, P.

  • In each phase, (C-1) mole fraction terms need to be defined.
  • Number of phases: P → Number of composition variables = P(C-1).
  • Total number of intensive variables = P(C-1) + 2.

Total Number of Equations (Constraints)

At equilibrium the chemical potential of a particular component is same in every phase:

\[ \mu_1^\alpha = \mu_1^\beta = \mu_1^\gamma = \ldots \quad (P-1 \text{ equations}) \]

…and similarly for components 2, 3, …, C.

  • For C components: C(P-1) total constraints.

Derivation Result

\[ F = \text{Total variables} - \text{Total equations} \] \[ F = P(C-1) + 2 - C(P-1) \] \[ \boxed{F = C - P + 2} \]

This is Gibbs Phase Rule.

The Process

Pattinson's process is the technique of heating argentiferous lead containing a very small quantity of silver (~0.1%) and cooling it to get pure lead and a liquid melt richer in silver.

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Argentiferous lead is heated to a temperature above the melting point of pure lead.
  2. The melt is allowed to cool.
  3. Temperature of the melt reaches the freezing curve of Pb where solid lead starts separating.
  4. As the system further cools, more and more lead separates and the liquid in equilibrium with the solid lead gets richer in silver.
  5. The lead that separates, floats and is continuously removed by ladles.
  6. When the temperature of the liquid reaches the eutectic temperature, solid lead is in equilibrium with the liquid having the eutectic composition.
  7. After removing the lead that separates, the liquid is cooled further when it solidifies to give a mixture of lead and silver having the eutectic composition of 2.6% of silver.
  8. This solid mixture of lead and silver is subjected to other processes for the recovery of silver.

Introduction to Electrochemical Cells

An electrochemical cell is a device that either converts chemical energy into electrical energy or uses electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction. There are two fundamental types of electrochemical cells:

  • Galvanic Cell (Voltaic Cell) — converts chemical energy into electrical energy via a spontaneous redox reaction.
  • Electrolytic Cell — uses electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction.

In both types, oxidation occurs at the anode and reduction occurs at the cathode. However, the polarity of the electrodes, the direction of current flow, and the source of energy differ fundamentally between the two cells.

Galvanic Cell (Voltaic Cell)

Definition

A Galvanic Cell (also called a voltaic cell) is an electrochemical cell in which a spontaneous redox reaction produces electrical energy. The chemical energy stored in the reactants is converted to electrical energy.

Key Features of a Galvanic Cell

  • The reaction is spontaneous — it occurs on its own without any external energy input.
  • The anode is negative (–) — oxidation occurs here, and electrons are released into the external circuit.
  • The cathode is positive (+) — reduction occurs here, and electrons are consumed from the external circuit.
  • Electrons flow through the external circuit from anode to cathode.
  • Conventional current flows from cathode to anode in the external circuit.
  • A salt bridge is used to maintain electrical neutrality between the two half-cells.
  • The EMF (electromotive force) of a galvanic cell is positive (\( E_{cell} > 0 \)) for a spontaneous reaction.
  • The Gibbs free energy change is negative: \( \Delta G = -nFE_{cell} < 0 \).

Example: Daniel Cell

The most classic example of a galvanic cell is the Daniel Cell:

Daniel Cell Notation

Zn(s) / Zn²⁺(1M) // Cu²⁺(1M) / Cu(s)

At Anode (–): \( \text{Zn}(s) \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \) (Oxidation)

At Cathode (+): \( \text{Cu}^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu}(s) \) (Reduction)

Overall: \( \text{Zn}(s) + \text{Cu}^{2+}(aq) \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}(aq) + \text{Cu}(s) \)

\( E^\circ_{cell} = E^\circ_{cathode} - E^\circ_{anode} = +0.34 - (-0.76) = +1.10 \text{ V} \)

[Daniel Cell Diagram] Left half-cell: Zinc rod (anode, –) immersed in ZnSO₄ solution. Right half-cell: Copper rod (cathode, +) immersed in CuSO₄ solution. A salt bridge (KCl or KNO₃) connects the two half-cells. Electrons flow through the external wire from Zn (anode) to Cu (cathode). Voltmeter measures EMF of approximately 1.10 V.

Other Examples of Galvanic Cells

  • Dry Cell (Leclanché Cell) — used in everyday batteries (torch, remote).
  • Lead Storage Battery — used in automobiles.
  • Hydrogen Fuel Cell — uses H₂ and O₂ to generate electricity with water as the only product.
  • Silver-Zinc Cell — used in watches and calculators.

Electrolytic Cell

Definition

An Electrolytic Cell is an electrochemical cell in which electrical energy from an external source is used to drive a non-spontaneous redox reaction. The electrical energy is converted to chemical energy.

Key Features of an Electrolytic Cell

  • The reaction is non-spontaneous — it requires an external source of electrical energy (battery or DC power supply) to proceed.
  • The anode is positive (+) — connected to the positive terminal of the external source; oxidation occurs here.
  • The cathode is negative (–) — connected to the negative terminal of the external source; reduction occurs here.
  • Electrons flow from the external source to the cathode, and from the anode back to the source.
  • No salt bridge is required — both electrodes are immersed in the same electrolyte.
  • The applied voltage must exceed the decomposition potential (minimum voltage needed to drive the reaction).
  • \( \Delta G > 0 \) for the reaction driven — the external electrical energy overcomes this positive free energy change.

Example: Electrolysis of Molten NaCl

Electrolysis of NaCl (Molten)

At Cathode (–): \( \text{Na}^+ + e^- \rightarrow \text{Na}(s) \) (Reduction)

At Anode (+): \( 2\text{Cl}^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}_2(g) + 2e^- \) (Oxidation)

Overall: \( 2\text{NaCl}(l) \rightarrow 2\text{Na}(s) + \text{Cl}_2(g) \)

[Electrolytic Cell Diagram] An external DC battery (or power supply) connects two electrodes dipped in the electrolyte. The positive terminal of the battery is connected to the anode (+) and the negative terminal to the cathode (–). Cations (Na⁺) migrate toward the cathode and get reduced. Anions (Cl⁻) migrate toward the anode and get oxidised. No salt bridge is needed.

Other Examples of Electrolytic Processes

  • Electroplating — deposition of a metal layer (e.g., silver, gold, chromium) on a base metal surface.
  • Electrorefining — purification of metals such as copper.
  • Electrolysis of water — production of H₂ and O₂ gases.
  • Chlor-alkali process — production of Cl₂, NaOH, and H₂ from brine (NaCl solution).
  • Anodising — formation of a protective oxide layer on aluminium.

Electrode Reactions in Both Cells

Galvanic Cell — Electrode Reactions

In a galvanic cell, the spontaneous redox reaction is split between the two half-cells:

  • Anode (–) — Oxidation:
    \[ M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \]
    Example: \( \text{Zn} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e^- \)
  • Cathode (+) — Reduction:
    \[ M^{n+} + ne^- \rightarrow M \]
    Example: \( \text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu} \)

Electrolytic Cell — Electrode Reactions

In an electrolytic cell, the external EMF forces the non-spontaneous reaction:

  • Cathode (–) — Reduction:
    \[ M^{n+} + ne^- \rightarrow M \]
    Example: \( \text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu} \) (metal deposited at cathode during electroplating)
  • Anode (+) — Oxidation:
    \[ M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \quad \text{or} \quad 2\text{Cl}^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}_2 + 2e^- \]
Key Similarity

In both types of cells: Oxidation always occurs at the anode and Reduction always occurs at the cathode. The difference lies only in the polarity of the electrodes and the source of energy driving the reaction.

Energy Conversion and Thermodynamics

Relationship Between ΔG, EMF, and Spontaneity

The relationship connecting Gibbs free energy change, cell EMF, and the number of electrons transferred is:

\[ \Delta G = -nFE_{cell} \]

Where:

  • \( \Delta G \) = Gibbs free energy change (J)
  • n = number of moles of electrons transferred
  • F = Faraday constant = 96,500 C mol⁻¹
  • \( E_{cell} \) = cell EMF (V)
Cell Type ΔG Ecell Reaction Type Energy Flow
Galvanic Cell ΔG < 0 (negative) Ecell > 0 (positive) Spontaneous Chemical → Electrical
Electrolytic Cell ΔG > 0 (positive) Applied voltage > Edecomp Non-spontaneous Electrical → Chemical

Decomposition Potential

In an electrolytic cell, the decomposition potential is the minimum voltage that must be applied to cause continuous electrolysis to occur. It is equal to the back-EMF of the cell:

\[ E_{applied} > E_{decomposition} \]
  • For water: decomposition potential ≈ 1.23 V (theoretically).
  • In practice, additional voltage called overvoltage (overpotential) is needed to overcome electrode kinetics.

Solved Examples

📐 Example 1 — Identifying Cell Type
Classify the following cells as galvanic or electrolytic: (i) A car battery discharging to power headlights. (ii) Electroplating of silver onto a spoon. (iii) A hydrogen fuel cell generating electricity. (iv) Electrolysis of brine in the chlor-alkali process.
Solution
  • (i) Car battery discharging — Galvanic cell. A spontaneous lead-acid cell reaction converts chemical energy to electrical energy to power the headlights.
  • (ii) Electroplating of silver — Electrolytic cell. An external DC source drives a non-spontaneous reaction to deposit silver metal at the cathode (spoon).
  • (iii) Hydrogen fuel cell — Galvanic cell. The spontaneous reaction between H₂ and O₂ produces electrical energy directly.
  • (iv) Chlor-alkali process — Electrolytic cell. External electrical energy drives the non-spontaneous decomposition of brine to produce Cl₂, NaOH, and H₂.
📐 Example 2 — ΔG and Spontaneity
The standard EMF of a Daniel cell is +1.10 V and n = 2. (i) Calculate ΔG° and determine if the reaction is spontaneous. (ii) If an external voltage of +1.10 V is applied in the reverse direction, what type of cell is formed?
Solution

Part (i): ΔG° of Daniel Cell

\[ \Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ_{cell} = -(2)(96500)(1.10) = -212{,}300 \text{ J} = -212.3 \text{ kJ} \]

Since ΔG° is negative, the reaction is spontaneous. The Daniel cell is a galvanic cell.

Part (ii): Applying reverse voltage

When a voltage ≥ 1.10 V is applied in the reverse direction, the cell becomes an electrolytic cell. The roles of anode and cathode are reversed: Cu is now oxidised at the anode and Zn²⁺ is reduced at the cathode. This is the principle of electrorefining of copper.

Electrode Potential

  • When a metal rod is dipped in a solution of its own ions, the electrical potential developed at the interface of the metal and its solution is called the electrode potential.
  • It is denoted by E.

Origin of Electrode Potential

When a metal M is in contact with solution containing its ions Mn+, two reactions are possible:

  • 1. Ionisation (Oxidation):
    \[ M \rightleftharpoons M^{n+} + ne^- \]
  • 2. Deposition (Reduction):
    \[ M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \]

Case I: If Ionization is Faster than Deposition

  • The metal acquires net negative charge, consequently retards the rate of ionization and increases the rate of deposition. This ultimately leads to the establishment of equilibrium.
  • The metal electrode gets negatively charged and attracts the layer of positive ions at the interface.
  • An electrical double layer is formed at the interface of metal and solution known as Helmholtz electrical double layer.
\[ M \rightleftharpoons M^{n+} + ne^- \]
[Helmholtz Double Layer — Case I] The metal surface is negatively charged (–). A layer of positive ions (Mn+) from the solution is attracted to the negatively charged metal surface, forming the Helmholtz electrical double layer. This double layer constitutes the electrode potential.

Case II: Deposition is Faster than Ionization

  • The metal acquires net positive charge, consequently retards the rate of deposition and increases rate of ionization. This ultimately leads to the establishment of equilibrium.
  • The metal electrode gets positively charged and attracts the layer of negative ions at the interface.
  • An electrical double layer is formed at the interface of metal and solution known as Helmholtz electrical double layer.
\[ M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \]

Standard Electrode Potential

  • The potential developed at the interface of metal and solution, when the metal is in contact with a solution of its own ions having unit concentration at 298 K.
  • In case of gas electrodes the partial pressure of gas is maintained at 1 atmospheric pressure.
  • It is represented as E°.

Electrochemical Cell

Single electrode potentials cannot be measured hence two electrodes are coupled together to form a cell.

Cell Notation

e.g. Daniel cell: Zn(s)/Zn²⁺ (1M)//Cu²⁺ (1M) / Cu(s)

Cell Potential

  • The difference in electrode potentials of the electrodes constituting the cell.
  • It is denoted by Ecell.

Standard Cell Potential

  • Ecell depends on concentration of the ions in the cell, temperature and the partial pressures of any gases involved in the cell reaction.
  • When all concentrations are 1M, all partial pressures of gases are 1 atm and temperature is 298 K, the emf is called Standard cell potential, E°cell.

Calculation of Ecell

\[ E_{cell} = E_{rhs} - E_{lhs} = E_{cathode} - E_{anode} \]
  • Ecell represents the driving force for the cell reaction to take place.
\[ \Delta G = -nFE_{CELL} \]
  • If reaction is spontaneous: ΔG is negative, thus ECELL should be positive.
  • If reaction is non-spontaneous: ΔG is positive, thus ECELL should be negative.

Electrochemical Series

  • In order to predict the electrochemical behavior of an electrode–electrolyte system, elements are arranged in the order of their standard reduction potentials.
  • This arrangement is known as the electrochemical series.
  • A negative value indicates oxidation tendency while a positive value indicates a reduction tendency.

Nernst Equation for a Single Electrode

A quantitative relationship between electrode potential and concentration of species with which the electrode is reversible.

The reaction at the electrode is: \( M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \)

The maximum work that can be obtained is: \( -\Delta G = W_{max} \)

For an electrochemical system, maximum work done is:

Wmax = Total charge available × Energy available per unit charge

  • Total charge available: No. of moles of electrons exchanged in redox reaction (n) multiplied by charge carried per mole of electrons, F (96,500 C/mol) = nF
  • Energy available per unit charge = electrode potential = E
\[ W_{max} = nFE \quad;\quad \Delta G = -nFE \]

Under standard conditions: \( \Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ \)

A thermodynamic equation which relates reaction quotient and decrease in free energy:

\[ \Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln Q \]

The reaction quotient for \( M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \) is: \( Q = \dfrac{[M]}{[M^{n+}]} \)

Substituting for ΔG, ΔG° and Q:

\[ -nFE = -nFE^\circ + RT\ln\left(\frac{[M]}{[M^{n+}]}\right) \]

Dividing throughout by –nF:

\[ E = E^\circ - \frac{RT}{nF}\ln\left(\frac{[M]}{[M^{n+}]}\right) \]

Since [M] = 1 for pure substances:

\[ E = E^\circ + \frac{RT}{nF}\ln([M^{n+}]) \]

At 298 K:

\[ E = E^\circ + \frac{0.0591}{n}\log([M^{n+}]) \]

Nernst Equation for Electrochemical Cells

Nernst equation may also be used to calculate the emf of electrochemical cells. For the cell reaction:

\[ aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD \]
\[ Q = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b} \]

Nernst equation is:

\[ E = E^\circ - \frac{0.0591}{n}\log\left(\frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}\right) \]

where n = no. of electrons transferred, E°cell = std. emf of the cell.

Types of Electrodes

  • In order to form a cell, 2 half cells or 2 electrodes are required.
  • Various types of electrodes are available which are constructed based on the application.

1. Metal-Metal Ion Electrode

  • Metal in contact with a solution of its own ions.
  • e.g., Zn/Zn²⁺, Cu/Cu²⁺, Ag/Ag⁺
  • \( M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \)

Nernst equation:

\[ E_{M/M^{n+}} = E^\circ_{M/M^{n+}} + \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log[M^{n+}] \]

2. Metal-Metal Insoluble Salt-Ion Electrode

  • These electrodes consist of a metal in contact with a sparingly soluble salt of the same metal dipped in a solution of soluble salt of the same anion.
  • e.g., Calomel electrode Hg/Hg₂Cl₂/KCl, Ag/AgCl(s)/HCl
  • For silver–silver chloride electrode: \( \text{AgCl} + e^- \rightleftharpoons \text{Ag} + \text{Cl}^- \)

Nernst equation:

\[ E_{Ag/AgCl/Cl^-} = E^\circ_{Ag/AgCl/Cl^-} - \frac{0.0591}{1}\log[\text{Cl}^-] \]

3. Gas Electrode

  • It consists of gas bubbling about an inert metal foil, immersed in solution containing ions to which the gas is reversible.
  • The metal provides electrical contact and facilitates the establishment of equilibrium between the gas and its ions.
  • e.g., Hydrogen electrode Pt/H₂/H⁺, Chlorine electrode Pt/Cl₂/Cl⁻
  • For a hydrogen electrode: \( 2H^+ + 2e^- \rightleftharpoons H_2 \)

Nernst equation:

\[ E_{Pt/H_2/H^+} = E^0_{Pt/H_2/H^+} - \frac{0.0591}{2}\log\left(\frac{p_{H_2}}{[H^+]^2}\right) \]

4. Amalgam Electrode

  • It is similar to metal-metal ion electrode in which metal amalgam is in contact with a solution containing its own ions.
  • e.g., Lead amalgam electrode Pb-Hg/Pb²⁺
  • For lead amalgam electrode: \( \text{Pb}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightleftharpoons \text{Pb-Hg} \)

Nernst equation:

\[ E_{Pb^{2+}/Pb-Hg} = E^0_{Pb^{2+}/Pb-Hg} - \frac{0.0591}{2}\log\left(\frac{[\text{Pb-Hg}]}{[\text{Pb}^{2+}]}\right) \]

5. Oxidation-Reduction Electrode

  • It consists of an inert metal such as platinum immersed in a solution containing an appropriate oxidized and reduced form of redox system.
  • The metal merely acts as electrical contact.
  • The potential arises due to the tendency of one form to change into the other form.
  • e.g., Pt/Fe²⁺,Fe³⁺, Pt/Ce³⁺,Ce⁴⁺, Pt/Sn²⁺,Sn⁴⁺
  • For stannous-stannic electrode: \( \text{Sn}^{4+} + 2e^- \rightleftharpoons \text{Sn}^{2+} \)

Nernst equation:

\[ E_{Pt/Sn^{4+}/Sn^{2+}} = E^0_{Pt/Sn^{4+}/Sn^{2+}} - \frac{0.0591}{2}\log\left(\frac{[\text{Sn}^{2+}]}{[\text{Sn}^{4+}]}\right) \]

Quinhydrone Electrode

  • It consists of an inert metal such as platinum immersed in a solution containing quinone and hydroquinone.
  • The metal merely acts as electrical contact.
  • The potential arises due to the tendency of quinone to change to hydroquinone.
  • Pt/Q,QH₂

Reaction: Quinone + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → Hydroquinone

Nernst equation:

\[ E_{Pt/Q/QH_2} = E^\circ_{Pt/Q/QH_2} - \frac{0.0591}{2}\log\left(\frac{[QH_2]}{[Q][H^+]^2}\right) \]

6. Ion Selective Electrode (Membrane Electrode)

  • It consists of a membrane in contact with a solution, with which it can exchange ions.
  • e.g., glass electrode: selective to H⁺, Na⁺, K⁺ etc.

Equation for determining potential for pH sensitive glass electrode:

\[ E_G = E^0_G + 0.0591\log_{10}[H^+] \]

Ion Selective Electrodes (ISE) — General Features

  • Selectively respond to a specific ion in a mixture.
  • Potential developed is a function of concentration of that ion.
  • Have a membrane which is capable of exchanging the specific ion with solution with which it is in contact.
  • Membrane electrodes — e.g., glass electrode.

Reference Electrodes

  • Electrodes whose potentials are accurately known, stable and with reference to which the electrode potential of any electrode can be measured.
  • Reference electrode is combined with indicator electrode and emf of the cell is measured.
  • Two types of reference electrodes:
    • Primary reference electrodes: Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)
    • Secondary reference electrodes: Calomel electrode, Silver-silver chloride electrode

Primary Reference Electrode: Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)

  • Electrode potential is assigned a value of 0.0 V.
  • Gas electrode: Pt/H₂/H⁺
  • \( 2H^+ + 2e^- \rightleftharpoons H_2 \)
  • Used to measure potential of other electrodes.

Examples of SHE in Use

For Zn/Zn²⁺//H⁺(1M)/H₂(1atm),Pt:

\[ E_{cell} = E_{cathode} - E_{anode} \] \[ 0.76 = 0.0 - E_{Zn/Zn^{2+}} \Rightarrow E_{Zn/Zn^{2+}} = -0.76\text{ V} \]

For Pt,H₂(1atm)/H⁺(1M)//Cu²⁺/Cu:

\[ 0.34 = E_{Cu/Cu^{2+}} - 0.0 \Rightarrow E_{Cu/Cu^{2+}} = +0.34\text{ V} \]

Disadvantages of SHE

  • Maintaining concentration of H⁺ ions at 1M and pressure of H₂ gas at 1 atm is difficult.
  • Platinum is highly susceptible to poisoning by different impurities in gas.
  • It cannot be used with oxidizing and reducing environments.

Secondary Reference Electrodes

Due to the limitations of standard hydrogen electrode, some other electrodes whose electrode potentials are accurately known and remain stable over a long period of time and can be easily assembled are used. With respect to these electrodes, electrode potentials of other electrodes can be assigned.

e.g., Calomel electrode, silver-silver chloride electrode

Secondary Reference Electrode: Calomel Electrode

  • Most widely used reference electrode.
  • Metal-insoluble salt–ion electrode.
  • Hg/Hg₂Cl₂(s)/Cl⁻

Construction

  • A glass tube containing a layer of mercury over which a paste of insoluble salt Hg₂Cl₂ (calomel) + Hg and the next layer is a solution of KCl.
  • A Pt wire dipped in Hg provides electrical contact.
  • Tube is fitted with a side tube to fill KCl solution of known concentration and another side tube which connects to the salt bridge.

Working

Can act as anode or cathode depending on the nature of the electrode with which it is coupled.

As anode:

  • \( 2Hg \rightleftharpoons Hg_2^{2+} + 2e^- \)
  • \( Hg_2^{2+} + 2Cl^- \rightleftharpoons Hg_2Cl_2 \)
  • Overall: \( 2Hg + 2Cl^- \rightleftharpoons Hg_2Cl_2 + 2e^- \)

As cathode:

  • \( Hg_2^{2+} + 2e^- \rightleftharpoons 2Hg \)
  • \( Hg_2Cl_2 \rightleftharpoons Hg_2^{2+} + 2Cl^- \)
  • Overall: \( Hg_2Cl_2 + 2e^- \rightleftharpoons 2Hg + 2Cl^- \)

Applying Nernst's equation:

\[ E = E^\circ - \frac{2.303RT}{2F}\log[\text{Cl}^-]^2 \]

At 298 K: \( E = E^\circ - 0.0591\log[\text{Cl}^-] \)

  • Electrode is reversible to chloride ions.
  • Electrode potential depends on chloride ion concentration.

Concentration Cells

  • An electrochemical cell in which identical electrodes are in contact with a solution of identical species but of different concentration.
  • An electrolyte has spontaneous tendency to diffuse from a solution of higher concentration to a solution of lower concentration which is the driving force for development of potential.
  • Oxidation takes place at anode and reduction takes place at cathode.
  • e.g., Cu/Cu²⁺(c₁)//Cu²⁺(c₂)/Cu (where c₂ > c₁)

Expression for Cell Potential (Copper Concentration Cell)

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\left(\frac{c_2}{c_1}\right) \]

At 298 K:

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{0.0591}{n}\log\left(\frac{c_2}{c_1}\right) \]
  • The emf of the cell is positive only if c₂ > c₁, i.e., conc. of metal ion at cathode > conc. of metal ion at anode.
  • The emf of the cell depends upon the ratio c₂/c₁.
  • When c₂ = c₁, the emf of the cell becomes zero.
  • During working of the cell, concentration of ions increases at anode and decreases at cathode.
  • When current is drawn from the cell, c₁ increases and c₂ decreases.
  • The cell can operate only as long as the concentration terms are different.

Types of Concentration Cells

Electrolyte Concentration Cell

  • Consists of two same electrodes that are dipped in the same electrolyte but with different concentrations of electrolytes.
  • Cu/Cu²⁺(c₁)//Cu²⁺(c₂)/Cu
\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\left(\frac{c_2}{c_1}\right) \]

Electrode Concentration Cell

  • Consists of two identical electrodes of different activity which are dipped in the same solution of electrolyte.
  • Na-Hg(c₁)/Na⁺/Na-Hg(c₂)
  • Reactions: At anode: Na-Hg(c₁) → Na⁺ + e⁻; At cathode: Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na-Hg(c₂)
\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\frac{\text{Na-Hg}(c_1)}{\text{Na-Hg}(c_2)} \]

Gas Concentration Cells

For Pt/H₂(p₁ atm)/H⁺/H₂(p₂ atm)/Pt:

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\frac{p_1}{p_2} \]

For Pt/Cl₂(p₁ atm)/Cl⁻/Cl₂(p₂ atm)/Pt:

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\frac{p_2}{p_1} \]

Glass Electrode

  • Ion-selective electrode.
  • Responds to Hydrogen ion.
  • pH sensitive; can be used to determine pH of a solution.
  • Consists of a glass membrane which is capable of exchanging H⁺ ions.

Construction

  • Glass tube, the end of which is a bulb of very thin glass membrane.
  • Glass bulb is made up of special type of glass, CORNING 015.
  • The glass bulb is filled with solution of known pH which is the reference solution.
  • A silver-silver chloride electrode is dipped inside the reference solution; serves as internal reference electrode and also provides external electrical contact.
  • The electrode is immersed in a solution containing H⁺ which is the analyte.
  • Ag/AgCl/HCl/glass

Working

  • The inner and outer surfaces of the glass membrane can exchange H⁺ ions with the solution they are in contact with.
  • Ion exchange reaction: \( H^+_{solution} + Na^+Gl^- \rightleftharpoons Na^+_{solution} + H^+Gl^- \)
  • The hydrated glass membrane brings about ion exchange reaction between singly charged cations in the interstices of glass lattice and protons from the solution.
  • A potential is developed, which is a function of H⁺ of the solution.

Electrode Potential of Glass Electrode

The boundary potential is:

\[ E_b = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\left(\frac{C_1}{C_2}\right) \]

Since concentration of reference solution C₂ is constant:

\[ E_b = L' + \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log C_1 \quad\text{where}\quad L' = -\frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log C_2 \]

At 298 K (since for H⁺, n = 1):

\[ E_b = L' + 0.0591\log[H^+] = L' - 0.0591\text{pH} \]

The glass electrode potential has 3 components:

  1. The boundary potential
  2. The potential of internal reference electrode
  3. Asymmetric potential
\[ E_G = E_b + E_{ref} + E_{asymmetric} \]

Asymmetric potential arises due to difference in responses of inner and outer surfaces of the glass bulb, due to differing conditions of stress on two glass surfaces.

\[ E_G = E^0_G + 0.0591\log[H^+] \quad\text{where}\quad E^0_G = L' + E_{ref} + E_{asymmetric} \]
\[ E_G = E^0_G - 0.0591\text{pH} \]

Determination of pH Using Glass Electrode

  • Glass electrode is combined with an external reference electrode.
  • Cell: Hg/Hg₂Cl₂/Cl⁻//analyte solution/glass/0.1N HCl/AgCl/Ag
  • The emf of the cell is determined potentiometrically.
\[ E_{cell} = E_G - E_{calomel} = E^0_G - 0.0591\text{pH} - E_{calomel} \]
\[ \text{pH} = \frac{E^0_G - E_{calomel} - E_{cell}}{0.0591} \]
Calibration

To evaluate E°G, the glass electrode is dipped in a solution of known pH (buffer solution) and combined with calomel electrode. The emf of the cell is measured from which E°G can be evaluated.

Applications of Glass Electrode

Used extensively in chemical, industrial, agricultural and biological labs.

Advantages of Glass Electrode

  • Can be used in oxidizing and reducing environments and metal ions.
  • Does not get poisoned.
  • Can be used for very small volumes.
  • Accurate results can be obtained between pH 1 to 9 by ordinary electrodes. However by using special glass electrodes pH 1 to 14 can be measured with accuracy.
  • Simple to operate and can be used with portable instruments.

Disadvantages of Glass Electrode

  • Because of high resistance of glass, a simple potentiometer cannot be used. It requires sensitive potentiometer for emf measurements.
  • Glass membrane is very delicate, hence has to be handled carefully.
  • At very high pH levels usually over a pH of 9, Alkaline error is observed — when the Sodium ion level is relatively high, some of the H⁺ ions in the gel layer around the sensitive electrode membrane are replaced by Na⁺ ions. The electrode may eventually respond to Na⁺ instead of H⁺ ions, giving a false lower pH value than the actual value.
Module VI

Corrosion Chemistry

Types of corrosion, electrochemical theory, factors affecting corrosion, protective methods and cathodic protection.

Corrosion

  • The destruction or deterioration & consequent loss of metal through direct chemical or electrochemical attack by the environment.
  • Most metals except very noble ones, corrode on contact with water (and moisture in the air), acids, bases, salts, oils, aggressive metal polishes, and other solid and liquid chemicals.
  • Spontaneous process.
  • Causes indirect losses.

Corrosion Products

Pure metal → undesirable products

  • Rusting of iron — a reddish brown scale formation on iron and steel objects. Due to formation of hydrated ferric oxide (Fe₂O₃.xH₂O).
  • Scaling of Copper — Green scales formed on copper articles. Due to formation of basic cupric carbonate (CuCO₃ + Cu(OH)₂).

Why Do Metals Undergo Corrosion?

  • Most metals occur in nature in the form of their compounds such as oxides, sulfides, carbonates, chlorides.
  • Metals are extracted from these ores by reduction which is an endothermic process, energy being supplied in the form of heat or electrical energy.
  • Pure metals are relatively at higher energy state compared to their corresponding ores, and they have a natural tendency to revert back to their combined state.
[Corrosion Cycle Diagram] Mineral (metal ore) at top. Arrow down: Extraction (Endothermic process). Metal at bottom. Arrow up: Corrosion (Spontaneous process). This cycle illustrates that corrosion is the reverse of the metal extraction process.

Types of Corrosion

Dry Corrosion

  • Direct chemical attack due to affinity of the metal to certain gases.
  • In the absence of the electrolytic medium.
  • Gases: O₂, F₂, H₂S, NH₃

Wet Corrosion

  • Electrochemical attack on the metal.
  • In the presence of moisture or conducting medium.

Electrochemical Theory of Corrosion

  • A large number of tiny galvanic cells are formed due to the formation of separate anodic and cathodic regions on the metal surface.
  • At the anodic region: metal undergoes oxidation and gets converted into its ions, liberating electrons and the metal undergoes corrosion.
  • At the cathodic region: reduction takes place but since the metal cannot be reduced further, metal atoms are unaffected by corrosion.
  • The electrons liberated at the anodic region migrate to the cathodic region constituting a small corrosion current.
  • The metal ions formed at the anode and some anions formed at the cathode diffuse towards each other through the conducting medium and form a corrosion product somewhere between anode and the cathode.
  • Corrosion of metal continues as long as both anodic and cathodic reactions take place simultaneously.
Corrosion Reactions

At Anode: \( M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \)

At Cathode (Liberation of hydrogen — acidic medium): \( 2H^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2\uparrow \)

At Cathode (Liberation of hydrogen — neutral/alkaline): \( 2H_2O + 2e^- \rightarrow 2OH^- + H_2\uparrow \)

At Cathode (Absorption of oxygen — acidic medium): \( 4H^+ + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 2H_2O \)

At Cathode (Absorption of oxygen — neutral/alkaline): \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Corrosion of Iron (Rusting)

At Anode: \( \text{Fe} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+} + 2e^- \)

At Cathode: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Fe²⁺ and OH⁻ ions move towards one another and Fe(OH)₂ is formed somewhere between anode and cathode regions:

\[ 2Fe^{2+} + 4OH^- \rightarrow 2Fe(OH)_2 \]

In an oxidizing environment, Fe(OH)₂ is oxidized to hydrated ferric oxide — yellow rust:

\[ 4Fe(OH)_2 + O_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2[Fe_2O_3.3H_2O] \]

In the presence of limited oxygen, 4Fe(OH)₂ is converted into magnetic oxide of iron (Fe₃O₄) — black rust:

\[ 3Fe(OH)_2 + \frac{1}{2}O_2 \rightarrow Fe_3O_4.3H_2O \]

Differential Metal Corrosion (Galvanic Corrosion)

  • Also called galvanic corrosion.
  • When two dissimilar metals are in contact with each other and are exposed to a corrosive environment.
  • The two metals differ in their electrode potentials. The metal with lower electrode potential acts as anode and the other metal with higher electrode potential acts as cathode.
  • The anodic metal undergoes oxidation and gets corroded. The cathodic metal remains unaffected.
  • The driving force for corrosion is the difference in electrode potentials of the two metals.
  • Example: Iron in contact with copper — E°(Fe) = −0.44 V, E°(Cu) = +0.34 V → Fe acts as anode and corrodes.
Reactions (Fe-Cu System)

At Anode: \( \text{Fe} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+} + 2e^- \)

At Cathode: \( \frac{1}{2}O_2 + H_2O + 2e^- \rightarrow 2OH^- \)

Differential Aeration Corrosion

  • When a metal is exposed to different concentrations of air (O₂), part of the metal exposed to lower concentration of O₂ becomes anodic and undergoes corrosion.
  • Other part of the metal exposed to higher concentration of oxygen becomes cathodic and remains unaffected.
  • The difference in oxygen concentration produces a potential difference and cause corrosion current to flow.
Reactions

At Anode: \( M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \)

At Cathode: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Waterline Corrosion

  • A special case of differential aeration corrosion.
  • Observed when metal is half immersed in water.
  • The part immersed in water is exposed to less O₂, hence acts as anode and gets corroded while the part not in water is exposed to more O₂ and acts as cathode — remains unaffected by corrosion.

Pitting Corrosion

  • A localized and accelerated corrosion.
  • Results in formation of pits or pin holes, around which the metal is relatively unattacked.
  • One of the most destructive forms of corrosion.
  • Characterized by small anodic area and large cathodic area resulting in accelerated corrosion at the anodic area.

Case I — Dust Particle Deposits

  • When dust particles or oil drops get deposited over the metal surface.
  • The portion of the metal covered by dust which is less aerated becomes anodic.
  • The adjacent area of the metal which is exposed to higher concentration of O₂ becomes cathodic.
  • A small anodic region surrounded by a large cathodic region is formed — metal undergoes accelerated corrosion forming a deep and narrow pit.
Reactions

At Anode: \( M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \)

At Cathode: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Case II — Breakdown of Protective Film

  • The break down of the protective film on a metal surface.
  • Peeling off of a small tin coating on iron gives rise to a small anodic area (Fe) and large cathodic area (Sn).
Reactions (Tin on Iron)

At Anode: \( \text{Fe} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+} + 2e^- \)

At Cathode: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Stress Corrosion (Stress Corrosion Cracking — SCC)

  • A type of corrosion that occurs when some part of the metallic material is under stress and exposed to specific corrosive environment.
  • During the manufacture or fabrication of articles, metals are subjected to mechanical operations such as pressing, hammering, rolling, bending, quenching, welding and riveting.
  • The stress can be external stress acting on the metal during service conditions or residual stress or both.
  • Due to stress a crack is initiated.
  • The metal atoms under stress are always at higher energy levels therefore becomes more reactive than the stress free part.
  • A corrosion cell is formed with the stressed part acting as anode and the stress free part acting as cathode.

Conditions for Stress Corrosion

  • Tensile stress
  • Specific corrosive environment

Specific Corrosive Environment

  • Brass — ammoniacal solution or ammonia vapours
  • Steel — NaOH and chloride ions

Causes serious damage — air crashes, bridge collapses, boiler explosions.

Factors Affecting Corrosion

Nature of the Metal

  • Metals with lower electrode potential values are more reactive than the metals with higher electrode potential values.
  • More reactive metals are more susceptible to corrosion.
  • Metals like K, Na, Mg, Zn etc., with low electrode potential values are highly susceptible for corrosion.
  • The noble metals such as silver, gold, platinum etc., with higher potential values are less susceptible for corrosion.
  • Galvanic series is required which arranges metals and alloys in the order of their tendency to undergo corrosion in a particular environment.

Nature of the Corrosion Product

  • For some metals like Chromium, Aluminum etc., the corrosion product formed acts as a protective film.
  • If the corrosion product deposited is insoluble, stable, uniform, and non-porous, it acts as a protective film preventing further corrosion.
  • A thin, invisible, impervious, continuous film formed on the surface acts as a barrier.
  • Metals like iron, zinc, magnesium do not form protective film; if the corrosion product is soluble, unstable, non-uniform, and porous, corrosion continues unabated.
  • Steel gets corroded but stainless steel does not.

Hydrogen Overvoltage

  • Overvoltage is defined as the extra voltage over the theoretical voltage required for an electrode reaction to take place.
  • Depends on the surface — if overvoltage of a gas is high on a surface, liberation of the gas on that surface will be less.
  • A metal with low hydrogen overvoltage on its surface is more susceptible to corrosion.
  • If cathodic reaction is hydrogen evolution type, hydrogen gas is liberated easily and the cathodic reaction rate is faster — makes anodic reaction also fast, thereby promoting overall corrosion.
  • When hydrogen overvoltage is high, cathodic reaction is slower and the corrosion also becomes slower.

Temperature

  • The rate of a chemical reaction, in general, increases with rise in temperature.
  • For corrosion process (redox reaction), the rate of corrosion increases as the temperature increases due to: increase in rate of reaction, increase in conductance of the medium, decrease in polarization effects, increase in solubility of corrosion product, breakdown in the protective film.
  • However, if corrosion is due to dissolved gases like O₂, CO₂, etc., the rate of corrosion decreases with increase in temperature as the solubility of these gases in the medium decreases.

pH

  • In general, lower the pH of the corrosion medium, higher is the corrosion rate.
  • At low pH, concentration of H⁺ ions is high; H⁺ ions help for cathodic reaction facilitating anodic oxidation of the metal: \( 2H^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2 \)
  • Corrosion product becomes more soluble in acidic medium. However some metals like Al undergo fast corrosion in highly alkaline solution as their corrosion product is soluble in alkaline medium.

Polarization at Anodic and Cathodic Region

Polarisation is the drift in electrode potential of an electrode due to change in concentration of species.

  • Anodic polarisation: Metal ions get concentrated at anodic area and this decreases tendency of metal to undergo oxidation; corrosion rate falls. Due to accumulation of ions in the anodic region.
  • Cathodic polarisation: Due to retarded movement of cathodic reactant (O₂, H⁺) or retarded removal of products. A retarded cathodic reaction results in retardation of anodic reaction also, which slows down the corrosion process. Due to depletion of ions in the cathodic region.
  • Polarisation effect can be reduced by: use of depolarisers (anodic — complexing agents; cathodic — cupric ions), increase in temperature, stirring the solution.

Protective Coatings: Metallic Coating

  • Corrosion of base metal is prevented by coating a layer of another metal over it.
  • Metal coated may be anodic or cathodic to the base metal.

Anodic Metal Coating

  • Coating of a layer of metal which is anodic to base metal.
  • Coating metal is more active with respect to the base metal.
  • Even if the base metal is not completely covered, it will not undergo pitting corrosion because the base metal acts as cathode.
  • e.g., Coating of Zn or Mg on iron.

Cathodic Metal Coating

  • Coating of a layer of metal which is cathodic to base metal.
  • Coating metal is less active with respect to the base metal.
  • Base metal should be completely covered — if some part is left uncovered it undergoes more intense pitting corrosion due to formation of small anodic and large cathodic area.
  • e.g., Coating of Sn or Ni metal over iron.

Galvanizing

  • Coating a layer of zinc on iron is called galvanization.

Process Steps

  1. Iron sheet is passed through organic solvent or caustic solution to remove oil or grease present on it.
  2. It is washed with dil. H₂SO₄ to remove any rust, scale or dust present on the surface (pickling).
  3. It is treated with a mixture of aqueous solution of ZnCl₂ and NH₄Cl which acts as flux which prevents oxidation and then dried.
  4. Finally it is dipped in molten zinc at 425–430°C.
  5. Excess zinc present on iron sheet is removed by passing through hot rollers.

Application

Galvanization of iron is carried out to produce roofing sheets, fencing wire, buckets, bolts, nuts, pipes etc.

Advantage

Even if the Zn coating peels off or there are gaps at some places, the base metal (Fe) does not get corroded because the base metal acts as cathode.

Disadvantage

Galvanized articles are not used for preparing and storing food because zinc dissolves in dilute acids producing toxic zinc compounds.

Inorganic Coating — Anodizing

Inorganic Coating

  • Referred to as chemical conversion coating.
  • Surface of the base metal is converted into a protective coating through appropriate chemical modification.
  • The protective film (corrosion product) is an inherent part of the metal.
  • Two methods: Anodising and Phosphating.

Anodizing

  • Induced oxidation in an oxidising environment by making the article anode and passing current.
  • Oxidation of outer layer of metal to its metal oxide which covers the metal surface and acts as protective layer.
  • Done for non-ferrous metals like Al, Cr, Ti.

Anodizing of Aluminum — Process

  1. Article is cleaned, degreased, and polished and taken as anode.
  2. It is immersed in an electrolyte consisting of 5–10% chromic acid, sulfuric acid, phosphoric acid, oxalic acid or their mixtures maintained at around 40°C.
  3. Lead or steel is taken as cathode.
  4. Voltage above 40 V is applied.
  5. Outer layer of Al is oxidised to Al₂O₃:
    \[ 2Al + 3H_2O \rightarrow Al_2O_3 + 3H_2 \]
  • Al₂O₃ formed on the surface is slightly porous in nature.
  • The porous layer may be made compact by sealing — immersing the anodised article in boiling water or steam. Al₂O₃ is converted into Al₂O₃.H₂O which occupies higher volume; the pores are sealed.
  • Anodized articles can be produced in good colour finishes by adsorbing an organic dye after anodising and then sealing, or depositing a metal like Ni, Co etc., into the pores.

Application

Anodized Al is used as an attractive, highly durable, corrosion resistant material in exteriors for roofs, walls, buildings and also in window frames, office partitions, tiffin carriers, etc.

Organic Coating

  • Coating of metal surfaces with organic materials, particularly paints and lacquers is the most widely used anti-corrosion coatings.
  • The coatings serve to keep out air and moisture from the metal surface or serve as a barrier between the metal surface and the corrosion environment.

Corrosion Inhibitors

  • Chemical substances which are added in small quantities to the corrosive environment to decrease the rate of corrosion.
  • Inhibitors slow down or inhibit the anodic or cathodic reaction and control corrosion.
  • They can be used only in confined environment.
  • The action of an inhibitor depends on the nature of the metal to be protected as well as the corrosive environment.

Anodic Inhibitors

  • If the formation of Mn+ is prevented, the corrosion process is retarded.
  • Achieved by the addition of large anions such as chromate (CrO₄²⁻), tungstate (WO₄²⁻), etc.
  • These ions combine with Mn+ and form a precipitate which covers the surface of the anode.
  • Anodic inhibitors are effective only when sufficient amount is added. If insufficient, a part of the anodic region is exposed → formation of small anodic area → intense corrosion.
  • Salts like Na₂CrO₄, Na₂WO₄.

Cathodic Inhibitors

Cathodic reactions include liberation of hydrogen and absorption of oxygen. Inhibiting any of these reactions will slow down the cathodic reaction, which in turn slows down the anodic reaction — corrosion is inhibited.

Inhibition of Hydrogen Liberation

  • Preventing diffusion of H⁺ ions to cathode: By addition of certain organic compounds which contain nitrogen or sulfur. Aliphatic amines, urea, thiourea, mercaptans, heterocyclic compounds — they get adsorbed on the cathodic region forming a protective film.
  • By increasing hydrogen overvoltage: By addition of oxides of arsenic, antimony (As₂O₃, Sb₂O₃) or salts like sodium meta-arsenite (NaAsO₂). They deposit on the cathode region — hydrogen overvoltage becomes very high; liberation of H₂ is reduced.

Inhibition of Oxygen Absorption

  • By removing oxygen from corrosive media: By adding oxygen scavengers like hydrazine: \( N_2H_4 + O_2 \rightarrow N_2 + 2H_2O \); by adding reducing agents like sodium sulfite: \( 2Na_2SO_3 + O_2 \rightarrow 2Na_2SO_4 \).
  • By decreasing the diffusion rate of oxygen to cathode: By adding salts with large cations such as ZnSO₄, MgSO₄, NiSO₄. The cations (Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺) migrate towards the cathode surface and react with the hydroxyl ions: \( Zn^{2+} + 2OH^- \rightarrow Zn(OH)_2 \). The hydroxides get deposited on the cathodic sites — the protective film impermeable to oxygen prevents its diffusion.

Limitations of Corrosion Inhibitors

  • They contaminate the environment.
  • Many of the inhibitors are toxic, cannot be used in systems which come in contact with humans.
  • Can be used only in closed systems in which corrosive environment is either contained or re-circulated.

Cathodic Protection

  • By converting the metal structure completely into cathode and no part of it is allowed to act as anode.
  • Cathode does not undergo corrosion so the structure is protected.

Two methods:

  • Sacrificial Anode Method
  • Impressed Current Method

Sacrificial Anode Method

  • Protected metal structure is converted into a cathode by connecting it to a more active metal.
  • This active metal (example: zinc, magnesium) acts as an auxiliary anode.
  • These metals being more active, act as anode and undergo preferential corrosion, protecting the metal structure.
  • Since the anodic metals are sacrificed to protect the metal structure, the method is known as sacrificial anode method.
  • Exhausted anodes have to be replaced periodically.

Advantages

  • The method is simple.
  • Low installation cost.
  • Does not require power supply.

Disadvantages

  • Involves recurring expenditure for replacement of consumed anodes.

Impressed Current Method

  • By applying a direct current.
  • The protected metal is made cathodic by connecting it to the cathode of the external source of current.
  • Anode of the external source is usually connected to an inert electrode like graphite; Platinum, silicon, iron are also used as anodes.
  • A backfill of coke, bentonite is used to improve efficiency of the anode (reduces electrical resistivity, provides lower anode-to-earth resistance and greater current outputs).
  • The metal structure being cathode does not undergo corrosion.
  • Anode being inert remains unaffected.

Advantages

  • One installation can protect large area of metal.

Limitations

  • Rather expensive, since it needs high current for safe protection of structure.
  • If the impressed current is not uniform on the entire surface of the protected structure, localized corrosion takes place on the protected metal.
  • If hydrogen liberation is the cathodic reaction on the metal surface, then the protected metal may suffer from hydrogen embrittlement — H₂ molecule being small enters the metal lattice and when the pressure increases, it causes blisters and makes the metal brittle.

Electrochemical Studies

Electrochemistry involves the study of:

  • Redox reaction — reactions involving simultaneous oxidation and reduction.
  • Electrodes — Anode (oxidation) and Cathode (reduction).
  • Electrolytic conductance through electrolyte due to movement of ions — through acid, alkali or salt solutions; molten electrolytes; solid electrolytes.

Electrode Potential

  • When a metal rod is dipped in a solution of its own ions, the electrical potential developed at the interface of the metal and its solution is called the electrode potential.
  • It is denoted by E.

Origin of Electrode Potential

When a metal M is in contact with solution containing its ions Mn+, two reactions are possible:

1. Ionisation (Oxidation)

\[ M \rightleftharpoons M^{n+} + ne^- \]

2. Deposition (Reduction)

\[ M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \]

Case I: If Ionization is Faster than Deposition

  • The metal acquires net negative charge, consequently retards the rate of ionization and increases the rate of deposition. This ultimately leads to the establishment of equilibrium.
  • The metal electrode gets negatively charged and attracts the layer of positive ions at the interface.
  • An electrical double layer is formed at the interface of metal and solution known as Helmholtz electrical double layer.
[Helmholtz Electrical Double Layer — Case I] The metal (blue) becomes negatively charged (−) and attracts a layer of positive ions (+) from the solution at the interface. This forms the Helmholtz electrical double layer. Reaction: M ⇌ Mn+ + ne−

Case II: If Deposition is Faster than Ionization

  • The metal acquires net positive charge, consequently retards the rate of deposition and increases rate of ionization. This ultimately leads to the establishment of equilibrium.
  • The metal electrode gets positively charged and attracts the layer of negative ions at the interface.
  • An electrical double layer is formed at the interface of metal and solution known as Helmholtz electrical double layer.
[Helmholtz Electrical Double Layer — Case II] The metal (blue) becomes positively charged (+) and attracts a layer of negative ions (−) from the solution at the interface. Reaction: Mn+ + ne− ⇌ M

Standard Electrode Potential

  • The potential developed at the interface of metal and solution, when the metal is in contact with a solution of its own ions having unit concentration at 298 K.
  • In case of gas electrodes the partial pressure of gas is maintained at 1 atmospheric pressure.
  • It is represented as E°.

Electrochemical Cell

Single electrode potentials cannot be measured; hence two electrodes are coupled together to form a cell.

Cell Notation

e.g., Daniel cell:

\[ Zn(s)/Zn^{2+}(1M)//Cu^{2+}(1M)/Cu(s) \]

Cell Potential

  • The difference in electrode potentials of the electrodes constituting the cell.
  • It is denoted by Ecell.

Standard Cell Potential

  • Ecell depends on concentration of the ions in the cell, temperature and the partial pressures of any gases involved in the cell reaction.
  • When all the concentrations are 1M, all partial pressures of gases are 1 atm and temperature is 298 K, the emf is called Standard cell potential, E°cell.

Calculation of Ecell

\[ E_{cell} = E_{rhs} - E_{lhs} = E_{cathode} - E_{anode} \]
  • Ecell represents the driving force for the cell reaction to take place.
\[ \Delta G = -nFE_{CELL} \]
  • If reaction is spontaneous: ΔG is negative, thus ECELL should be positive.
  • If reaction is non-spontaneous: ΔG is positive, thus ECELL should be negative.

Nernst Equation for a Single Electrode

A quantitative relationship between electrode potential and concentration of species with which the electrode is reversible.

The reaction at the electrode is:

\[ M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \]

The maximum work that can be obtained is: \( -\Delta G = W_{max} \)

For an electrochemical system, maximum work done is:

Maximum Work

\( W_{max} = \text{Total charge available} \times \text{Energy available per unit charge} \)

  • Total charge available: No. of moles of electrons exchanged in redox reaction (n) multiplied by charge carried per mole of electrons, F (96,500 C/mol) = nF
  • Energy available per unit charge: electrode potential E

Therefore: \( W_{max} = nFE \) ; \( \Delta G = -nFE \)

Under standard conditions: \( \Delta G° = -nFE° \)

Derivation of Nernst Equation

A thermodynamic equation relates reaction quotient and decrease in free energy:

\[ \Delta G = \Delta G° + RT \ln Q \quad \text{where Q is the reaction quotient} \]

The reaction quotient for the reaction \(M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M\) is: \( Q = \dfrac{[M]}{[M^{n+}]} \)

Substituting for ΔG, ΔG° and Q, we get:

\[ -nFE = -nFE° + RT\ln\left(\frac{[M]}{[M^{n+}]}\right) \]

Since [M] = 1 for pure substances:

\[ E = E° + \frac{RT}{nF}\ln([M^{n+}]) \]

At 298 K:

\[ \boxed{E = E° + \frac{0.0591}{n}\log([M^{n+}])} \]

Nernst Equation for a Cell Reaction

For the cell reaction: \( aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD \)

\[ Q = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b} \]
\[ E = E° - \frac{0.0591}{n}\log\left(\frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}\right) \]

where n = no. of electrons transferred, E°cell = standard emf of the cell.

Types of Electrodes

  • In order to form a cell, 2 half cells or 2 electrodes are required.
  • Various types of electrodes are available which are constructed based on the application.

1. Metal–Metal Ion Electrode

▼
  • Metal in contact with a solution of its own ions.
  • e.g., Zn/Zn²⁺, Cu/Cu²⁺, Ag/Ag⁺
  • Reaction: \( M^{n+} + ne^- \rightleftharpoons M \)

Nernst equation:

▼

▼
  • An inert metal such as platinum immersed in a solution containing an appropriate oxidized and reduced form of redox system.

Secondary Reference Electrode: Calomel Electrode

  • Most widely used reference electrode.
  • Metal-insoluble salt–ion electrode: Hg/Hg₂Cl₂(s)/Cl⁻

Construction

  • A Pt wire dipped in Hg provides electrical contact.
  • Tube is fitted with a side tube to fill KCl solution of known concentration and another side tube which connects to the salt bridge.

Working

\[ E = E° - 0.0591\log[Cl^-] \]

  • Electrode potential depends on chloride ion concentration.

Concentration Cells

Definition

An electrochemical cell in which identical electrodes are in contact with a solution of identical species but of different concentration.

In this cell 2 copper electrodes are immersed in copper sulphate solutions of concentration c₁ & c₂, such that c₂ > c₁.

  • An electrolyte has spontaneous tendency to diffuse from a solution of higher concentration to a solution of lower concentration which is the driving force for development of potential.
  • Oxidation takes place at anode and reduction takes place at cathode.
  • e.g., Cu/Cu²⁺(c₁)//Cu²⁺(c₂)/Cu

Reactions and Cell Potential

At anode: Cu → Cu²⁺(c₁) + 2e⁻

At cathode: Cu²⁺(c₂) + 2e⁻ → Cu

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\frac{c_2}{c_1} \]

At 298 K:

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{0.0591}{n}\log\frac{c_2}{c_1} \]
  • The emf of the cell is positive only if c₂ > c₁ (i.e., conc. of metal ion at cathode > conc. of metal ion at anode).
  • When c₂ = c₁, the emf of the cell becomes zero.
  • During working of the cell, concentration of ions increases at anode and decreases at cathode.
  • The cell can operate only as long as the concentration terms are different.

e.g., Cu/Cu²⁺(c₁)//Cu²⁺(c₂)/Cu

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\frac{c_2}{c_1} \]

Consists of two identical electrodes of different activity dipped in the same solution of electrolyte.

\[ E_{cell} = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\frac{Na\text{-}Hg(c_1)}{Na\text{-}Hg(c_2)} \]

At anode: Na-Hg(c₁) → Na⁺ + e⁻

  • Selectively respond to a specific ion in a mixture.
  • Potential developed is a function of concentration of that ion.
  • Have a membrane which is capable of exchanging the specific ion with solution with which it is in contact.
  • Membrane electrodes, e.g., glass electrode.

Glass Electrode

  • Ion-selective electrode.
  • Responds to Hydrogen ion.
  • pH sensitive; can be used to determine pH of a solution.
  • Consists of a glass membrane which is capable of exchanging H⁺ ions.

Construction

  • Glass tube, the end of which is a bulb of very thin glass membrane.
  • Glass bulb is made up of special type of glass, CORNING 015.
  • The glass bulb is filled with solution of known pH which is the reference solution.
  • A silver–silver chloride electrode is dipped inside the reference solution — serves as internal reference electrode and also provides external electrical contact.
  • The electrode is immersed in a solution containing H⁺ which is the analyte.
  • Notation: Ag/AgCl/HCl/glass

Working

The inner and outer surfaces of the glass membrane can exchange H⁺ ions with the solution they are in contact with.

\[ H^+_{solution} + Na^+Gl^- \rightleftharpoons Na^+_{solution} + H^+Gl^- \]
  • The hydrated glass membrane brings about ion exchange reaction between singly charged cations in the interstices of glass lattice and protons from the solution.
  • A potential is developed, which is a function of H⁺ of the solution.

Electrode Potential of a Glass Electrode

Boundary potential is:

\[ E_b = \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log\frac{C_1}{C_2} \]

Since concentration of reference solution, C₂ is constant:

\[ E_b = L' + \frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log C_1 \quad \text{where} \quad L' = -\frac{2.303RT}{nF}\log C_2 \]

At 298 K (since for H⁺, n = 1):

\[ E_b = L' + 0.0591\log[H^+] \] \[ \boxed{E_b = L' - 0.0591\text{pH}} \]

Components of Glass Electrode Potential

The glass electrode potential has 3 components:

  1. The boundary potential
  2. The potential of internal reference electrode
  3. Asymmetric potential — arises due to difference in responses of inner and outer surfaces of the glass bulb, due to differing conditions of stress on two glass surfaces.
\[ E_G = E_b + E_{ref} + E_{asymmetric} \] \[ = L' + \frac{0.0591}{n}\log[H^+] + E_{ref} + E_{asymmetric} \] \[ = E°_G + 0.0591\log[H^+] \quad \text{where } E°_G = L' + E_{ref} + E_{asymmetric} \] \[ \boxed{E_G = E°_G - 0.0591\text{pH}} \]

Determination of pH using Glass Electrode

  • Glass electrode is combined with an external reference electrode.
\[ Hg/Hg_2Cl_2/Cl^-//\text{analyte solution}/glass/0.1N\text{ HCl}/AgCl/Ag \]
  • The emf of the cell is determined potentiometrically.
\[ E_{cell} = E_G - E_{calomel} \] \[ E_G = E°_G - 0.0591\text{pH} \] \[ E_{cell} = E°_G - 0.0591\text{pH} - E_{calomel} \] \[ \boxed{\text{pH} = \frac{E°_G - E_{calomel} - E_{cell}}{0.0591}} \]
  • To evaluate E°G, the glass electrode is dipped in a solution of known pH (buffer solution) and combined with calomel electrode, the emf of the cell is measured from which E°G can be evaluated.

Applications of Glass Electrode

Used extensively in chemical, industrial, agricultural and biological labs.

Advantages of Glass Electrode

  • Can be used in oxidizing and reducing environments and metal ions.
  • Does not get poisoned.
  • Can be used for very small volumes.
  • Accurate results can be obtained between pH 1 to 9 by ordinary electrodes. However by using special glass electrodes pH 1 to 14 can be measured with accuracy.
  • Simple to operate and can be used with portable instruments.

Disadvantages of Glass Electrode

  • Because of high resistance of glass, a simple potentiometer cannot be used. It requires sensitive potentiometer for emf measurements.
  • Glass membrane is very delicate, hence has to be handled carefully.
  • At very high pH levels usually over a pH of 9, Alkaline error is observed. When the Sodium ion level is relatively high, some of the H⁺ ions in the gel layer around the sensitive electrode membrane are replaced by Na⁺ ions. The electrode may eventually respond to Na⁺ instead of H⁺ ions, giving a false lower pH value than the actual value.

Corrosion Chemistry

Types of corrosion, electrochemical theory, factors affecting corrosion, protective coatings, inhibitors, and cathodic protection.

Corrosion

Definition

The destruction or deterioration & consequent loss of metal through direct chemical or electrochemical attack by the environment.

  • Most metals except very noble ones, corrode on contact with water (and moisture in the air), acids, bases, salts, oils, aggressive metal polishes, and other solid and liquid chemicals.
  • It is a spontaneous process.
  • Causes indirect losses.

Examples of Corrosion Products

  • Rusting of iron — a reddish brown scale formation on iron and steel objects; due to formation of hydrated ferric oxide (Fe₂O₃.xH₂O).
  • Scaling of Copper — green scales formed on copper articles; due to formation of basic cupric carbonate (CuCO₃ + Cu(OH)₂).

Why Do Metals Undergo Corrosion?

  • Most metals occur in nature in the form of their compounds such as oxides, sulfides, carbonates, chlorides.
  • Metals are extracted from these ores by reduction which is an endothermic process, energy being supplied in the form of heat or electrical energy.
  • Pure metals are relatively at a higher energy state compared to their corresponding ores, and they have a natural tendency to revert back to their combined state.
[Mineral-Metal Cycle] Mineral (metal ore) → Extraction (Endothermic process) → Metal → Corrosion (Spontaneous process) → back to Mineral (metal ore). This illustrates why corrosion is thermodynamically spontaneous.

Types of Corrosion

Dry Corrosion

  • Direct chemical attack due to affinity of the metal to certain gases.
  • In the absence of the electrolytic medium.
  • Caused by gases: O₂, F₂, H₂S, NH₃

Wet Corrosion

  • Electrochemical attack on the metal.
  • In the presence of moisture or conducting medium.

Electrochemical Theory of Corrosion

  • A large number of tiny galvanic cells are formed due to the formation of separate anodic and cathodic regions on the metal surface.
  • At the anodic region, metal undergoes oxidation and gets converted into its ions, liberating electrons and the metal undergoes corrosion.
  • At the cathodic region, reduction takes place but since the metal cannot be reduced further, metal atoms are unaffected by corrosion.
  • The electrons liberated at the anodic region migrate to the cathodic region constituting a small corrosion current.
  • The metal ions formed at the anode and some anions formed at the cathode diffuse towards each other through the conducting medium and form a corrosion product somewhere between anode and cathode.
  • Corrosion of metal continues as long as both anodic and cathodic reactions take place simultaneously.
Corrosion Reactions

At anode: \( M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \)

At cathode: \( A + ne^- \rightarrow A^{n-} \)

Cathodic Reactions

Liberation of Hydrogen

  • In acidic medium: \( 2H^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2\uparrow \)
  • In neutral or alkaline medium: \( 2H_2O + 2e^- \rightarrow 2OH^- + H_2\uparrow \)

Absorption of Oxygen

  • In acidic medium: \( 4H^+ + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 2H_2O \)
  • In neutral or alkaline medium: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Corrosion of Iron (Rusting)

Reactions During Rusting

Anode: \( Fe \rightarrow Fe^{2+} + 2e^- \)

Cathode: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Fe²⁺ and OH⁻ ions move towards one another and Fe(OH)₂ is formed somewhere between anode and cathode:

\( 2Fe^{2+} + 4OH^- \rightarrow 2Fe(OH)_2 \)

Formation of Yellow Rust

In an oxidizing environment, Fe(OH)₂ is oxidized to hydrated ferric oxide:

\[ 4Fe(OH)_2 + O_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2[Fe_2O_3.3H_2O] \quad \text{(Yellow rust)} \]

Formation of Black Rust

In the presence of limited oxygen, 4Fe(OH)₂ is converted into magnetic oxide of iron (Fe₃O₄):

\[ 3Fe(OH)_2 + \frac{1}{2}O_2 \rightarrow Fe_3O_4.3H_2O \quad \text{(Black rust)} \]

Differential Metal Corrosion (Galvanic Corrosion)

  • Also called galvanic corrosion.
  • When two dissimilar metals are in contact with each other and are exposed to a corrosive environment.
  • The two metals differ in their electrode potentials. The metal with lower electrode potential acts as anode and the other metal with higher electrode potential acts as cathode.
  • The anodic metal undergoes oxidation and gets corroded. The cathodic metal remains unaffected.
  • The driving force for corrosion is the difference in electrode potentials of the two metals.
Example

Iron in contact with copper: Standard electrode potential of Fe (−0.44 V) vs. Cu (+0.34 V). Iron (lower potential) acts as anode and gets corroded; copper (higher potential) acts as cathode and remains protected.

Reactions

At anode: \( M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \)

At cathode: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Differential Aeration Corrosion

  • When a metal is exposed to different concentrations of air (O₂), part of the metal exposed to lower concentration of O₂ becomes anodic and undergoes corrosion.
  • Other part of the metal exposed to higher concentration of oxygen becomes cathodic and remains unaffected.
  • The difference in oxygen concentration produces a potential difference and causes corrosion current to flow.

At anode: \( M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \)

At cathode: \( 2H_2O + O_2 + 4e^- \rightarrow 4OH^- \)

Waterline Corrosion

  • A special case of differential aeration corrosion.
  • Observed when metal is half immersed in water.
  • The part immersed in water is exposed to less O₂, hence acts as anode and gets corroded.
  • The part not in water is exposed to more O₂ and acts as cathode; remains unaffected by corrosion.

Pitting Corrosion

  • A localized and accelerated corrosion.
  • Results in formation of pits or pin holes, around which the metal is relatively unattacked.
  • One of the most destructive forms of corrosion.
  • Characterized by small anodic area and large cathodic area resulting in accelerated corrosion at the anodic area.

Case I: Dust Particles or Oil Drops on Metal Surface

  • When dust particles or oil drops get deposited over the metal surface.
  • The portion of the metal covered by dust which is less aerated becomes anodic.
  • The adjacent area of the metal which is exposed to higher concentration of O₂ becomes cathodic.
  • A small anodic region surrounded by a large cathodic region is formed.
  • The demand for electrons is high from the cathode.
  • Metal underneath the surface of dust particle being anode undergoes accelerated corrosion forming a deep and narrow pit.

Case II: Breakdown of the Protective Film

  • The breakdown of the protective film on a metal surface.
  • Peeling off of a small tin coating on iron gives rise to a small anodic area (Fe) and large cathodic area (Sn).

Reactions:

At anode: Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻

At cathode: 2H₂O + O₂ + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻

Stress Corrosion (Stress Corrosion Cracking, SCC)

  • A type of corrosion that occurs when some part of the metallic material is under stress and exposed to specific corrosive environment.
  • During the manufacture or fabrication of articles, when the metals are subjected to mechanical operations such as pressing, hammering, rolling, bending, quenching, welding and riveting.
  • The stress can be external stress acting on the metal during service conditions or residual stress or both.
  • Due to stress, a crack is initiated.
  • The metal atoms under stress are always at higher energy levels, therefore become more reactive than the stress free part.
  • A corrosion cell is formed with the stressed part acting as anode and the stress free part acting as cathode.

Conditions for Stress Corrosion

  • Tensile stress
  • Specific corrosive environment

Under specific corrosive environment, the stressed part undergoes corrosion. The crack deepens and results in the breakdown of the structure.

Specific Corrosive Environments

  • Brass — ammoniacal solution or ammonia vapours.
  • Steel — NaOH and chloride ions.

Causes serious damage — air crashes, bridge collapses, boiler explosions.

Factors Affecting Corrosion

1. Nature of the Metal

▼
  • Metals with lower electrode potential values are more reactive than the metals with higher electrode potential values.
  • More reactive metals are more susceptible to corrosion.
  • Metals like K, Na, Mg, Zn etc., with low electrode potential values are highly susceptible for corrosion.
  • Noble metals such as silver, gold, platinum etc., with higher potential values are less susceptible for corrosion.
  • The Electrochemical series gives an idea of the order in which metals undergo corrosion.
  • But metals like Chromium, Aluminum, Titanium etc. are exceptions as they are passive.
  • Galvanic series is required which arranges metals and alloys in the order of their tendency to undergo corrosion in a particular environment.

2. Nature of the Corrosion Product

▼
  • For some metals like Chromium, Aluminum etc., the corrosion product formed on the surface of the metal acts as a protective film.
  • If the corrosion product deposited is insoluble, stable, uniform, and non-porous, it acts as a protective film preventing further corrosion of metal.
  • A thin, invisible, impervious, continuous film formed on the surface acts as a barrier between the fresh metal surface and the corrosion environment.
  • On the other hand, metals like iron, zinc, magnesium, etc., do not form protective film on the surface of the metal.
  • If the corrosion product is soluble, unstable, non-uniform, and porous, the corrosion continues unabated.
  • In such cases, the fresh metal surface is continuously exposed to the corrosion environment and corrosion takes place continuously.
  • Steel gets corroded but stainless steel does not.

3. Hydrogen Overvoltage

▼
Definition

Overvoltage is defined as the extra voltage over the theoretical voltage required for an electrode reaction to take place. Applicable especially for evolution of gases. Depends on the surface; if overvoltage of a gas is high on a surface, liberation of the gas on that surface will be less.

  • A metal with low hydrogen overvoltage on its surface is more susceptible to corrosion.
  • Hydrogen evolution is a cathodic reaction.
  • If cathodic reaction is hydrogen evolution type, hydrogen gas is liberated easily and thus the cathodic reaction rate is faster.
  • This will make anodic reaction also fast, thereby promoting overall corrosion reaction.
  • When the hydrogen overvoltage on the metal surface is high, cathodic reaction is slower and the corrosion of the metal also becomes slower.

4. Temperature

▼
  • The rate of a chemical reaction, in general, increases with rise in temperature.
  • For corrosion process (redox reaction), the rate of corrosion increases as the temperature increases due to:
    • Increase in rate of reaction.
    • Increase in conductance of the medium — ions migrate fast at higher temperature.
    • Decrease in the polarization effects at the anodic and cathodic sites.
    • Increase in solubility of corrosion product in the medium.
    • Breakdown in the protective film that might exist on the surface.
  • However, if corrosion is due to dissolved gases like O₂, CO₂, etc., the rate of corrosion decreases with increase in temperature as the solubility of these gases in the medium decreases.

5. pH

▼
  • In general, lower the pH of the corrosion medium, higher is the corrosion rate.
  • At low pH, concentration of H⁺ ions is high; H⁺ ions help for cathodic reaction facilitating anodic oxidation of the metal: \( 2H^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2 \)
  • Corrosion product becomes more soluble in acidic medium.
  • However, some metals like Al undergo fast corrosion in highly alkaline solution as their corrosion product is soluble in alkaline medium.

6. Polarization at Anodic and Cathodic Region

▼

Polarisation is the drift in electrode potential of an electrode due to change in concentration of species.

Anodic Polarisation

  • At anodic area, metal is oxidized, liberating metal ions which move towards cathodic area.
  • Metal ions liberated at anode cannot move freely towards cathodic area because of opposing factors like lower conductivity of the medium.
  • Metal ions get concentrated at anodic area and this decreases tendency of metal to undergo oxidation; corrosion rate falls.
  • Anodic polarisation is due to accumulation of ions in the anodic region.

Cathodic Polarisation

  • Due to retarded movement of cathodic reactant (O₂, H⁺) towards the cathodic surface or retarded removal of products formed in cathodic region.
  • Hence, the rate of cathodic reaction decreases. A retarded cathodic reaction results in retardation of anodic reaction also which slows down the corrosion process.
  • Cathodic polarisation is due to depletion of ions in the cathodic region.

Polarisation Effect Can Be Reduced By

  • Use of depolarisers — e.g., Anodic polarisers: complexing agents; Cathodic polarisers: cupric ions.
  • Increase in temperature.
  • Stirring the solution.

Protective Coatings

Metallic Coating

Corrosion of base metal is prevented by coating a layer of another metal over it. Metal coated may be anodic or cathodic to the base metal.

Anodic Metal Coating

  • Coating of a layer of metal which is anodic to base metal.
  • Coating metal is more active with respect to the base metal.
  • Even if the base metal is not completely covered, it will not undergo pitting corrosion.
  • e.g., Coating of Zn or Mg on iron.

Cathodic Metal Coating

  • Coating of a layer of metal which is cathodic to base metal.
  • Coating metal is less active with respect to the base metal.
  • Base metal should be completely covered and there should not be any gaps in the coating.
  • If some part of the base metal is left uncovered, then it undergoes more intense pitting corrosion due to formation of small anodic and large cathodic area.
  • e.g., Coating of Sn or Ni metal over iron.

Inorganic Coating (Chemical Conversion Coating)

  • Surface of the base metal is converted into a protective coating through appropriate chemical modification.
  • The protective film (corrosion product) is an inherent part of the metal.
  • Two methods: Anodising and Phosphating.

Organic Coating

  • Coating of metal surfaces with organic materials, particularly paints and lacquers is the most widely used anti-corrosion coating.
  • The coatings serve to keep out air and moisture from the metal surface or serve as a barrier between the metal surface and the corrosion environment.

Galvanizing

Coating a layer of zinc on iron is called galvanization.

Process

  1. Iron sheet is passed through organic solvent or caustic solution to remove oil or grease present on it.
  2. It is washed with dil. H₂SO₄ to remove any rust, scale or dust present on the surface (pickling).
  3. It is treated with a mixture of aqueous solution of ZnCl₂ and NH₄Cl which acts as flux which prevents oxidation and then dried.
  4. Finally it is dipped in molten zinc at 425–430°C.
  5. Excess zinc present on iron sheet is removed by passing through hot rollers.

Application

Galvanization of iron is carried out to produce roofing sheets, fencing wire, buckets, bolts, nuts, pipes etc.

Advantage

Even if the Zn coating peels off or there are gaps at some places, the base metal (Fe) does not get corroded because the base metal acts as cathode.

Disadvantage

Galvanized articles are not used for preparing and storing food because zinc dissolves in dilute acids producing toxic zinc compounds.

Anodizing

Induced oxidation in an oxidising environment by making the article anode and passing current. Oxidation of outer layer of metal to its metal oxide which covers the metal surface and acts as protective layer. Done for non-ferrous metals like Al, Cr, Ti.

Anodizing of Aluminum — Process

  1. Article is cleaned, degreased, and polished and taken as anode.
  2. It is immersed in an electrolyte consisting of 5–10% chromic acid, sulfuric acid, phosphoric acid, oxalic acid or their mixtures maintained at around 40°C.
  3. Lead or steel is taken as cathode.
  4. Voltage above 40 V is applied.
  5. Outer layer of Al is oxidised to Al₂O₃:
\[ 2Al + 3H_2O \rightarrow Al_2O_3 + 3H_2 \]
  • Al₂O₃ formed on the surface is slightly porous in nature.
  • The porous layer may be made compact by sealing which is done by immersing the anodised article in boiling water or steam.
  • Al₂O₃ is converted into Al₂O₃.H₂O which occupies higher volume; the pores are sealed.

Colour Finishes

Anodized articles can be produced in good colour finishes by:

  • Adsorbing an organic dye after anodising and then sealing.
  • Depositing a metal like Ni, Co etc., into the pores.

Applications

Anodized Al is used as an attractive, highly durable, corrosion resistant material in exteriors for roofs, walls, buildings and also in window frames, office partitions, tiffin carriers, etc.

Corrosion Inhibitors

Definition

Chemical substances which are added in small quantities to the corrosive environment to decrease the rate of corrosion.

  • Inhibitors slow down or inhibit the anodic or cathodic reaction and control corrosion.
  • They can be used only in confined environment.
  • The action of an inhibitor depends on the nature of the metal to be protected as well as corrosive environment.

Types: Anodic Inhibitors and Cathodic Inhibitors

Anodic Inhibitors

▼

Anode reaction: \( M \rightarrow M^{n+} + ne^- \)

  • If the formation of Mn+ is prevented, the corrosion process is retarded.
  • Achieved by the addition of large anions such as chromate (CrO₄²⁻), tungstate (WO₄²⁻), etc.
  • These ions combine with Mn+ and form a precipitate which covers the surface of the anode.
  • Anodic inhibitors are effective only when sufficient amount of the inhibitor is added.
  • If insufficient quantity is added, then a part of the anodic region is exposed to the environment — formation of small anodic area results in intense corrosion.
  • Salts like Na₂CrO₄, Na₂WO₄.

Cathodic Inhibitors

▼

Cathodic reactions involve: liberation of hydrogen and absorption of oxygen. Inhibiting any of these reactions will slow down the cathodic reaction which in turn slows down the anodic reaction; corrosion is inhibited.

Inhibition of Hydrogen Liberation

By preventing the diffusion of H⁺ ions to the cathode:

  • By addition of certain organic compounds which contain nitrogen or sulfur.
  • Aliphatic amines, urea, thiourea, mercaptans, heterocyclic compounds are widely used as cathodic inhibitors.
  • They get adsorbed on the cathodic region forming a protective film, preventing the H⁺ ions from coming in contact with the cathodic metal surface.

By increasing the hydrogen overvoltage on the metal surface:

  • By the addition of oxides of arsenic, antimony (As₂O₃, Sb₂O₃) or salts like sodium meta-arsenite (NaAsO₂).
  • They deposit on the cathode region. The hydrogen overvoltage on these metals is very high; liberation of H₂ is reduced.

Inhibition of Oxygen Absorption

By removing the oxygen from the corrosive media:

  • By adding Oxygen scavengers like hydrazine: \( N_2H_4 + O_2 \rightarrow N_2 + 2H_2O \)
  • By adding reducing agents like sodium sulfite: \( 2Na_2SO_3 + O_2 \rightarrow 2Na_2SO_4 \)

By decreasing the diffusion rate of oxygen to cathode:

  • By adding salts with large cations such as ZnSO₄, MgSO₄, NiSO₄ etc.
  • The cations (Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺) migrate towards the cathode surface and react with the hydroxyl ions formed at the cathode: \( Zn^{2+} + 2OH^- \rightarrow Zn(OH)_2 \)
  • The hydroxides get deposited on the cathodic sites.
  • The protective film impermeable to oxygen prevents its diffusion to the cathodic region.

Limitations of Corrosion Inhibitors

  • They contaminate the environment.
  • Many of the inhibitors are toxic, cannot be used in systems which come in contact with humans.
  • Can be used only in closed systems in which corrosive environment is either contained or re-circulated.

Cathodic Protection

Principle

By converting the metal structure completely into cathode and no part of it is allowed to act as anode. Cathode does not undergo corrosion, so the structure is protected.

Methods of Cathodic Protection

  1. Sacrificial Anode Method
  2. Impressed Current Method

1. Sacrificial Anode Method

▼
  • Protected metal structure is converted into a cathode by connecting it to a more active metal.
  • This active metal (example: zinc, magnesium) acts as an auxiliary anode.
  • These metals being more active, act as anode and undergo preferential corrosion, protecting the metal structure.
  • Since the anodic metals are sacrificed to protect the metal structure, the method is known as sacrificial anode method.
  • Exhausted anodes have to be replaced periodically.

Advantages

  • The method is simple.
  • Low installation cost.
  • Does not require power supply.

Disadvantages

  • Involves recurring expenditure for replacement of consumed anodes.

2. Impressed Current Method

▼
  • By applying a direct current.
  • The protected metal is made cathodic by connecting it to the cathode of the external source of current.
  • Anode of the external source is usually connected to an inert electrode like graphite; Platinum, silicon, iron are also used as anodes.
  • A backfill of coke, bentonite is used to improve efficiency of the anode (to reduce electrical resistivity — provides lower anode-to-earth resistance and greater current outputs in cases where the surrounding soil is of high resistivity).
  • The metal structure being cathode does not undergo corrosion.
  • Anode being inert remains unaffected.

Advantages

  • One installation can protect large area of metal.

Limitations

  • Rather expensive, since it needs high current for safe protection of structure.
  • If the impressed current is not uniform on the entire surface of the protected structure, localized corrosion takes place on the protected metal.
  • If hydrogen liberation is the cathodic reaction on the metal surface, then the protected metal may suffer from hydrogen embrittlement.
Hydrogen Embrittlement

H₂ molecule being small enters the metal lattice and when the pressure increases, it causes blisters and makes the metal brittle.
