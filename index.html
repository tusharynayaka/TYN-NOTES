1. An Introduction to Semiconductor

Semiconductor: A special class of elements having a conductivity between that of a good conductor and that of an insulator.

The three semiconductors used most frequently in the construction of electronic devices are Ge (Germanium), Si (Silicon), and GaAs (Gallium Arsenide).

1.1 Atomic Structure

ElementAtomic NumberElectronic Configuration
Silicon (Si)141s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p²
Germanium (Ge)321s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p²

Both silicon and germanium have four valence electrons in their outermost shell. Silicon atoms form a crystal lattice by sharing these electrons with neighboring atoms (covalent bonding).

1.2 Energy Bands

Electrons in atoms occupy discrete energy levels. When atoms are brought together in a solid, these discrete levels broaden into energy bands. The key bands are:

  • Valence Band: The outermost shell occupied by valence electrons (bound to atoms).
  • Conduction Band: The band where electrons are free to move and establish conduction.
  • Energy Gap (Eg): The gap between valence and conduction bands.

1.3 Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material that has been carefully refined to reduce the number of impurities to a very low level — essentially as pure as can be made available through modern technology.
Extrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material that has been subjected to the doping process is called an extrinsic material.

In pure (intrinsic) silicon, each Si atom shares its 4 valence electrons with 4 neighbors, forming covalent bonds. This creates a complete valence shell for each atom, leaving no free carriers at 0 K.

1.4 N-Type Semiconductor

An n-type material is created by introducing impurity elements that have five valence electrons (pentavalent), such as:

  • Antimony (Sb)
  • Arsenic (As)
  • Phosphorus (P)

When a pentavalent atom replaces a silicon atom in the lattice, four of its five valence electrons form covalent bonds with neighboring Si atoms. The fifth electron is free to move through the lattice, contributing to electrical conduction.

Donor Atoms: Diffused impurities with five valence electrons are called donor atoms because they "donate" a free electron to the material.
In n-type material:
  • Majority carriers: Electrons (negative)
  • Minority carriers: Holes (positive)
  • Donor ions (positive) are fixed in the lattice

1.5 P-Type Semiconductor

The p-type material is formed by doping a pure germanium or silicon crystal with impurity atoms having three valence electrons (trivalent). The elements most frequently used are:

  • Boron (B)
  • Gallium (Ga)
  • Indium (In)

When a trivalent atom is placed in the silicon lattice, it can only form three covalent bonds. The fourth bond position has a void (hole) — a missing electron that acts as a positive charge carrier.

Acceptor Atoms: The diffused impurities with three valence electrons are called acceptor atoms because they "accept" an electron from the lattice, creating a hole.
In p-type material:
  • Majority carriers: Holes (positive)
  • Minority carriers: Electrons (negative)
  • Acceptor ions (negative) are fixed in the lattice

2. An Introduction to the Semiconductor Diode

Diode: A two-electrode (two-terminal) device. We can construct our first solid-state electronic device — the semiconductor diode — by sandwiching p-type and n-type material together to form a depletion region between the two. It is a fabrication of one material with a majority carrier of electrons joined to one with a majority carrier of holes.

2.1 Biasing of Diodes

  • Forward Bias: A material type connected to the same polarity terminal of the voltage source (p-type to positive, n-type to negative).
  • Reverse Bias: A material type connected to the opposite polarity terminal of the voltage source (p-type to negative, n-type to positive).

2.2 No-Bias Condition

At the instant the two materials are "joined," the electrons and the holes in the region of the junction will combine, resulting in a lack of free carriers in the region near the junction.

The only particles remaining in this region are the positive and negative ions after the free carriers have been absorbed.

Depletion Region: This region of uncovered positive and negative ions is called the depletion region due to the "depletion" of free carriers in the region.
Key fact: In the absence of an applied bias across a semiconductor diode, the net flow of charge in one direction is zero. (\( I_D = 0 \text{ mA}, \; V_D = 0 \text{ V} \))

2.3 Forward-Bias Condition

A forward-bias or "on" condition is established by applying the positive potential to the p-type material and the negative potential to the n-type material.

The application of a forward-bias potential \(V_D\) will "pressure" electrons in the n-type material and holes in the p-type material to recombine with the ions near the boundary and reduce the width of the depletion region.

The current that exists under forward-bias conditions is called the forward-bias current and is represented by \(I_D\): \[ I_D = I_{\text{majority}} - I_s \]

2.4 Reverse-Bias Condition

When a P-N junction is reverse-biased, the number of uncovered positive ions in the depletion region of the n-type material will increase due to the large number of free electrons drawn to the positive potential of the applied voltage. For similar reasons, the number of uncovered negative ions will increase in the p-type material.

The net effect is a widening of the depletion region. This widening will establish too great a barrier for the majority carriers to overcome, effectively reducing the majority carrier flow to zero.

The current that exists under reverse-bias conditions is called the reverse saturation current and is represented by \(I_s\).

3. Semiconductor Diode's Characteristics & Parameters

From the characteristics curve of a semiconductor diode, the following regions and parameters are observed:

3.1 Forward Bias Parameters

  • Knee Voltage (VF): The forward voltage at which the diode begins to conduct significantly.
    • Silicon (Si): ~0.7 V
    • Germanium (Ge): ~0.3 V
  • Forward Current Rating: Maximum allowable forward current.
  • Maximum Power Dissipation: P = VD × ID

3.2 Reverse Bias Parameters

  • Reverse Saturation Current (Is): Very small current due to minority carriers.
  • Breakdown Voltage (VBR): Reverse voltage at which large reverse current flows.
  • Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) Rating: Maximum reverse voltage the diode can withstand.

4. Diode's Current Expression — Shockley's Current Equation

Shockley's Equation — also called the diode equation — helps us determine the current at a given temperature knowing the diode voltage and saturation current.

\[ I_D = I_S \left( e^{\frac{qV_D}{nkT}} - 1 \right) \]

4.1 Variables and Constants

SymbolMeaningValue / Unit
\(I_D\)Diode currentAmperes (A)
\(V_D\)Diode voltageVolts (V)
\(I_S\)Reverse saturation currentAmperes (A)
\(q\)Charge of electron1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
\(n\)Ideality factor1 (Si, Ge — indirect); 2 (GaAs, InP — direct)
\(k\)Boltzmann's constant1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K
\(T\)TemperatureKelvin (K)
\(V_T = kT/q\)Thermal voltage≈ 25.9 mV at 300 K (room temp)

4.2 Special Cases

  • When \(V_D\) is negative (Reverse bias):
    \[ I_D \approx -I_S \]
  • When \(V_D\) is positive (Forward bias):
    \[ I_D \approx I_S \, e^{\frac{qV_D}{nkT}} \]

4.3 Thermal Voltage

\[ V_T = \frac{kT}{q} = \frac{T}{11600} \text{ (V)} \]

At room temperature (T = 300 K): \(V_T \approx 25.9 \text{ mV} \approx 26 \text{ mV}\)

5. Numericals on Diode's Current Equations

Example 1.1

Q: At a temperature of 27°C (common temperature for components in an enclosed operating system), determine the thermal voltage \(V_T\).

Solution:
\[ T = 273 + °C = 273 + 27 = 300 \text{ K} \] \[ V_T = \frac{kT_K}{q} = \frac{(1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K})(300 \text{ K})}{1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}} \] \[ = 25.875 \text{ mV} \approx 26 \text{ mV} \]
Q. No 1

Q: Calculate the thermal voltage when the temperature is 25°C.

Solution:

Thermal voltage \(V_T = kT/q\). This can be reduced to \(V_T = T/11600\) because \(q/k = 11600\).

\[ V_T = \frac{298}{11600} = 0.0257 \text{ V} = 25.7 \text{ mV} \]
Q. No 2

Q: Calculate the forward bias current of a Si diode when forward bias voltage of 0.4 V is applied, the reverse saturation current is 1.17×10⁻⁹ A and the thermal voltage is 25.2 mV.

Solution:

\(I_D = I_s(e^{V_D/\eta V_T} - 1)\), where \(I_s\) = reverse saturation current, \(\eta\) = ideality factor, \(V_T\) = thermal voltage, \(V_D\) = applied voltage.

Since ideality factor is not mentioned, take \(\eta = 1\).

Given: \(I_s = 1.17 \times 10^{-9} \text{ A}\), \(V_T = 0.0252 \text{ V}\), \(\eta = 1\), \(V_D = 0.4 \text{ V}\)

\[ I_D = 1.17 \times 10^{-9} \times (e^{0.4/0.0252} - 1) = 9.156 \text{ mA} \]
Q. No 3

Q: Given a diode current of 8 mA and η = 1, find the reverse saturation current (Is) if the applied voltage is 0.5 V and the room temperature is 25°C.

Solution:

Given: \(I_D = 8 \text{ mA}\), \(\eta = 1\), \(V_D = 0.5 \text{ V}\), \(T = 25°C = 298 \text{ K}\)

\[ V_T = \frac{kT_K}{q} = \frac{(1.38 \times 10^{-23})(298)}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 25.70 \text{ mV} \] \[ 8 \text{ mA} = I_s(e^{(0.5)/(1)(25.70 \text{ mV})} - 1) = I_s(2.8 \times 10^8) \] \[ I_s = \frac{8 \text{ mA}}{2.8 \times 10^8} = 28.57 \text{ pA} \]
Q. No 4

Q: Calculate the applied voltage \(V_D\), if diode current is 6 mA, Thermal Voltage is 26 mV, Ideality factor is 1, and Reverse saturation current is 1 nA.

Solution:

Given: \(I_D = 6 \text{ mA}\), \(V_T = 26 \text{ mV}\), \(\eta = 1\), \(I_S = 1 \text{ nA}\)

\[ I_D = I_s(e^{V_D/\eta V_T} - 1) \] \[ 6 \text{ mA} = 1 \text{ nA}(e^{V_D/(1)(26 \text{ mV})} - 1) \] \[ 6 \times 10^6 = e^{V_D/26 \text{ mV}} - 1 \] \[ e^{V_D/26 \text{ mV}} = 6 \times 10^6 + 1 \approx 6 \times 10^6 \] \[ \ln_e(e^{V_D/26 \text{ mV}}) = \ln_e(6 \times 10^6) \] \[ \frac{V_D}{26 \text{ mV}} = 15.61 \] \[ V_D = 15.61 \times 26 \text{ mV} \approx 0.41 \text{ V} \]
Q. No 5

Q: Consider a silicon diode with η = 1.2. Find change in voltage if the current changes from 0.1 mA to 10 mA.

Solution:

Equation for diode current: \(I = I_0 \times (e^{V/\eta V_T} - 1)\)

Given: \(\eta = 1.2\), \(I_2 = 10 \text{ mA}\), \(I_1 = 0.1 \text{ mA}\), \(V_T = 0.026 \text{ V}\)

\[ \Delta V = \eta V_T \ln\left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right) = 1.2 \times 0.026 \times \ln\left(10 \times \frac{10^{-3}}{0.1 \times 10^{-3}}\right) = 0.143 \text{ V} \]

7. Types of Diode Breakdown

7.1 Avalanche Breakdown

In the reverse bias condition, as the voltage increases, the free charge carriers obtain velocity and associated kinetic energy and release additional carriers through collision with other atomic structures.

Hence covalent bonds are broken and electron–hole pairs are generated. These charge carriers acquire energy from the applied potential and produce more and more free charge carriers. This cumulative process is called Avalanche multiplication or ionization process.

Avalanche breakdown is observed in diodes with Zener breakdown voltage Vz > 8 V.

7.2 Zener Breakdown

Zener breakdown occurs when both P and N type material are heavily doped in a semiconductor diode. Hence the depletion layer region is narrowed down.

When a small reverse bias voltage is applied across a diode, a very strong electric field is produced. Due to this electric field, covalent bonds break and produce a large number of free charge carriers.

Zener breakdown is observed in diodes with Zener breakdown voltage Vz of 5 to 8 V.

7.3 Comparison: Zener vs. Avalanche Breakdown

#Zener BreakdownAvalanche Breakdown
01 The process in which the electrons move across the barrier from the valence band of p-type material to the conduction band of n-type material is known as Zener breakdown. The process of applying high voltage and increasing the free electrons or electric current in semiconductors and insulating materials is called an avalanche breakdown.
02 This is observed in Zener diodes having a Zener breakdown voltage Vz of 5 to 8 volts. This is observed in Zener diode having a Zener breakdown voltage Vz greater than 8 volts.
03 The valence electrons are pulled into conduction due to the high electric field in the narrow depletion region. The valence electrons are pushed to conduction due to the energy imparted by accelerated electrons, which gain their velocity due to their collision with other atoms.
04 The increase in temperature decreases the breakdown voltage. The increase in temperature increases the breakdown voltage.
05 The VI characteristics of a Zener breakdown has a sharp curve. The VI characteristic curve of the avalanche breakdown is not as sharp as the Zener breakdown.
06 It occurs in diodes that are highly doped. It occurs in diodes that are lightly doped.

8. Temperature Effects on V-I Characteristics of a Diode

  • The effect of variation in temperature across a semiconductor diode is observed both in the forward as well as in reverse characteristics.
  • Rise in temperature generates more electron-hole pairs, thus conductivity increases and current increases.
  • PN junction diode parameters like reverse saturation current, bias current, reverse breakdown voltage, and barrier voltage are dependent on temperature.
  • Increase in the temperature increases carrier concentration. As a result:
    • Knee voltage decreases
    • Breakdown voltage increases
    • Reverse saturation current increases

8.1 Under Forward Bias

Barrier voltage is dependent on temperature — it decreases by 2.5 mV/°C rise in temperature for both Germanium and Silicon.

8.2 Under Reverse Bias

Reverse saturation current (IS) of diode increases with increase in temperature. The rise is 7%/°C for both Germanium & Silicon and approximately doubles for every 10°C rise in temperature. Reverse breakdown voltage (VR) also increases as the temperature increases.

8.3 Summary Rules

  1. The reverse saturation current Is will double in magnitude for every 10°C increase in temperature.
  2. The Knee voltage shifts to the left at a rate of 2.5 mV per degree centigrade increase in temperature.

9. Ideal and Practical Diode Characteristics

9.1 Ideal Diode

Ideal Diode: When an ideal diode is forward biased, it offers no resistance and acts like a closed switch. Likewise, the ideal diode under reverse bias offers infinite resistance and acts like an open switch.

Characteristics: \(V_d = 0\) V when forward biased, \(I_D = 0\) A when reverse biased.

9.2 Practical Diode

Practical Diode: A diode which is said to be forward biased, it starts conducting at the knee voltage (VK). Under reverse bias no current flows due to majority charges. Hence a practical diode is considered to be an open switch (minority charge current is ignored). For Si: \(V_K = 0.7 \text{ V}\); For Ge: \(V_K = 0.3 \text{ V}\)

10. Diode Resistances

An actual diode offers a very small resistance (not zero) when forward biased — called forward resistance. Whereas, it offers a very high resistance (not infinite) when reverse biased — called reverse resistance.

The type of applied voltage or signal defines the following resistance levels:

  1. DC or Static Resistance
  2. AC or Dynamic Resistance
  3. Average AC Resistance

10.1 DC or Static Resistance (RD)

The application of a DC voltage to a circuit containing a semiconductor diode will result in an operating point on the semiconductor diode characteristic curve. Finding the corresponding levels of VD and ID at a particular operating point gives the DC resistance.

\[ R_D = \frac{V_D}{I_D} \]
Typical DC resistance of a diode:
  • Forward bias: 10 Ω to 80 Ω
  • Reverse bias: 10 MΩ

10.2 AC or Dynamic Resistance (rd)

The application of a small AC voltage to a circuit containing a semiconductor diode offers AC resistance. A straight line drawn tangent to the curve through the Q-point will define a particular change in voltage and current, which determines the AC or dynamic resistance for this region of the diode characteristics.

\[ r_d = \frac{\Delta V_d}{\Delta I_d} = \frac{26 \text{ mV}}{I_D} \text{ (ohms)} \]

Including bulk resistance:

\[ r'_d = \frac{26 \text{ mV}}{I_D} + r_B \text{ (ohms)} \]

where \(r_B\) is bulk or body resistance.

Typically, the AC resistance of a diode in the active region will range from 1 Ω to 100 Ω.

10.3 Average AC Resistance (rav)

If the applied input signal is sufficiently large to produce a broad swing, the resistance determined by a straight line drawn between the two intersections established by the maximum and minimum values of input voltage is called the Average AC Resistance.

\[ r_{av} = \frac{\Delta V_d}{\Delta I_d}\bigg|_{\text{pt. to pt.}} \]

11. Diode Equivalent Circuits / Diode Approximations

Diode Approximation: A mathematical method used to approximate the nonlinear behavior of real diodes to enable calculations and circuit analysis.

There are three different approximations used to analyze diode circuits:

  1. Piecewise-Linear Equivalent Circuit
  2. Simplified Diode Characteristics
  3. Ideal Diode Characteristics

11.1 Piecewise-Linear Equivalent Circuit

  • Under forward-bias: The diode behaves as a practical voltage source with magnitude equal to the knee voltage, in series with the diode's average AC resistance. Formula: \(V_d = V_B + I_d \times R_B\) where \(V_B\) = Knee voltage.
  • Under reverse-bias: The diode is an open switch and no current flows through it.
  • Characteristics resemble closely to that of a practical diode.
Note: VB = Knee voltage

11.2 Simplified Diode Characteristics

  • Under forward-bias: The diode turns ON when a voltage of VK or VB is applied across it. It turns off if the applied voltage is less than VK. Thus, the diode is represented by a voltage of VK in series with a closed switch. Therefore, \(V_d = V_B\).
  • Under reverse bias: It is an open switch.
For Si: VB = 0.7 V; For Ge: VB = 0.3 V

11.3 Ideal Diode Characteristics

  • The diode is considered as a closed switch with zero voltage drop when forward-biased (\(V_d = 0\)).
  • It acts as an open switch with zero current when reverse biased.

13. Logical Operations

Logical Operation: It is an operation that acts on binary numbers to produce a result according to the Laws of Boolean Logic.
Logic Gate: A basic building block of digital circuits. A logic gate is considered as a device that has the ability to produce one output level with the combinations of input levels. Example: AND, OR and NOT functions.
  • Logic gates are implemented by using diodes, transistors, and by other several devices.
  • Hence logic gates can also be considered as electronic circuits.
  • The logic gates obtained by Diode are called Diode Resistor Logic (DRL).
  • Inputs and outputs of logic gates are in two levels which are termed as HIGH and LOW, or TRUE and FALSE, or ON and OFF, or simply 1 and 0.
Truth Table: For all logic gates, a table with all combinations is listed out. The combination of the input variables and the corresponding output is termed as "TRUTH TABLE". It explains how the logic circuit output responds to various combinations of logic levels at the inputs.

An AND gate may have two or more inputs but produces only one output. AND gate produces an output of logic 1 state when all of its inputs are in logic 1 state, and produces an output of logic 0 state if any of its inputs are in logic 0 state.

14.2 Series Clippers

The series configuration is defined as one where the diode is in series with the load. The response of the series configuration to a variety of alternating waveforms is well defined. There are no boundaries on the type of signals that can be applied to a clipper.

14.2.1 Biased Series Clipper

Fig. 2 shows the series clipper with DC supply. This configuration is called as biased series clipper. The addition of a DC supply to the network can have a pronounced effect on the analysis of the series clipper configuration. The DC supply can be in the leg between the supply and output or in the branch parallel to the output. The output response is not as obvious because the DC supply can aid or work against the source voltage.

General instructions for analysis:

  1. Take careful note of where the output voltage is defined.
  2. Try to develop an overall sense of the response by simply noting how the input supply affects the conventional current direction through the diode.
  3. Determine the "transition voltage" that will result in a change of state for the diode from the "OFF" to the "ON" state. Note the substitution of the short-circuit equivalent for the ideal diode, and the fact that the voltage across the resistor is 0 V because the diode current is 0 mA. The result is \(v_i - V = 0\) or \(v_i = V\) is the applied (transition) voltage.
  4. It is often helpful to draw the output waveform directly below the applied voltage using the same scales for the horizontal axis and the vertical axis.

For the "ON" region:

\[ v_o = v_i - V \]

For the "OFF" region (diode open circuit, ID = 0 mA):

\[ v_o = 0 \text{ V} \]

For the "ON" condition, output voltage when input has peak value:

\[ v_{o\text{peak}} = V_m - V \]
Example 1 — Biased Series Clipper

Q: Determine the output waveform for the sinusoidal input of Fig. 7. (Vm = 20 V, V = 5 V, R in series, Si diode)

Solution:

Step 1: The output is directly across the resistor R.

Step 2: The positive region of vi and the DC supply are both working in the same direction to turn the diode "ON". The result is that we can safely assume the diode is in the "on" state for the entire range of positive voltages for vi. Once the supply goes negative, it would have to exceed the DC supply voltage of 5 V before it could turn the diode "OFF".

Step 3: The transition model is substituted and we find that the transition from one state to the other will occur when: \[ v_i + 5 \text{ V} = 0 \text{ V} \Rightarrow v_i = -5 \text{ V} \]

Step 4: A horizontal line is drawn through the applied voltage at the transition level (−5 V). For voltages less than −5 V the diode is in the open-circuit state and the output is 0 V. For conditions when the diode is on, using KVL: \[ v_o = v_i + 5 \text{ V} \]

Peak output: \(v_o = 20 \text{ V} + 5 \text{ V} = 25 \text{ V}\)

Note: The analysis of clipper networks with square-wave inputs is actually easier than with sinusoidal inputs because only two levels have to be considered.

14.3 Parallel Clippers

In parallel clippers, the diode is connected in parallel with the load. The analysis of parallel configurations is very similar to that of series configurations. The simplest parallel clipper has the diode in parallel with the output resistor.

The response to various waveforms shows that the clipper passes the negative portions of the signal while clipping the positive (or vice versa, depending on diode orientation).

14.3.1 Biased Parallel Clipper

Example 3 — Biased Parallel Clipper (Ideal Diode)

Q: Determine vo for the network (triangular wave, Vm = 16 V, V = 4 V battery with reversed diode).

Solution:
  • When the diode is in its short-circuit "ON" state, the output voltage will be directly across the 4-V DC supply. When the diode is "ON" the output will be 4 V.
  • When the diode is an open circuit "OFF" state, the current through the series network will be 0 mA and the voltage drop across the resistor will be 0 V. That will result in \(v_o = v_i\).

Transition level: Substituting the short-circuit equivalent and remembering the diode current is 0 mA at the instant of transition: \(v_i = 4 \text{ V}\).

Therefore:

\[ \text{For } v_i \geq 4 \text{ V}: \quad v_o = v_i \] \[ \text{For } v_i \leq 4 \text{ V}: \quad v_o = 4 \text{ V} \]
Example 4 — With Silicon Diode (VK = 0.7 V)

Q: Repeat Example 3, using a silicon diode with VK = 0.7 V.

Solution:

Determine Transition voltage — Using the condition id = 0 A at vd = VD = 0.7 V and applying KVL:

\[ v_i + V_K - V = 0 \Rightarrow v_i = V - V_K = 4 \text{ V} - 0.7 \text{ V} = 3.3 \text{ V} \]
  • Diode will be "OFF" for vi > 3.3 V: \(v_o = v_i\)
  • Diode will be "ON" for vi < 3.3 V: \(v_o = 4 \text{ V} - 0.7 \text{ V} = 3.3 \text{ V}\)

Note: The only effect of VK was to drop the transition level from 4 V to 3.3 V.

15. Clampers

Clamper: A circuit with a diode, resistor, and capacitor that shifts a waveform to a new DC level without altering its shape. Also known as a DC restorer.
  • Clamping networks have a capacitor connected directly from input.
  • A resistive element connected in parallel with the output signal.
  • The diode is also in parallel with the output signal but may or may not have a series DC supply as an added element.

15.1 Steps Used for Analysis

  1. Step 1: Examine the response of the portion of the input signal that will forward bias the diode. For a clamper with diode pointing downward, the diode is forward biased during the positive portion of the signal. The short-circuit equivalent for the diode results in \(V_o = 0 \text{ V}\) for \(t = 0\) to \(T/2\).
  2. Step 2: During the on period of the diode, assume that the capacitor will charge up instantaneously to a voltage level determined by the surrounding network.
  3. Step 3: Assume the capacitor retains its voltage while the diode is "off."
  4. Step 4: Track the position and polarity of vo to ensure correct levels. When the input turns negative, the network appears with the diode as an open circuit and voltage stored across the capacitor.
  5. Step 5: Check that the total swing of the output matches that of the input.

15.2 Clamper Numericals

Example 1 — Clamper

Q: Determine Vo for the network shown, for the input indicated in Fig. 1.1. (f = 1000 Hz, vi = square wave: +10 V (t1→t2), −20 V (t2→t3); C = 1 μF, R = 100 kΩ, V = 5 V battery)

Solution:

Note: f = 1000 Hz ⟹ Time period T = 1 ms.

Step 1: For t1→t2 of vi, since the diode is in its short-circuit state. The network appears as shown in Fig. 1.2.

Step 2: The output is across R (also directly across the 5-V battery). So, \(v_o = 5 \text{ V}\). Applying KVL around the input loop: \(-20 \text{ V} + V_C - 5 \text{ V} = 0 \Rightarrow V_C = 25 \text{ V}\). The capacitor charges to 25 V.

Step 3: For the period t2→t3 the network will appear as in Fig. 1.3 with the diode as open circuit. Applying KVL around the outside loop:

\[ +10 \text{ V} + 25 \text{ V} - v_o = 0 \Rightarrow v_o = 35 \text{ V} \]

Step 4: Time constant: \(\zeta = RC = (100 \text{ k}\Omega)(0.1\ \mu\text{F}) = 0.01 \text{ s} = 10 \text{ ms}\). Total discharge time: \(5\zeta = 50 \text{ ms}\).

Step 5: The output swing of 30 V matches the input swing. Output: \(v_o = 35 \text{ V}\) (t1→t2 period), \(v_o = 5 \text{ V}\) (t2→t3 period).

Example 2 — With Silicon Diode (VK = 0.7 V)

Q: Repeat Example 1, using a silicon diode with Vk = 0.7 V.

Solution:

For the short-circuit state, vo is determined by KVL in the output section as:

\[ +5 \text{ V} - 0.7 \text{ V} - v_o = 0 \Rightarrow v_o = 4.3 \text{ V} \]

For the input section, KVL results in:

\[ -20 \text{ V} + V_C + 0.7 \text{ V} - 5 \text{ V} = 0 \Rightarrow V_C = 24.3 \text{ V} \]

For the period t2→t3, the network will appear with only the voltage across the capacitor changed. Applying KVL:

\[ +10 \text{ V} + 24.3 \text{ V} - v_o = 0 \Rightarrow v_o = 34.3 \text{ V} \]

The resulting output verifies the statement that the input and output swings are the same (30 V swing).
