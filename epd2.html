1. An Introduction to Semiconductor

Semiconductor: A special class of elements having a conductivity between that of a good conductor and that of an insulator.

The three semiconductors used most frequently in the construction of electronic devices are Ge (Germanium), Si (Silicon), and GaAs (Gallium Arsenide).

1.1 Atomic Structure

Both silicon and germanium have four valence electrons in their outermost shell. Silicon atoms form a crystal lattice by sharing these electrons with neighboring atoms (covalent bonding).

1.2 Energy Bands

Electrons in atoms occupy discrete energy levels. When atoms are brought together in a solid, these discrete levels broaden into energy bands. The key bands are:

  • Valence Band: The outermost shell occupied by valence electrons (bound to atoms).
  • Conduction Band: The band where electrons are free to move and establish conduction.
  • Energy Gap (Eg): The gap between valence and conduction bands.

1.3 Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material that has been carefully refined to reduce the number of impurities to a very low level — essentially as pure as can be made available through modern technology.
Extrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material that has been subjected to the doping process is called an extrinsic material.

In pure (intrinsic) silicon, each Si atom shares its 4 valence electrons with 4 neighbors, forming covalent bonds. This creates a complete valence shell for each atom, leaving no free carriers at 0 K.

1.4 N-Type Semiconductor

An n-type material is created by introducing impurity elements that have five valence electrons (pentavalent), such as: Antimony (Sb), Arsenic (As), Phosphorus (P).

When a pentavalent atom replaces a silicon atom in the lattice, four of its five valence electrons form covalent bonds with neighboring Si atoms. The fifth electron is free to move through the lattice, contributing to electrical conduction.

Donor Atoms: Diffused impurities with five valence electrons are called donor atoms because they "donate" a free electron to the material.
In n-type material: Majority carriers: Electrons (negative) | Minority carriers: Holes (positive) | Donor ions (positive) are fixed in the lattice

1.5 P-Type Semiconductor

The p-type material is formed by doping with impurity atoms having three valence electrons (trivalent): Boron (B), Gallium (Ga), Indium (In).

When a trivalent atom is placed in the silicon lattice, it can only form three covalent bonds. The fourth bond position has a void (hole) — a missing electron that acts as a positive charge carrier.

Acceptor Atoms: The diffused impurities with three valence electrons are called acceptor atoms because they "accept" an electron from the lattice, creating a hole.
In p-type material: Majority carriers: Holes (positive) | Minority carriers: Electrons (negative) | Acceptor ions (negative) are fixed in the lattice

2. An Introduction to the Semiconductor Diode

Diode: A two-electrode (two-terminal) device. We can construct our first solid-state electronic device — the semiconductor diode — by sandwiching p-type and n-type material together to form a depletion region between the two. It is a fabrication of one material with a majority carrier of electrons joined to one with a majority carrier of holes.

2.1 Biasing of Diodes

  • Forward Bias: p-type to positive terminal, n-type to negative terminal of voltage source.
  • Reverse Bias: p-type to negative terminal, n-type to positive terminal of voltage source.

2.2 No-Bias Condition

At the instant the two materials are "joined," the electrons and the holes in the region of the junction will combine, resulting in a lack of free carriers in the region near the junction. The only particles remaining in this region are the positive and negative ions after the free carriers have been absorbed.

Depletion Region: This region of uncovered positive and negative ions is called the depletion region due to the "depletion" of free carriers in the region.
Key fact: In the absence of an applied bias across a semiconductor diode, the net flow of charge in one direction is zero. \( I_D = 0 \text{ mA}, \; V_D = 0 \text{ V} \)

2.3 Forward-Bias Condition

A forward-bias or "on" condition is established by applying the positive potential to the p-type material and the negative potential to the n-type material. The application of a forward-bias potential \(V_D\) will reduce the width of the depletion region.

The current that exists under forward-bias conditions is called the forward-bias current: \[ I_D = I_{\text{majority}} - I_s \]

2.4 Reverse-Bias Condition

When a P-N junction is reverse-biased, the depletion region widens, establishing too great a barrier for the majority carriers to overcome, effectively reducing the majority carrier flow to zero.

The current that exists under reverse-bias conditions is called the reverse saturation current and is represented by \(I_s\).

3. Semiconductor Diode Characteristics and Parameters

3.1 Forward Bias Parameters

  • Knee Voltage (VF): Si: ~0.7 V | Ge: ~0.3 V
  • Forward Current Rating: Maximum allowable forward current.
  • Maximum Power Dissipation: P = VD × ID

3.2 Reverse Bias Parameters

  • Reverse Saturation Current (Is): Very small current due to minority carriers.
  • Breakdown Voltage (VBR): Reverse voltage at which large reverse current flows.
  • Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) Rating: Maximum reverse voltage the diode can withstand.

4. Diode's Current Expression — Shockley's Current Equation

Shockley's Equation — also called the diode equation — helps us determine the current at a given temperature knowing the diode voltage and saturation current.

\[ I_D = I_S \left( e^{\frac{qV_D}{nkT}} - 1 \right) \]

4.1 Variables and Constants

SymbolMeaningValue / Unit
\(I_D\)Diode currentAmperes (A)
\(V_D\)Diode voltageVolts (V)
\(I_S\)Reverse saturation currentAmperes (A)
\(q\)Charge of electron1.6 × 10−19 C
\(n\)Ideality factor1 (Si, Ge); 2 (GaAs, InP)
\(k\)Boltzmann's constant1.38 × 10−23 J/K
\(T\)TemperatureKelvin (K)
\(V_T = kT/q\)Thermal voltage≈ 25.9 mV at 300 K

4.2 Special Cases

  • Reverse bias (\(V_D\) negative):
    \[ I_D \approx -I_S \]
  • Forward bias (\(V_D\) positive):
    \[ I_D \approx I_S \, e^{\frac{qV_D}{nkT}} \]

4.3 Thermal Voltage

\[ V_T = \frac{kT}{q} = \frac{T}{11600} \text{ (V)} \]

At room temperature (T = 300 K): \(V_T \approx 25.9 \text{ mV} \approx 26 \text{ mV}\)

7. Types of Diode Breakdown

7.1 Avalanche Breakdown

In the reverse bias condition, as the voltage increases, free charge carriers obtain velocity and associated kinetic energy and release additional carriers through collision with other atomic structures. Covalent bonds are broken and electron-hole pairs are generated. This cumulative process is called Avalanche multiplication.

Avalanche breakdown is observed in diodes with Zener breakdown voltage Vz > 8 V.

7.2 Zener Breakdown

Zener breakdown occurs when both P and N type material are heavily doped in a semiconductor diode. Hence the depletion layer region is narrowed down. When a small reverse bias voltage is applied, a very strong electric field is produced, and covalent bonds break producing a large number of free charge carriers.

Zener breakdown is observed in diodes with Zener breakdown voltage Vz of 5 to 8 V.

8. Temperature Effects on V-I Characteristics of a Diode

  • Rise in temperature generates more electron-hole pairs, thus conductivity increases and current increases.
  • PN junction diode parameters like reverse saturation current, bias current, reverse breakdown voltage, and barrier voltage are dependent on temperature.
  • Increase in temperature: Knee voltage decreases | Breakdown voltage increases | Reverse saturation current increases

8.1 Under Forward Bias

Barrier voltage decreases by 2.5 mV/°C rise in temperature for both Germanium and Silicon.

8.2 Under Reverse Bias

Reverse saturation current (IS) doubles for every 10°C rise in temperature. Rise is 7%/°C for both Ge and Si. Reverse breakdown voltage (VR) also increases as temperature increases.

8.3 Summary Rules

  1. The reverse saturation current Is will double in magnitude for every 10°C increase in temperature.
  2. The Knee voltage shifts to the left at a rate of 2.5 mV per degree centigrade increase in temperature.

9. Ideal and Practical Diode Characteristics

9.1 Ideal Diode

Ideal Diode: When an ideal diode is forward biased, it offers no resistance and acts like a closed switch. Under reverse bias it offers infinite resistance and acts like an open switch. \(V_d = 0\) V when forward biased; \(I_D = 0\) A when reverse biased.

9.2 Practical Diode

Practical Diode: A diode which starts conducting at the knee voltage (VK). For Si: \(V_K = 0.7\text{ V}\); For Ge: \(V_K = 0.3\text{ V}\). Under reverse bias no current flows (minority charge current is ignored).

10. Diode Resistances

An actual diode offers a very small resistance (not zero) when forward biased — forward resistance. It offers a very high resistance (not infinite) when reverse biased — reverse resistance. Types: DC or Static Resistance | AC or Dynamic Resistance | Average AC Resistance.

10.1 DC or Static Resistance (RD)

\[ R_D = \frac{V_D}{I_D} \]
Typical DC resistance: Forward bias: 10 Ω to 80 Ω | Reverse bias: 10 MΩ

10.2 AC or Dynamic Resistance (rd)

\[ r_d = \frac{\Delta V_d}{\Delta I_d} = \frac{26\text{ mV}}{I_D}\text{ (ohms)} \]

Including bulk resistance: \(r'_d = \frac{26\text{ mV}}{I_D} + r_B\) where \(r_B\) is bulk or body resistance.

AC resistance of a diode in the active region will range from 1 Ω to 100 Ω.

10.3 Average AC Resistance (rav)

The resistance determined by a straight line drawn between the two intersections established by the maximum and minimum values of input voltage.

\[ r_{av} = \frac{\Delta V_d}{\Delta I_d}\bigg|_{\text{pt. to pt.}} \]

11. Diode Equivalent Circuits / Diode Approximations

Diode Approximation: A mathematical method used to approximate the nonlinear behavior of real diodes to enable calculations and circuit analysis.
  1. Piecewise-Linear Equivalent Circuit
  2. Simplified Diode Characteristics
  3. Ideal Diode Characteristics

11.1 Piecewise-Linear Equivalent Circuit

  • Under forward-bias: Diode behaves as a practical voltage source = knee voltage, in series with the diode's average AC resistance. \(V_d = V_B + I_d \times R_B\)
  • Under reverse-bias: Diode is an open switch and no current flows.
Note: VB = Knee voltage

11.2 Simplified Diode Characteristics

  • Under forward-bias: The diode turns ON when a voltage of VK is applied. \(V_d = V_B\)
  • Under reverse bias: It is an open switch.
For Si: VB = 0.7 V; For Ge: VB = 0.3 V

11.3 Ideal Diode Characteristics

  • The diode is a closed switch with zero voltage drop when forward-biased (\(V_d = 0\)).
  • It acts as an open switch with zero current when reverse biased.

  3. Determine the "transition voltage" that results in a change of state: \(v_i - V = 0\) or \(v_i = V\).
  4. Draw the output waveform directly below the applied voltage using the same scales.
\[ v_o = v_i - V \text{ (ON region)}\qquad v_o = 0\text{ V (OFF region)}\qquad v_{o\text{peak}} = V_m - V \]

15. Clampers

Clamper: A circuit with a diode, resistor, and capacitor that shifts a waveform to a new DC level without altering its shape. Also known as a DC restorer.
  • Clamping networks have a capacitor connected directly from input.
  • A resistive element connected in parallel with the output signal.
  • The diode is also in parallel with the output signal but may or may not have a series DC supply as an added element.

15.1 Steps Used for Analysis

  1. Examine the response of the portion of the input signal that will forward bias the diode. The short-circuit equivalent gives \(V_o = 0\text{ V}\).
  2. During the on period of the diode, the capacitor charges instantaneously to a voltage level determined by the surrounding network.
  3. Assume the capacitor retains its voltage while the diode is "off."
  4. Track the position and polarity of vo to ensure correct levels.
  5. Check that the total swing of the output matches that of the input.

15.2 Clamper Numericals

Example 1 — Clamper

Q: f = 1000 Hz, vi: +10 V (t1-t2), −20 V (t2-t3). C = 1 μF, R = 100 kΩ, V = 5 V. Determine Vo.

Step 1: Diode "ON": \(v_o = 5\text{ V}\). KVL: \(-20 + V_C - 5 = 0 \implies V_C = 25\text{ V}\).

Step 2: Diode "OFF" period:

\[ 10 + 25 - v_o = 0 \implies v_o = 35\text{ V} \]

Time constant: \(\tau = (100\text{ k})(1\;\mu) = 0.1\text{ s} = 100\text{ ms}\). Output swing = 30 V = input swing.

Example 2 — With Silicon Diode (VK = 0.7 V)

Q: Repeat Example 1 using silicon diode.

\[ v_o = 5 - 0.7 = 4.3\text{ V (ON state)} \] \[ -20 + V_C + 0.7 - 5 = 0 \implies V_C = 24.3\text{ V} \] \[ 10 + 24.3 - v_o = 0 \implies v_o = 34.3\text{ V} \]

Output swing = 30 V = input swing confirmed.

Regulated Power Supply

Power supplies are designed for a dedicated load and environment. But sometimes main supply voltage, load, and surrounding temperature keep changing, altering the component parameters and hence changing the output voltage. Regulated power supply converts an alternating current signal to a constant signal.

Block diagram of regulated power supply:
120 V rms → Transformer → Rectifier → Filter → IC Regulator → Load
Each stage progressively smooths the signal: sinusoidal AC → pulsating DC → ripple-reduced DC → constant regulated DC

Block Diagram Components

  • Transformer: Output sinusoidal voltage is either step-up or step-down from the input sinusoidal voltage value.
  • Rectifier: Converts alternating current into direct current by allowing current to flow in one direction only.
  • Filter: Removes the AC ripples from the DC signal obtained from the rectifier.
  • Regulator: Converts DC voltage into a lower constant voltage.

Rectifier Classification

Rectifier → Half Wave Rectifier | Full Wave Rectifier → Centre Tapped FWR | Bridge Rectifier

Half Wave Rectifier (HWR)

HWR — Ideal Diode

The input waveform is \(v_i = V_m \sin \omega t\). The HWR circuit consists of a diode in series with a load resistor \(R_L\).

Positive Half Cycle (Conduction: 0 to T/2):

\[ I_m = \frac{V_m}{R_L},\quad V_o = V_m \]

Negative Half Cycle (Non-conduction: T/2 to T):

\[ I = 0,\quad V_o = 0 \]

HWR — Non-Ideal Diode

For a silicon diode with knee voltage \(V_k = 0.7\text{ V}\), the diode only conducts when \(v_i > V_k\). The output is offset by \(V_k\).

\[ V_{o\,\max} = V_m - V_k,\quad I_{o\,\max} = \frac{V_m - V_k}{R_L} \]

Average Voltage / Current for HWR — Ideal Diode

\[ V_{dc} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}V_m\sin\omega t\,d(\omega t) = \frac{V_m}{\pi} \] \[ \boxed{V_{dc} = \frac{V_m}{\pi} = 0.318\,V_m}\qquad I_{dc} = \frac{V_m}{\pi R_L} = \frac{I_m}{\pi} \]

RMS Voltage / Current for HWR — Ideal Diode

\[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_m}{2},\qquad I_{rms} = \frac{I_m}{2} \]

Average & RMS — HWR Non-Ideal Diode

\[ V_{dc} = \frac{V_m - V_k}{\pi},\quad I_{dc} = \frac{I_m}{\pi}\;\left(I_m = \frac{V_m-V_k}{R_L}\right) \] \[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_m-V_k}{2},\quad I_{rms} = \frac{I_m}{2} \]

Ripple Factor of HWR

Ripple Factor is a measure of purity of the dc output of a rectifier:

\[ r = \frac{\text{rms value of ac component}}{\text{dc value of output}} \] \[ \gamma_{HWR} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_{rms}}{V_{dc}}\right)^2-1} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_m/2}{V_m/\pi}\right)^2-1} \] \[ V'_{rms} = \sqrt{(V_m/2)^2-(V_m/\pi)^2} = 0.385\,V_m \implies \boxed{\gamma_{HWR} = 1.21} \]

Half Wave Rectifier PIV

Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV) is the maximum reverse biased voltage the diode can withstand without entering the breakdown region. During the negative half cycle, \(I = 0\), so \(V_o = 0\). The full source voltage appears across the reverse-biased diode.

\[ \boxed{\text{PIV rating} \geq V_m\quad\text{(half-wave rectifier)}} \]

Full Wave Rectifier (FWR)

FWR: Centre Tap — Ideal Diode

A centre-tap transformer with turns ratio 1:2 provides two equal secondary voltages. Two diodes \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) conduct on alternating half-cycles. Both half-cycles produce a positive output giving a full-wave rectified signal with \(V_{dc} = 0.636\,V_m\).

Positive Half Cycle (\(D_1\) conducts, \(D_2\) off):

\[ V_o = V_m,\quad I_o = V_m/R_L \]

Negative Half Cycle (\(D_2\) conducts, \(D_1\) off):

\[ V_o = V_m,\quad I_m = V_m/R_L \]

FWR PIV — Centre Tap (Ideal Diode)

\[ \text{PIV} = V_m + V_m \implies \boxed{\text{PIV} \geq 2V_m} \]

FWR: Centre Tap — Non-Ideal Diode

\[ V_{o\,max} = V_m-V_k,\quad \text{PIV} = 2V_m-V_k \]

FWR: Bridge Rectifier — Ideal Diode

Four diodes arranged in a bridge configuration. \(D_3\) and \(D_4\) conduct on positive half-cycle; \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) conduct on negative half-cycle. Current always flows in the same direction through the load R.

Both Half Cycles:

\[ V_o = V_m,\quad I_o = V_m/R_L,\quad V_{dc} = 0.636\,V_m \]

FWR: Bridge Rectifier — Non-Ideal Diode

Two diodes conduct per half-cycle, each dropping \(V_T = 0.7\text{ V}\):

\[ V_{o\,max} = V_m - 2V_T \implies \boxed{V_{dc} \cong 0.636(V_m-2V_T)} \]

FWR PIV: Bridge Rectifier

\[ \text{Ideal: PIV} \geq V_m \qquad \text{Non-Ideal: PIV} \geq V_m - V_k \]

Average Voltage / Current for FWR — Ideal Diode

\[ V_{dc} = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}V_m\sin\omega t\,d(\omega t) = \frac{2V_m}{\pi} = 0.636\,V_m \] \[ I_{dc} = \frac{2I_m}{\pi} \]

RMS Voltage / Current for FWR — Ideal Diode

\[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_m}{\sqrt{2}},\qquad I_{rms} = \frac{I_m}{\sqrt{2}} \]

Average & RMS — Centre Tap Non-Ideal

\[ V_{dc} = \frac{2(V_m-V_k)}{\pi},\quad I_{dc} = \frac{2I_m}{\pi}\quad\left(I_m=\frac{V_m-V_k}{R_L}\right) \] \[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_m-V_k}{\sqrt{2}},\quad I_{rms} = \frac{I_m}{\sqrt{2}} \]

Average & RMS — Bridge Non-Ideal

\[ V_{dc} = \frac{2(V_m-2V_k)}{\pi},\quad I_{dc} = \frac{2I_m}{\pi}\quad\left(I_m=\frac{V_m-2V_k}{R_L}\right) \] \[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_m-2V_k}{\sqrt{2}},\quad I_{rms} = \frac{I_m}{\sqrt{2}} \]

Ripple Factor of FWR

\[ \gamma_{FWR} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_m/\sqrt{2}}{2V_m/\pi}\right)^2-1} \] \[ V'_{rms} = \sqrt{(V_m/\sqrt{2})^2-(2V_m/\pi)^2} = 0.307\,V_m \implies \boxed{\gamma_{FWR} = 0.48} \]

Shunt Capacitor Filter

Filter is a circuit which converts pulsating DC to smooth DC. By connecting a capacitor in parallel with the load, the ac ripple present at the output of a rectifier can be reduced.

Working

During positive half cycle the diode D1 is forward biased and the capacitor C will charge from 0 V towards the peak value of input \(V_m\). When input voltage reaches \(V_m\), the charge on capacitor also equals \(V_m\), hence the diodes turn off. Now the capacitor starts discharging through the load \(R_L\). If the load resistance is very high and load current is low, the capacitor will discharge by a small amount, and the next half cycle charges the capacitor back to \(V_m\). Thus the output voltage remains almost constant.

  • Time T1: Diodes conduct, charging the capacitor up to the peak rectifier voltage \(V_m\).
  • Time T2: Capacitor discharges through the load when rectifier voltage drops below peak.
  • Since the charge-discharge cycle occurs for each half-cycle for a full-wave rectifier, the period of the rectified waveform is \(T/2\).

Expression for Ripple Factor — Shunt Capacitor Filter (FWR)

\[ \boxed{\gamma_{FWR} = \frac{1}{4\sqrt{3}\,f\,C\,R_L}} \] \[ \gamma \propto \frac{1}{f}\cdot\frac{1}{C}\cdot\frac{1}{R_L} \] \[ V_{r(rms)} = \frac{I_{DC}}{4\sqrt{3}\,fC},\qquad V_{dc} = V_m - \frac{I_{DC}}{4\,fC} \]

Shunt Capacitor Filter for Half-Wave Rectifier

\[ \boxed{\text{Ripple Factor (HWR)} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}\,f\,C\,R_L}} \] \[ V_{r(rms)} = \frac{I_{DC}}{2\sqrt{3}\,fC},\qquad V_{dc} = V_m - \frac{I_{DC}}{2\,fC} \]

Zener Diode

  • Heavily Doped PN-Junction Device specially designed to operate under breakdown region.
  • Zener Diode under forward bias condition works like a normal semiconductor diode.
  • Zener Diode under reverse bias condition acts like a Voltage regulator.

Zener diodes are usually heavily doped diodes hence the depletion layer is very narrow. When the reverse voltage across the junction is increased, the electric field becomes high so that electrons are pulled out of covalent bonds, resulting in a sudden rise in current known as zener breakdown.

Zener Diode Characteristics

V-I Characteristics of Zener Diode:
Forward Bias: Behaves like a normal diode; current increases rapidly above cut-in voltage (~0.7 V for Si).
Reverse Bias: Small reverse current (Io) until breakdown at −VZ; then current rises sharply. Voltage remains nearly constant at VZ for a wide range of currents from IZ(min) to IZ(max). This is the "Constant Zener Voltage" region.
At test current IZT = 12.5 mA, \(r_Z = 8.5\;\Omega\). Maximum current IZM = 32 mA.

Zener Diode as Voltage Regulator

Conditions required:

  • Zener Diode should be in Reverse Biased Condition.
  • \(V_{in}\) should be Greater than \(V_Z\).
  • \(I_Z\) should be greater than \(I_{Zmin}\) and less than or equal to \(I_{Zmax}\).
Zener Voltage Regulator Circuit:
\(V_i\) (variable input) → Series resistor R → Node → Zener diode VZ (in parallel) → Ground.
Load \(R_L\) in parallel with Zener. \(I_R = I_Z + I_L\)

CASE-1: Fixed Vi and Fixed RL

Determine the state of the Zener diode by removing it from the network and calculating the voltage across the resulting open circuit:

\[ V = V_L = \frac{R_L\,V_i}{R + R_L} \]
  • If \(V \geq V_Z\): Zener diode is ON
  • If \(V < V_Z\): Zener diode is OFF

BJT Transistor: Introduction

A bipolar junction transistor (BJT) is a three terminal device consisting of either a p-type semiconductor or n-type semiconductor sandwiched between opposite types. It has two p-n junctions connected back-to-back.

In a BJT, the operation depends on the active participation of both the majority carriers and the minority carriers; hence the name "bipolar". The combination of two terms transfer + resistor results in TRANSISTOR as the current is transferred from a low to a high-resistance circuit.

BJT Transistor: Construction

The three terminals of a Bipolar junction transistor are Emitter, Base, and Collector.

  • Emitter (E): Supplies charge carriers; heavily doped; moderate in area.
  • Base (B): Middle portion forming two PN junctions; lightly doped; smallest in width; controls carrier flow.
  • Collector (C): Collects charge carriers; moderately doped; widest of all three regions as maximum heat is dissipated here.

BJT Operation

The transistor operated in Common Base configuration:

  • The emitter-base junction is forward biased (majority carrier current flow)
  • The collector-base junction is reverse biased (minority carrier current flow; wider depletion region)

Currents in a BJT

  • A large number of majority carriers diffuse across the forward biased p-n junction into the n-type material.
  • Since the n-type material (base) is very thin and lightly doped, a very small number will recombine with electrons in base and flow through the base terminal as base current (typically in microamperes).
  • A larger number of majority carriers diffuse across the reverse-biased junction into the collector terminal.

Base Width Modulation (Early Effect)

A greater reverse bias across the collector-base junction increases the collector-base depletion width. There is a variation in the width of the base in a bipolar transistor due to a variation in the applied base-to-collector voltage. Base-narrowing has two consequences:

  • There is a lesser chance for recombination within the "smaller" base region.
  • The charge gradient is increased across the base, and consequently, the current of minority carriers injected across the collector-base junction increases.

CE Active Region — Derivation

\[ I_C = \alpha I_E + I_{CB0} = \alpha(I_C+I_B) + I_{CB0} \implies I_C = \frac{\alpha I_B + I_{CB0}}{1-\alpha} \]

Let \(\beta = \dfrac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\):

\[ I_C = \beta I_B + (1+\beta)I_{CB0} = \beta I_B + I_{CE0} \] \[ \boxed{I_C = \beta I_B}\;\text{(neglecting leakage)},\qquad \boxed{50 \leq \beta \leq 500} \] \[ I_E = I_C + I_B \implies \boxed{I_E = (\beta+1)I_B} \]

Relationship Between α and β

\[ \frac{1}{\alpha} = 1 + \frac{1}{\beta} \implies \boxed{\alpha = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta}\qquad\text{or}\qquad\beta = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}} \]

Operating Point (Q-Point)

Biasing refers to application of DC voltages to establish a fixed level of current and voltage in a transistor. The resulting DC current and voltage on the characteristic curve establish an Operating Point. Since the operating point is fixed on the Characteristic curve, it is also called as Quiescent point or Q point.

  • Q Point at A: No bias, device completely off. Complete input range cannot be covered. Not suitable.
  • Q Point at B: Covers a large range of input signal. Far from both cut-off and saturation region. Ideal.
  • Q Point at C: Near to both cutoff and saturation region, limiting the swing of input signal.
  • Q Point at D: Near maximum voltage and power level, limits the positive swing.

Effect of Temperature on Operating Point

Temperature causes device parameters like transistor current gain (βac) and leakage current (ICEO) to change. As temperature increases, ICEO increases, resulting in changing the Q point. The variation of operating point due to temperature variation is indicated by stability factor (S).

Transistor Biasing

Biasing is the process of providing DC voltage which helps in the functioning of the circuit. A transistor is biased in order to make the base-emitter junction forward biased and base-collector junction reverse biased, so that it maintains in active region, to work as an amplifier.

Need for DC Biasing

For a BJT to amplify a signal, two conditions have to be met:

  • The input voltage should exceed cut-in voltage for the transistor to be ON.
  • The BJT should be in the linear/active region to be operated as an amplifier.

In the previous bias configurations, the bias current ICQ and voltage VCEQ were a function of the current gain β. Since β is temperature sensitive, it is necessary to develop a bias circuit that is independent of the transistor beta. Thus, Voltage divider bias configuration is preferred.

Approximation Condition: If βRE ≥ 10R2, then the approximation method can be applied.
\[ V_B = \frac{R_2 V_{CC}}{R_1+R_2},\quad V_E = V_B-V_{BE},\quad I_{CQ} \cong I_E = \frac{V_E}{R_E} \] \[ \boxed{V_{CE_Q} = V_{CC}-I_C(R_C+R_E)} \] \[ I_{C_{\text{sat}}} = \frac{V_{CC}}{R_C+R_E} \]

Single Stage CE Amplifier

Circuit Elements and Functions

  • (i) Biasing Circuit: R1, R2 and RE form the biasing and stabilization circuit.
  • (ii) Input Capacitance Cin: Used to couple the signal to the base. Prevents signal source resistance from coming across R2 and changing bias condition. Allows only a.c. signal to flow.
  • (iii) Emitter Bypass Capacitor CE: Connected in parallel with RE to provide a low reactance path to the amplified a.c. signal. Without it, amplified a.c. signal flowing through RE will cause a voltage drop, thereby shifting the output voltage.
  • (iv) Coupling Capacitor C: Used to couple the amplified signal to the output device. Allows only a.c. signal to flow.
  • (v) Working: A weak input a.c. signal applied to the base causes a small base current. Due to transistor action, a much larger a.c. current flows through collector load RC, a large voltage appears across RC and hence at the output. Voltage gain Av is the ratio of the amplified output voltage to the input voltage.

Operational Amplifier — Introduction

  • Operational Amplifier or op-amp is a very high gain differential amplifier.
  • It has very high input impedance. Ideally its value is infinity.
  • It has a low output impedance. Ideally its value is zero.
  • It finds applications in filters and oscillators.

Op-Amp Input/Output Configurations

Single-Ended Input Operation

One input with the other connected to ground. When input signal is applied to the minus input, the output is phase-shifted by 180°. When applied to the plus input, output is in phase.

Double-Ended (Differential) Input

Differential input Vd applied between the two input terminals (neither at ground). The resulting amplified output is in phase with Vd.

Double-Ended Output

An input applied to either input will result in outputs from both output terminals, always opposite in polarity. The difference output signal Vo1 − Vo2 is also referred to as a floating signal. The difference output is twice as large as either Vo1 or Vo2.

Common Mode Operation

When the same input signals are applied to both inputs, common-mode operation results. Ideally, the two inputs are equally amplified, and since they result in opposite-polarity signals at the output, these signals cancel, resulting in 0-V output. Practically, a small output signal will result.

Basic Op-Amp and Ideal Voltage Transfer Curve

  • Input 1 = Noninverting input (+)
  • Input 2 = Inverting input (−)
  • Vd = Differential Voltage
  • +Vsat ≈ +VCC | −Vsat ≈ −VEE
  • Output rises linearly between +Vsat and −Vsat

Op-Amp Parameters

Input Offset Voltage

DC voltage that must be applied between the input terminals to make the output voltage zero. Ideally 0 V. Value: 1 mV to 6 mV for 741.

Output Offset Voltage

Small DC voltage that appears at output even when input terminals are shorted. Caused by internal mismatches. Ideally 0 V.

Input Resistance

Effective resistance seen at input terminals. Ideally infinity. Value: 2 MΩ for 741 under open loop condition.

Output Resistance

Resistance seen at the output terminal in open-loop condition. Ideally 0 ohm. Value: 75 ohms for 741.

Gain Bandwidth

The frequency at which the gain drops by 3 dB is known as the cutoff frequency fc. The unity-gain frequency f1 and cutoff frequency are related by:

\[ f_1 = A_{VD} f_c \]

Unity-gain frequency = gain-bandwidth product. Ideally infinity. Value: 1 MHz for 741.

Slew Rate

The maximum rate at which amplifier output can change in volts per microsecond. Ideal value: infinity. Value: 0.5 V/μs for 741.

\[ SR = \frac{\Delta V_o}{\Delta t}\;\text{V}/\mu\text{s} \]

Common Mode Rejection Ratio (CMRR)

Ratio of differential gain to the common mode gain of the amplifier. Ideally CMRR is infinity. Typically 90 dB for 741.

\[ CMRR = \frac{A_d}{A_c},\qquad CMRR(\text{dB}) = 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{A_d}{A_c}\right) \]

Virtual Ground Concept

  • The output voltage of an Op-amp is limited by the supply voltage and the voltage gains are very high.
  • Example: if Vo = −10 V and gain Av = 20,000, then Vi = −10/20,000 = 0.5 mV.
  • Compared to all other input and output voltages, Vi is very small and can be considered as 0 V.
  • The fact that Vi ≈ 0 V leads to the concept that at the amplifier input there exists a virtual short-circuit or virtual ground.
  • Although Vi ≈ 0 V, there is no current through the amplifier input to ground.
  • Current will not flow through short and ground; it flows only through resistors R1 and Rf.
\[ I = \frac{V_1}{R_1} = -\frac{V_0}{R_f} \implies \frac{V_0}{V_1} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1} \]

Practical Op-Amp Circuits

1. Noninverting Amplifier

Input is given to noninverting terminal. Voltage across R1 is V1 (since Vi = 0 V) and is equal to the output voltage through a voltage divider of R1 and Rf:

\[ V_1 = \frac{R_1}{R_1+R_f}V_o \implies \boxed{\frac{V_o}{V_1} = 1 + \frac{R_f}{R_1}} \]

2. Inverting Amplifier

VA = VB = 0 (Virtual ground). At Input: \(I = V_1/R_1\). At Output: \(I = -V_O/R_f\). Equating:

\[ \boxed{V_O = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}\cdot V_1},\qquad \text{Gain} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1} \]
  • Inverting amplifier is the most widely used constant-gain amplifier circuit.
  • The output is obtained by multiplying the input by a fixed gain set by R1 and Rf, and output is inverted from the input.

Ideal OP-Amp Negative Feedback

\[ \text{Closed loop Gain} = \frac{V_{\text{out}}}{V_{\text{in}}} = 1+\frac{R_1}{R_2},\qquad \text{Feedback}\;\beta = \frac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} \]

3. Unity Follower (Voltage Follower)

  • Output directly connected back to inverting (−) input (100% feedback).
  • Provides a gain of unity with no polarity or phase reversal. \(V_o = V_1\).

4. Summing Amplifier

Three-input inverting summing amplifier circuit algebraically sums three voltages, each multiplied by a constant-gain through resistors. Applying KCL at node A:

\[ \frac{v_1}{R_1}+\frac{v_2}{R_2}+\frac{v_3}{R_3} = \frac{-v_{\text{out}}}{R_f} \] \[ \boxed{v_{\text{out}} = -R_f\left(\frac{v_1}{R_1}+\frac{v_2}{R_2}+\frac{v_3}{R_3}\right)} \]

5. Constant Gain / Fixed Gain Amplifier

Inverting Constant Gain Multiplier
\[ A = -\frac{R_f}{R_1} \]
Non-Inverting Constant Gain Multiplier
\[ A = 1+\frac{R_f}{R_1} \]

6. Voltage Subtraction (Difference Amplifier)

Superposition principle is used. Vo1 is the output with input V1 (V2 = 0); Vo2 is the output with input V2 (V1 = 0):

\[ V_{o1} = -\frac{R_f}{R_1}V_1,\qquad V_B = \frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}V_2 \] \[ V_{o2} = \left[1+\frac{R_f}{R_1}\right]\left[\frac{R_f}{R_2+R_f}\right]V_2 \] \[ \text{When }R_1=R_2:\quad V_o = +\frac{R_f}{R_1}(V_2-V_1) \] \[ \text{When }R_1=R_2=R_f:\quad \boxed{V_o = V_2-V_1} \]

The output voltage is proportional to the difference between the two input voltages. Thus it acts as a Subtractor or Difference Amplifier.

Combinational circuits are constructed by interconnection of logic gates whose outputs at any time are determined from only the present combination of inputs. Introduction: Sequential Circuits

Sequential Circuits: Circuits that produce output depending on current and past input values — circuits with memory. A sequential circuit contains a Combinational Circuit and a Storage Element with feedback.
1. Synchronous Circuits: The state of the device changes only at discrete times in response to a clock pulse.
2. Asynchronous Circuits: Not synchronized by a clock signal; outputs change directly in response to the change in the inputs.

Flip-Flops — Introduction

Flip-flop circuits are constructed to operate properly when part of a sequential circuit using a common clock. The key to the proper operation of a flip-flop is to trigger it only during a signal transition.

Latch (Level Sensitive): Changes output when the clock remains at logic 1 — Positive level response.
Flip-Flop (Edge Triggered): Changes output when the clock transitions from 0 to 1 — Positive-edge response.

Edge Triggered D Flip-Flop

Edge-Triggered D Flip-Flop: Construction uses three SR latches. Two latches respond to the external D (data) and Clk (clock) inputs. The third latch provides the outputs for the flip-flop.

Operation

  • The S and R inputs of the output latch are maintained at logic-1 level when Clk = 0. This causes the output to remain in its present state.
  • If D = 0 when Clk becomes 1, R changes to 0. This causes the flip-flop to go to the reset state, making Q = 0. The flip-flop is then locked out and is unresponsive to further changes in the input.
  • Similarly, if D = 1 when Clk goes from 0 to 1, S changes to 0. This causes the circuit to go to the set state, making Q = 1. Any change in D while Clk = 1 does not affect the output.
Positive-Edge Triggered: Absence of bubble in front of Clk input. Output changes on 0→1 clock transition.
Negative-Edge Triggered: Presence of bubble in front of Clk input. Output changes on 1→0 clock transition.

JK Flip-Flop: Inputs are J-K along with the Clk. J-K inputs named after its inventors Jack Kilby. In JK flip-flop, all rows are valid states in the characteristic table. The J=K=1 condition produces a Toggle state (output complements), eliminating the forbidden state.

Registers

Register: A group of flip-flops, each one of which shares a common clock and is capable of storing one bit of information. An n-bit register consists of a group of n flip-flops capable of storing n bits of binary information. In addition to flip-flops, a register may have combinational gates that perform certain data-processing tasks.

4-bit Register

The simplest register consists of only flip-flops. A 4-bit register is constructed with four D-type flip-flops.

  • The common clock input triggers all flip-flops on the positive edge of each pulse, and the binary data available at the four inputs are transferred into the register.
  • The input Clear goes to the active-low R (reset) input of all four flip-flops. When this input goes to 0, all flip-flops are reset asynchronously.
  • The R inputs must be maintained at logic 1 (de-asserted) during normal clocked operation.
  • The transfer of new information into a register is referred to as loading or updating the register.
  • If all the bits of the register are loaded simultaneously with a common clock pulse, we say that the loading is done in parallel.

Shift Registers (SISO)

Shift Register: A register capable of shifting the binary information held in each cell to its neighboring cell, in a selected direction. The logical configuration consists of a chain of flip-flops in cascade, with the output of one flip-flop connected to the input of the next. All flip-flops receive common clock pulses which activate the shift of data from one stage to the next.

The simplest possible shift register (SISO) uses only flip-flops. The output of a given flip-flop is connected to the D input of the flip-flop at its right. This shift register is unidirectional (left-to-right). Each clock pulse shifts the contents of the register one bit position to the right.

Synchronous and Asynchronous Counters

Counter: A register (group of flip-flops) that goes through a prescribed sequence of states upon the application of input pulses. A counter that follows the binary number sequence is called a binary counter. An n-bit binary counter consists of n flip-flops and can count in binary from 0 through \(2^n - 1\).
Ripple (Asynchronous) Counters: A flip-flop output transition serves as a source for triggering other flip-flops. The C inputs of some or all flip-flops are triggered not by the common clock pulses, but rather by transition that occurs in other flip-flop outputs.
Synchronous Counters: The C inputs of all flip-flops receive the common clock. All flip-flops change state simultaneously at the clock edge.
